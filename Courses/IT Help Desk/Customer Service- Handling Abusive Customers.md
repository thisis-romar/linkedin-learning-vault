The first question you must answer is crucial. Why is the customer upset? And the best way to find the answer is to ask the customer. You might say to the customer, "I want to do what I can to make sure you're happy. Can you tell me what happened?" Even if you think you know the answer, it's best not to assume anything. Let them tell you in their own words. If the customer won't tell you or can't tell you, then it's up to you to investigate. Talk to your colleagues, check the purchase history notes, or ask your manager. Good customer service means going that extra mile to research the problem so you can fully solve the right problem. The second question to consider is what does the customer want at the end of the transaction? Regardless of whether or not you can accommodate your customer's request, you need to know what they expect. Again, the best way to find out is to ask. Let them tell you in their own words. You might ask, "What would be the best solution for you in this situation?" Or, "What can I do to make sure you leave happy?" Now, this can be the scariest question (laughs) in all of customer service because you don't know what their response will be, but through all the research I've done, I found your customers ultimately only need three things from you at the end of the day.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-handling-abusive-customers-25071433/assessing-the-customer-service-interaction?u=76281980&t=94)** They need to feel heard, understood, and cared for. By asking what they want in a sincere manner, your customer will feel all three of those things. In addition, once your customer feels these things, their response to this question is usually quite reasonable. Now, if the customer's still angry after that second question, or they're making unreasonable demands, this is where the third question comes in. What can you do to solve their problem, even if it's not the exact outcome they desire? Your intent is to show them that they've been heard, understood, and cared for. Go ahead and write that phrase down now so you can use it next time your customer asks for more than you can deliver. It shows you're maxing out your ability to help them. With these three questions, you'll be better equipped to handle abusive customers quickly and effectively.

First, remind your customer that you care about them, that they're important to you and your company, and you want them to leave happy. Second, apologize for not being able to serve them at this time. Tell them that you cannot serve them while they're being abusive towards you, and to come back or call back when they can have a calm conversation and be respectful. Be careful with the language that you use in that second step. How do you feel when someone says to you, "Calm down"? That phrase makes most people even more upset. Say, instead, "I care about you and solving your issue. My goal is for you to leave happy, but I cannot continue to help you when you are upset. Please call back when you can have a calm and respectful conversation." Then hang up the phone or walk away from the customer if you're face to face with them. Most of the time,
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-handling-abusive-customers-25071433/ending-the-customer-interaction?u=76281980&t=93)** this will successfully end the interaction with that particular customer. If the customer refuses to leave or keeps calling you back, you need to take further action. If you have a manager, notify them that you're having trouble ending the transaction with that customer. If you feel like you're in physical danger or if the customer continues to manipulate you or demean you, get security or run away from that area and call the authorities immediately. When someone is emotionally out of control or looking to harm you, get as far away from that person as fast as you can and get to safety. I came up with a simple code system that you and your managers can implement into your policies and procedures for times like this. There are three stages to the system and they are color coded. The first stage is code orange. This is when you have a customer that is using explicit language, yelling, and has disdain towards your company or his situation. The second stage is code yellow. This is when your customer is using explicit language, yelling, and degrading or being abusive towards you. The third stage is code red. This is when your customer is using explicit language, yelling, degrading, and threatening you either mentally or physically. Take some time at your next team meeting to discuss these codes and what behavior in each one looks like to you
>
> **[3:07](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-handling-abusive-customers-25071433/ending-the-customer-interaction?u=76281980&t=187)** and your team. You can even use the code system in their account file if they're a repeat offender. This is a great way managers can potentially protect their people proactively. The great thing about this system is that it helps you recognize and label an escalating situation so you can take the appropriate action. Above all, I want to remind you that as a customer service representative, you can end any transaction at any time if you feel unsafe. You do not have to tolerate abuse from customers.
