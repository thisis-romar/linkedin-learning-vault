#### [Give a limited response on your customer call](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-call-control-strategies/give-a-limited-response-on-your-customer-call?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-call-control-strategies/give-a-limited-response-on-your-customer-call?u=76281980&t=0)** - Think of conversations with your customers as a ball being passed back and forth. When the customer says something, they're passing the ball to you, and when you speak, you're throwing the ball back. If you want to pause the passing game, all you have to do is hold the ball. In other words, you can pause the conversation by giving a limited response. Let's unpack this. If your friendly customer asks, "How are you?", you can answer politely and concisely, "I'm well. How nice of you to ask. How may I help you?" This replies stops the back and forth that would've happened had your answer been, "I'm doing well. How are you?" The limited response technique is a polite way to control conversations by not offering up more detail than needed. Here are three different ways you can swap a longer reply for a limited response. First, answer personal questions swiftly and then move right into how you can help. For example, if a client asks, "How are your children?", you could limit detail and share, "My children are fantastic. What can I do for you?" Next, choose not to debate with the customer when you disagree. If your customer tells you they've been on hold for 12 minutes, but your display shows a hold time of four minutes, give a minimal, non-combative response such as, "That sounds frustrating. What can I do for you?" A friendly tone is essential here.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/customer-service-call-control-strategies/give-a-limited-response-on-your-customer-call?u=76281980&t=95)** You don't want to come off as annoyed or brushing the customer's reaction off. Finally, make sure you don't belabor the point. Unless your customer asks for more information or it's crucial for you to share something, don't provide excessive detail. Still being friendly, of course, jump to the point of what the customer needs and then wrap up the conversation. "I've just sent you an email with your tracking number and my contact information. Reach out with any questions." When you use the limited response technique to control conversations, it's important that you're polite and friendly. Make sure you don't come across as rushed, irritated, or blunt.

A third way to make customers feel heard is to offer little reassurances as they vent, while your customer is telling you about their issue. Make customers feel heard and understood, and with ease you'll steer the conversation forward and bring things to a quick closure.
