> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-learning-design/what-is-inclusive-learning-design?u=76281980&t=1)** - Do you ever feel like there's a lot of terminology spinning around the world of design, and as soon as you learn something new, the term is updated or discontinued? Like cars, fashion, even social media apps, approaches to learning design and the terms that support them can go in and out of style. In an attempt to offer the latest and greatest terms related to Inclusive Learning Design and how to differentiate between them, here's some to know. First, learning design in its simplest form is the practice of designing and developing learning experiences and accompanying materials. As designers, we organize instructional content to make it digestible and engaging for the learner. Next, inclusive design creates blueprints for [[Microsoft Products|products]] with the full range of human diversity in mind. By including and learning from people with diverse perspectives in the design process, we enrich experiences, strengthen our connections and develop products for all users. Combining these two definitions and approaches, Inclusive Learning Design, or ILD, applies methods to design learning, content and experiences by placing learner's backgrounds, perspectives and needs at the center of the design. These needs and perspectives not only guide design decisions, but also shape and enhance an overall learning experience.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-learning-design/what-is-inclusive-learning-design?u=76281980&t=94)** Here, diversity is considered a tool to inform the design. Now I get a lot of questions about the difference between accessibility and inclusion, since both of them are usually referenced in [[Product Design]]. So let's explore this. I like to think that accessibility and design live under the bigger umbrella of inclusion. [[Accessible Design]] refers to design with the needs of differently abled people in mind. Inclusive design focuses on designing for a broader range of a potential audience. It accounts for locations, disabilities, neuro diversities, perspectives of people, and anything else that may impact the ability and/or equal access to a learning experience. Inclusive Learning Design might also seem similar to Universal Design for Learning, or UDL. UDL is a framework that aims to give all students an equal learning opportunity. The difference between UDL and ILD is that universal design attempts to design for everyone, and Inclusive Learning Design attempts to adapt the learning experience to reach the full range of a known and targeted audience. An inclusive approach to learning design takes the learner's whole self into consideration at the beginning of the design process, and then designs content and systems that adapt and accommodate to that whole self.
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-learning-design/what-is-inclusive-learning-design?u=76281980&t=189)** And as a result, learners and instructors or facilitators have richer interactions, a deeper understanding of subjects and connectivity to those around them. As I've explained these terms and concepts, what came up for you? Are there ones you are familiar with? As you move forward in your exploration of ILD, don't get too bogged down in the details or worry about what's in or out. Just make sure you know what your intentions are with Inclusive Learning Design and build from there.

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-learning-design/the-five-steps-of-ild?u=76281980&t=0)** - When you're setting out to build a learning experience or a course or a program, you'll map out the steps from start to finish. Many instructional design approaches lay out a process in steps to get you from here to there. ADDIE, SAM, [[Design Thinking]] to name just a few. The five steps of inclusive learning design are meant to do the same thing, offer a step-by-step approach to integrate inclusion into your design. They're also meant to easily align to and layer on any instructional design approach you're using so they become a companion to your existing process. Your front seat sidekick, if you will. The essence of the five steps is they take a human-centered shift-left approach. The term shift-left is commonly used in [[Software Development]] when issues are detected early in a development process. This applies to learning when we identify learner needs, motivations, and preferences at the beginning of a project lifecycle and then respond by integrating inclusive learning design throughout the process. >
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-learning-design/the-five-steps-of-ild?u=76281980&t=96)** First, getting to know the learners. Here, we start to identify who we are reaching in our learning, the gaps in that reach, and then explore how we can decrease or close those gaps. Next, applying the four pillars of ILD. They include building personalized, responsive, flexible, and communal learning into your design, then implementing ILD. Here, you consider what it will take to release an inclusive learning offering to your audience, potential barriers you might face and who your partners are. And finally, assessing impact. One note, you might not get to all these steps in your first design iteration. You could already be deep in the development phase of your project when you start considering ILD,
