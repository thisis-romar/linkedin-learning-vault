While the terms are often used interchangeably, I'll share a few general guidelines about some distinguishing characteristics. A speech is typically considered more formal than a presentation. It may be part of a ceremony or cultural ritual and therefore includes certain written and unwritten rules for what should be included. A traditional speech tends to include less audience participation directly with the speaker. A presentation is typically more conversational and more interactive. It's often more informal and may take place on a regular basis, say at regular staff meetings, for example, where a speech might take place at an annual staff retreat. Most speeches don't offer visual aids for the audience to look at as the information is being delivered. Presentations, on the other hand, typically include visual aids that the audience can see or even touch. Now, none of these rules are set in stone. If you've been invited to speak, the best thing to do is to find out what the expectations of your talk are. You don't want to prepare an informal and interactive presentation if the occasion calls for a more formal and traditional speech, and vice versa. The good news is the tips included in this course apply to both speeches and presentations. All you have to do is adjust these best practices to fit the event or occasion you're speaking at. > **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-and-delivering-speeches/speaking-to-persuade?u=76281980&t=1)** - Even if you're the boss or a subject matter expert, that doesn't mean you can simply tell people what to do and expect them to follow along. Some might, which is awesome, but many will require some nudging or persuading. And this is where understanding and applying the elements of persuasion will come in handy. A well-delivered persuasive speech can be a career defining moment. Think of the best speeches you've heard in your lifetime, from leaders, politicians, and so on. They grab and keep your attention and interest. They influence your [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md). They make the little hairs on your arms stand up. But why? What makes them so appealing? They reach you at your core because of reasons tied to the science of persuasion. These speeches include appeals to credibility, emotion, and logic. They also clearly explain the why, or key points, well and use examples or metaphors that paint a vivid picture. While exceptional speakers make all of this seem natural, anyone can be persuasive using the right tactics. I'll walk you through some of the most notable strategies for winning over your audience. When writing a speech, here are ways you can incorporate signals of your credibility. Cite credible sources, point to common experiences, and remind the audience how you know what you know by talking about your expertise and past experiences.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-and-delivering-speeches/speaking-to-persuade?u=76281980&t=96)** Or, if you're uncomfortable tooting your own horn, make sure relevant accolades are included in the introduction that's given before you take the platform. This will help you appear credible as you deliver your speech. You can incorporate emotion in both your writing and delivery as well. Remember that audiences respond to what they feel. We trust our gut when it comes to making big decisions. When writing your speech, use language that evokes emotion. Then match the emotion of your words with your delivery. If you're speaking about an embarrassing or disappointing outcome, your facial expressions, tone, and body language should be aligned. Similarly, if you're excited about a promising new innovation, that passion should come through in the delivery. The last of the three pillars is incorporating a logical appeal. You can be credible, use appropriate emotion, but if the logic of your arguments are difficult to follow, you'll lose your audience. When writing your speech, organize your content in a simple and easy-to-follow format. Give only the necessary details that help your audience understand the sound logic of your recommendation. Remember that audiences are not necessarily

It's interesting to note, many are more persuaded by the idea of shielding themselves from losses than they are by thinking about potential gains. For example, in a research study, participants were more likely to be persuaded to make an investment if you tell them they'll lose X amount per day, rather than telling them they'd save Y amount per day. This formula, the three pillars of influence, dates all the way back to Aristotle. Remember to appeal to credibility, emotion, and logic when writing and delivering persuasive speeches.

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-and-delivering-speeches/delivering-bad-news?u=76281980&t=1)** - No one wants to be the bearer of bad news. And yet at times you may have to deliver difficult or negative messages during a speech. Given the delicate nature of your content you'll want to set up your structure and delivery with a nuanced approach. First, open with a buffer. Start off by acknowledging the positive, or something good about the circumstances you're facing. You'll want to give the crowd some context to kick off. Then transition in a way that signals you have some bad news to share. Don't linger here. Just go ahead and get to it. Next, provide the negative information, say it directly and clearly.
