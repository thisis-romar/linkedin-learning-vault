> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/empathy-map-usage?u=76281980&t=1)** - The tool most commonly called an empathy map is a bit of a misnomer. You aren't documenting someone's empathy is a situation or for a product, rather you're looking to represent what you know about a specific user or user group in order to build empathy for them. Empathy is a commonly used but not always well-understood term in UX. There are actually multiple kinds of empathy but in this context, we're talking about the ability to deeply understand another person and their perspective. You don't necessarily need to share their feelings or agree with their point of view but you need to get where they're coming from so you can make decisions that consider their stance and needs. With that in mind, an empathy map is a tool to quickly summarize a user or type of user to help others understand them. Empathy maps are very often created as part of or tangent to persona discovery work. And they're intended to help you quickly summarize core descriptors about each persona. If you're not already familiar with personas try checking out UX Foundations: Research for an overview and UX Design: 3 Creating Personas. In short, personas are archetypal representations of different types of users. You might start with something called the proto-persona which is your best guess as to what defines your users and a starting hypothesis to conduct further research. You'll want to explore and learn things like their basic role in a system, core motivation and goals, current behavior including things like pain points or work-arounds, and desired outcome.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/empathy-map-usage?u=76281980&t=96)** Importantly, you'll want to figure out what delineates one type of user from another and tell a story about who the people are, why they behave the way they do, and what they ultimately need. The building of an empathy map can help provide a framework as you analyze and synthesize data about your users. Building out the map can also help you see what sort of information you might be missing. If you create individual maps, like after conducting a single interview, you can use the maps to spot trends, or see patterns, and begin to understand the nuances between and within user sets. The completed map can be used to help other team members initially understand the users and keep those users top of mind throughout the process. I've used maps to help explain users to cross-functional teams that include UX, product, and development members and then reference the maps during discussions about future plans to remind team members about who we're building for and what they really need. I recommend viewing the maps as living documents, updating them as you learn more about your users and keeping them at the center of conversation. You can use maps during planning and brainstorming sessions or design discussions to make sure you're always thinking of your user base. Empathy maps are a great tool to help you and your team understand your users.

#### [Building an empathy map](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-an-empathy-map?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-an-empathy-map?u=76281980&t=0)** - An empathy map is a way to quickly summarize what you learn about your users. In this context, the map is a visual representation of components but doesn't necessarily represent any relationships or imply a time or order to the pieces of information. An empathy map is usually split into four quadrants where you represent what your persona says, does, thinks, and feels. While not everything represented will be specifically related to the product or service you're working on, it'll paint a picture of the person as a whole and how your product fits into their life. You typically put a representative image or the name of the persona type in the middle and create four sections around it. The says section can be taken literally from research sessions and not to represent typical verbatim quotes from this kind of user. Let's say that you're working on a new type of retirement savings tool and you've identified a persona that describes themselves as unfamiliar with saving or investment options but wants to learn about how to put more money aside for their future. This is the information that they are willing to externalize and the words they use to describe themselves. The does area describes the actions that a user takes. You'll want to include information about the individual activities and the context within which or how they go about them. For instance, you might find that the person who describes themselves as unfamiliar with investing signed up for their employer's 401(k) offering when hired but never checks or adjusts anything. You'll also notice that what they do may not be aligned with what they say.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-an-empathy-map?u=76281980&t=94)** For instance, you might learn that a person who states that they actively save X amount of money is actually putting away less than they admit. It's important to note that a user may not be intentionally hiding things or even aware of the mismatch but you need to be aware of both. The thinks section is a description of what's running through a person's mind at various points. You'll want to describe what is most top of mind, what comes up again and again, and what takes precedence. And again, what users think may or may not be aligned with what they say or do. In fact, you'll want to pay particular attention to uncovering the things that people are thinking but not necessarily vocalizing. For instance, maybe someone feels confused or frustrated but doesn't want to appear not to be knowledgeable or they're reluctant to give critical feedback. The feels quadrant represents the emotional state of the user type for various situations. You'll want to identify the specific emotion separate from their thinking but that may be a result of their thoughts or the fuel for their behaviors. It's helpful to identify an emotion and a little bit of context about how and when that feeling occurs. For instance, our financial tool user might feel worried that they'll never have enough savings or overwhelmed every time they try to look up different options. In some versions of an empathy map, you might want to specifically call out individual components of the sections such as pain points or unspoken goals. Pay attention to where the quadrants overlap
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-an-empathy-map?u=76281980&t=188)** and where there are inconsistencies because this can help you understand the nuances of the user such as when they have thoughts they aren't willing to verbalize. Otherwise, don't worry too much about where the information lives as long as you have all the relevant information to tell your user's story. Remember that an empathy map is meant to be a summary of information that will help people understand their users. It's up to you to decide how much detail is necessary but an empty section probably means you need to do some more investigation. If you have tons of information for each section I recommend curating the content to best illustrate relevant points that pertain to what you're trying to build. In all, an empathy map can be a great way to build understanding of your users and their core elements.

#### [What is a journey map?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-journey-map?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-journey-map?u=76281980&t=1)** - A journey map is just what it sounds like. It's a visualization that documents a person's steps through a particular process or interactions with a product or service. It's presented chronologically and typically focused on a single person or persona-types perspective. You may hear this called just a journey map, a customer journey map, or a user journey map. While not all users are technically customers, for mapping purposes you can use any term. Journey maps help build a shared understanding of that user and their context. There are two main types of journey maps, those that document the existing state of affairs and those that suggest an ideal future state. Documenting the current precises can help build shared focus on individual users and their needs and highlight critical ping points or other opportunities to improve. The active building of journey maps together can be used to increase cross-functional empathy and understanding of users and ensure that teams are aligned. You'll want to base current state maps on a wide breadth of data. You should gather any existing information such as site analytics, feedback from support representatives, or sales feedback. You can use that information to inform hypothesis that frame the [[Representational State Transfer (REST)|rest]] of your research. While no project looks exactly the same it's very likely you'll want to both talk to and observe your users, so things like interviews, usability tests, diaries or other longitudinal studies and contextual inquiries,
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-journey-map?u=76281980&t=95)** or other ethnic graphic studies are likely. The goal of current state mapping is to factually represent the steps, interaction points, channels and behaviors and layer on narrative about the users [[Microsoft Outlook|outlook]] and emotions. Ultimately you want to build shared understanding so you can collectively work to improve the experience. To that end, you may also use a journey map as a tool to plan out what an ideal scenario would look like. You can look at your current state to see where are the biggest opportunities to improve, assess competitors or associated providers, or brainstorm innovative solutions. The future state map can be used to help you get on the same page about what work ought to come next. Either way journey maps should be used as a tool to help everyone see the full picture of what's happening and build shared vision to guide future work.

#### [Journey map preparation](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/journey-map-preparation?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/journey-map-preparation?u=76281980&t=1)** - Journey maps are flexible tools used to help visualize users' paths, and their associated needs and opportunities along the way. They're also used to build shared understanding across team members of those journeys and potential next steps. Because journey maps are supposed to represent a holistic view of all users' actions to achieve a specific goal or perform a specific task, it's important to have input from cross-functional team members. While it's probably up to the UX team members to conduct research and lead the way on visuals, make sure to include stakeholders from across departments and teams, to share their existing knowledge, lend their thoughts on scope and goals, and stay in the loop as you learn new things about your users and their context. Before you start building out your journey map, you should set a scope specifying the user and goal or activity you want to represent. Journey maps can be flexible, and scale up or down, but narrowing down a scenario helps you focus, and helps the map be clear. For instance, let's say you're working on creating an app for new parents. Trying to map out all of the considerations, conversations, tasks, appointments, activities, and responsibilities for all preparation would be way too long and complicated. But, it might make sense to map out the scenario of brand new parents signing up for a baby registry.

#### [Building a journey map](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-a-journey-map?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-a-journey-map?u=76281980&t=0)** - Once you've determined scope, it's time to start gathering the information you need to put together your journey map. Regardless of the type, journey maps are typically comprised of the same core parts. The first section sets the context of what you're looking at and includes a summary of the actor whose journey will be shown and a brief description of the scenario you're mapping. The next section describes the core of the journey and generally includes a high-level breakdown of the phases, the specific interactions and touchpoints, and a description of the user's mindsets and emotions. Emotion is often presented as a graph over time. There may also be an additional section that ties the information together and provides relevant information, such as pain points to address, opportunities to improve, notes on future plans or who owns the data. This section will be determined based on the context and what you learn. Remember, all of this information should be derived from your research you're conducting with your team. You can begin by mining existing research and then create a plan to capture the information you don't already know. If you need a research refresher, check out UX Foundations: Research. Even if you're creating a future state map, you'll still want to have a base of core research to thoroughly understand your users, their goals, needs, and motivations. Research won't give you the solutions, but it should inform what ideal would look like from their perspective. When it comes to creating the visual, there's no one exact right way or specific tool you ought to use.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-a-journey-map?u=76281980&t=95)** But remember that your goal is to tell a story that will help people understand the user and their journey. I like to sketch out the journey using a whiteboard and Post-its before I commit to anything digital. But of course, your mileage may vary. It's also important to be sure to make the mapping a collaborative process. So consider blocking off a few hours for the cross-functional team to work on the initial draft. I recommend starting by breaking down the core phases of the journey in chronological order. You don't have to be super precise, and you can always change, but you want to form a skeleton to build off of. For instance, let's say you've decided that you're mapping the experience of an expecting parent buying a car to accommodate their growing family. Your core sections might be something like trigger, research, test, and purchase. You'll then want to layer in additional levels of information. I'd next go to the specific actions, interactions, and related emotions or mindset. For instance, during the research phase, you might have the actions of review customer reports and the related emotion of feeling overwhelmed by choices and how expensive the best options are. You'll also want to specifically call out touchpoints and channels. For instance, if you find that your users are likely to do research both on their phone and at their desktop, take note of that and how those experiences may differ. Maybe information is even more overwhelming on the phone due to limited space. Once you've fleshed out the core data,
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/building-a-journey-map?u=76281980&t=188)** I like to look for ways to connect the pieces of data to quickly communicate relationships. For instance, you might overlay a line graph of the emotional state of the user during different stages to point out which parts of the process are most painful, color-code similar actions, or demonstrate connections with arrow lines. The nature of the connection visuals will depend on your context, but the goal is to tie together discrete pieces of information into a visual narrative. Finally, you might want to call out additional insights that aren't specific to any one part of the map or that need more space, such as the core opportunities you observed. It's up to you and your team to consider the context of the project and decide how detailed and what format to make the journey map. Sometimes, a picture of sticky notes on a whiteboard might be enough to have gotten everyone aligned. And other times, you'll need to create a polished digital deliverable that will live on its own. You may choose a traditional column and row format or get creative with the arrangement of data. Just remember, a journey map is meant to be a tool that quickly communicates a user's story and helps build alignment. Ideally, it can be interpreted on its own and circulated to other team members and referenced later. It should be a living document that stays part of design conversations and evolves with new insights.

#### [What is a experience map?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-experience-map?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-experience-map?u=76281980&t=0)** - An experience map is really just an extension of an individual journey map, meant to show that particular journey in context of a larger experience. While a journey map is focused on one persona and one discreet scenario or goal, an experience map visualizes a holistic end-to-end experience. The scope will be much broader and necessarily more general. For instance, you might create one journey map for a new parent to purchase a vehicle and another journey map for their child to inherit it and take over scheduling service appointments. You'd use an experience map to describe the total experience of vehicle ownership, including both of those scenarios. Each user will not have the exact same behaviors or experience things in the same order, but the goal is to represent an overarching story describing the whole picture of an experience or behavior. Mapping the entire experience allows a team to see how a product or service is integrated into a greater context and better plan for next steps. Just like with journey maps, experience maps ought to be based on a wide breadth of research data. You'll want to include much of the same information, such as the chronological steps of the experience, description of actions and interactions, identification of channels of communication, description of user's mindset and emotions, and insights about opportunities or other takeaways. Because the scope is broader, you might need to compile and organize the information a bit differently.
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-experience-map?u=76281980&t=93)** For instance, you might start by listing out the chronological phases of the experience, then identifying the specific touchpoints or high-level actions, and doing some prioritization to determine how many steps need to be represented. Take the vehicle ownership experience. For a journey map of an expecting parent, it would likely be very important that they test drive the vehicle with their car seat and check how easy it is to install and maneuver the seat. But in an overall experience of vehicle ownership, that could be compiled into one of the more granular elements of test driving and not be individually represented. The information is meant to represent a general experience, and as always, that experience will dictate the specific components and how you model the relationships. It's up to you to choose how to best illustrate the experience in a digestible way.

#### [What is a service blueprint?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-service-blueprint?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-service-blueprint?u=76281980&t=1)** - Another common map is known as a service blueprint, which is really just the counterpart of a customer journey, documenting internal processes and employee actions that support that journey. Service blueprints describe the complicated processes and players involved with providing all the components of a service. Both those that are visible to the end users and the backend actions. Let's think of the example of a hotel. There are many moving parts that go into providing a unified service. At a minimum, there will be a system for creating and keeping track of reservations, staff that needs to help guests check-in and out, staff that needs to clean and service rooms, a system that keeps track of additional costs incurred during the stay, and various touchpoints for employees, hotel guests, and the systems to interact. The process for building a service blueprint is much the same as that for journey maps. You'll need to start by choosing a specific scenario, and then conducting research to understand the end users and the internal players and processes. The end user research is much the same, but you'll also need to be sure that you include employees and stakeholders as participants. When conducting internal research, keep in mind that the way things happen in real life doesn't often exactly match training guides or ideal situations. Make sure able to talk to you and observe real employees and be sure that they won't get into trouble for their workarounds or modifications to processes. Once you've gathered your information, map out the end users core steps just like a journey map, but then, rather than focusing on the end users,
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/what-is-a-service-blueprint?u=76281980&t=96)** describe the employee or internal actions, processes and touchpoints. Make sure to highlight both the front-end interactions that are visible to customers such as a clerk answering the phone, and backend processes that aren't as visible such as the inventory system being updated after an order is placed. Visually separate customer interactions from internal actions and processes and highlight what is and isn't visible to customers. You'll also want to take note of tangible evidence which are the physical props or other tangible items that can impact experience such as price tags, signage, delivery vehicles, et cetera. Once you've mapped out the core steps, you'll also want to layer on supporting elements such as arrows to denote relationships, timestamps or emotional state. Particularly when focused on internal processes, you may have things like regulatory concerns or currently measured metrics that you could layer in to help give context and paint a full picture. For instance, in a restaurant, you might want to take note of food safety regulations or length of time from order to delivery. This kind of mapping is especially helpful for evaluating internal processes and driving change within an organization. It will give you a non-judgemental way to view gaps, tie internal actions to end user goals, and point out where things could be improved. Service blueprints are a valuable tool for shedding light on complicated internal processes, and building alignment on how to optimize offerings.

There are also some non-UX-specific tools and techniques with very similar names that you might come across in your work. Here are a few of the most common. Storymapping is the more general term for using classic narrative structure as a tool to help visualize and communicate ideas. You can use storymapping to describe things like the overall value of a product or experience, the specific steps someone takes through the product, or how they come to use the product. The point is to use the well-established sequence of events that make up a story in the context of a product. Very generally, a story always follows the same structure, with a beginning, middle, and end, where context is set, action builds, and then some sort of resolution is reached. The beginning of the story is called the exposition, which sets the context, introduces the people involved, and establishes a goal for the main character. In the context of UX, that main character is often the target user or persona. The inciting incident occurs when something gets in the way of said goal, inspiring change or action. This is usually the core need or challenge said persona has. The rising action is the core of the story, the series of events or steps that the main character or user takes. Eventually, there will be a crucial moment called the crisis which is a dramatic event or period that serves
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-mapping/other-types-of-maps?u=76281980&t=94)** as a turning point for the user. Whatever happens next is called the climax, the peak of excitement and action in the story. In movies, these are the final battle scenes, the exciting kiss, or the dramatic deaths. The final actions that serve to tie up loose ends, provide closure, and answer final questions are called the denouement. These are the crucial steps that help a story feel complete. Storymapping uses these steps to highlight the important players, context, and actions for [[Microsoft Products|products]]. Like with other kinds of mapping, you can conduct research and use narrative arc to describe what you found or use story mapping to plot out ideal future scenarios. Donna Lichaw has an amazing book that provides much more detail on storymapping and its various uses. Similarly named but quite different in context, you might have also heard of a user storymap. This particular tool isn't a strictly UX practice but it's commonly used within [[Agile Development|agile]] development teams. In agile environments, a user's story is a description of work that needs to get done, written from the perspective of the person who will use it. User stories usually follow a pretty simple template. As a type of user, I want a thing so that reason. This helps agile teams keep their target audience and their end goal in mind. For example, let's say you're working

These stories can be really high level so they're often broken down into more granular tasks and stories. With that in mind, a user storymap is a way to model the individual user stories together so the team can understand the core goals and visualize the necessary work. Very roughly, a user storymap breaks out core tasks or activities within a product or service and then categorizes the smaller, more detailed steps and tasks that need to happen to accomplish those larger goals. The top level or backbone of the map roughly correlates to the core features or capabilities and the steps underneath are the more granular things that need to happen but they're all done from the point of view of the end user.
