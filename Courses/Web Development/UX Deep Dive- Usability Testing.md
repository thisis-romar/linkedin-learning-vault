#### Moderated vs. unmoderated usability tests
> [LinkedIn Learning](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=0)** - Along with the option to conduct sessions remotely, more and more tools have been popping up to conduct unmoderated sessions, where you write out individual tasks for the users, and the participants complete each task on their own time.
>
> **[0:14](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=14)** A tool provides the instructions, shows the questions, and records the actions of the user.
>
> **[0:19](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=19)** There is no direct interaction between the participants and the researcher, which means that you need to plan unmoderated sessions even more carefully.
>
> **[0:29](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=29)** They're also most appropriate for certain scenarios only.
>
> **[0:32](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=32)** Because you pre-write the introduction, the core tasks, and the followup questions, unmoderated tests are best for live or very functional work that requires very little explanation.
>
> **[0:44](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=44)** When you show a low fidelity or early stage prototype in person, you can explain limitations and provide context along the way.
>
> **[0:52](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=52)** It's possible to navigate around known issues or work around empty areas when you moderate.
>
> **[0:58](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=58)** Unmoderated sessions are best suited for straightforward, clear tasks that don't leave much room for interpretation, like assessing how easily someone can find a certain sort of a product on an eCommerce page, or how long it takes to check out on one site versus another.
>
> **[1:13](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=73)** Participants are frequently put into artificial situations in usability tests anyway, but without a moderator present, it's even easier to fake things or tune out.
>
> **[1:23](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=83)** Tasks that require emotional engagement or creativity are unlikely to get an authentic response in an unmoderated test.
>
> **[1:32](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=92)** You also need to carefully construct the tasks to be sure they will get you the results you want.
>
> **[1:38](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=98)** Turning goals into clear tasks already takes practice to master, and all the normal best practices apply.
>
> **[1:44](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=104)** But writing unmoderated tasks is even more difficult.
>
> **[1:48](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=108)** You have to explicit about you want the participant to do and when they should stop.
>
> **[1:53](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=113)** And provide clear, specific, and realistic instructions, all without being too obvious.
>
> **[2:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=120)** Make sure the tasks are written as directions, not questions.
>
> **[2:03](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=123)** And be precise in the instruction.
>
> **[2:06](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=126)** Let's say we were testing the LinkedIn Learning site.
>
> **[2:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=129)** A good task might be something like, find a UX related LinkedIn Learning course you haven't yet watched, and stop when you get to a brief description of the course.
>
> **[2:20](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=140)** Remember, participants won't have a chance to clarify, and you won't have a chance to probe.
>
> **[2:25](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=145)** So if they misinterpret, the task may be wasted.
>
> **[2:28](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=148)** It's especially important to pilot test unmoderated studies so that you can get the most authentic useful feedback.
>
> **[2:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=156)** You also don't get the chance to probe, so make sure you include followup questions that are just as thoughtfully crafted.
>
> **[2:43](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=163)** With all those limitations in mind, unmoderated studies also have some great benefits.
>
> **[2:48](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=168)** You can run sessions in parallel and therefore collect many responses in a short amount of time.

> **[2:54](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=174)** You save time and have access to a much larger and more spread out participant base than you might otherwise.
>
> **[3:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=181)** You can usually offer a lower incentive to save yourself some money as well.
>
> **[3:05](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=185)** I've used unmoderated studies to get super quick feedback from across the globe literally overnight.

> **[3:11](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=191)** Everything from response to a new design direction, to interpretation of messaging options, to gut-checking which of an interaction pattern best suited our goals.
>
> **[3:21](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=201)** These broad, quick studies have been especially useful when working in agile settings, and we needed data super fast.
>
> **[3:28](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=208)** I also like to use unmoderated studies when I'm benchmarking and comparing the results over time.
>
> **[3:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=214)** That makes it easy to set up and assures the questions remain consistent.
>
> **[3:38](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-usability-testing/moderated-vs-unmoderated-usability-tests?u=76281980&t=218)** As always, carefully consider the core goal and context of your research goals to determine the right kind of test for you.
