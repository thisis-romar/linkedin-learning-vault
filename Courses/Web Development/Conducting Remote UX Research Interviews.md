# Conducting Remote UX Research Interviews

> Interviews are one of the most important methods in a UX researcher’s tool kit. They’re crucial for gathering insights from key project stakeholders. But how do you conduct a successful interview when your team is working remotely? #### [Research planning](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/research-planning?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/research-planning?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Just like with any other research effort you want to begin by outlining your research goals and ensuring that interviews are the right way to gather the kind of information that you need. UX interviews are designed to help you explore people and their beliefs. We commonly use them in the early exploratory stages of a new product to help us define target personas, and throughout [Product Development](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Product%20Development.md) to see how people and their needs evolve. We really dig into people's motivations, fears, needs, intentions, goals, impressions, and attitudes. If this is the kind of information you need, you're on the right path. If you need something else, like to evaluate a design or explore a behavior, you probably want to choose something else. There's more on that in my [UX Research](../../Skills/Web%20Development/UX%20Research.md) Fundamentals course. Your research goals will also help you start to create your moderation or discussion guide, which is the plan that outlines the high-level topics you want to cover, identify specific questions you want to ask and areas you want to probe. The process for creating a moderation guide is much the same for remote sessions as in person, but I recommend pruning and leaving extra time for delays, tangents, or other issues. It's always a good idea to be crisp in your research focus, but I find that the ideal amount of time for a remote interview is about 60 minutes: 15 minutes for setup, expectation setting, et cetera, and 45 minutes of actual content. I find it's hard to get folks to stay engaged for much longer than an hour.
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/research-planning?u=76281980&t=93)** That's usually two to three overarching topics with lots of areas to probe and follow-up questions. Working in an interactive activity or visual stimulus can help keep participants engaged on remote sessions. For instance, on a recent project we wanted to explore reactions to some new internal processes, so we listed them on a slide deck and participants walked me through pros and cons. I didn't need the visualization and there wasn't much interaction, but it was something for both of us to look at while the participants were talking. While that may make your session veer from a very traditional interview, incorporating some activity into remote sessions can be really valuable. To enable that sort of thing, you'll want to use video conferencing software that allows sharing a voice, camera, and screen. There are dozens of ubiquitous tools that have fairly comparable feature sets. My priorities are my comfort with the tool and ability to control the settings, ease of use for participants, the ability to see participants' faces and let them see yours, recording capabilities, transcription, security, and integrations with my other favorite tools. Some UX-specific tools integrate additional features, like live note-taking, analysis tools, participant wait rooms, and stakeholder rooms with integrated chats. But if you're limited by your organization's restrictions or budget, pretty much any video conferencing tool will suffice. Just make sure you're comfortable doing things like sharing your screen,
>
> **[3:05](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/research-planning?u=76281980&t=185)** requesting participants to share their screen, muting people, and starting recording. You'll also want to be sure that you're able to communicate to stakeholders or the participant during the session. If there isn't a chat in your video tool, I recommend setting up messaging or text session. It's helpful to be able to reach each other in case there are technical difficulties with audio or someone has a question but doesn't want to interrupt. For observers you'll need to decide on a communication method that is the least distracting for you as the moderator and clearly set expectations about the best way to do that. If there're going to be multiple observers, I ask one person to collect the feedback and questions along the way and then send me a cohesive list at the end. I always remind people to make sure that they're muted, with their camera off, and to remain muted throughout the study. This expectation-setting phase is also when I like to talk about note-taking. I'll say this again and again, but I absolutely do not recommend that you try to take notes as a moderator of a remote interview. It can be distracting for both you and the participant, and you need every bit of attention to focus on the conversation. Note-taking preferences vary but for remote sessions I usually like to set up a shared spreadsheet with the core open questions or areas to probe and a column for each participant. That way, anyone who listens to any part of the sessions can input any relevant notes in context of the area they're related to, which will set you up for easier analysis later.
>
> **[4:39](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/research-planning?u=76281980&t=279)** If the tool you use has integrated timestamped notes, I recommend nominating a colleague to take advantage, at least to tag interesting spots where you may want to go back to grab a quote or clip. Whether or not you have anyone else to take notes, I always recommend recording the sessions. I prefer to record, whether remote or not, for transparency and to be able to make video clips, but especially with remote sessions you can sometimes miss a nuance the first time around. It is super useful to have the transcripts to go back and skim, and the videos to re-listen to. In all, planning out a remote UX session is still based on the same core principles as any interview. Being intentional about narrowing the scope of the moderation guide, incorporating activities, choosing appropriate tools, and setting internal expectations will help remote sessions go as smoothly as possible.

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-logistics?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Tutor] The best research sessions of all kinds are carefully planned and involve much more upfront work than is likely noticeable during the session. This is especially true when you're conducting remote interview sessions. As we've discussed, the core tool used for a remote interview is usually video conferencing software. While the tools have greatly improved, and more of the general population than ever is comfortable with the technology, it's absolutely crucial to be sure to test whatever setup you're planning to use and have backup plans ready, for when there are inevitable hiccups. I recommend laying out an ideal scenario, and then back up plans for the various things that could go wrong. For instance, if the video is choppy or won't connect, you can still plan to use your tool, but not see their face. If the audio quality is poor, you could have them call into a phone line instead of dialing in on their computer. If a participant isn't allowed to use a specific tool but can talk on the phone, maybe they can call you on FaceTime and you can record the screen of your computer. If screen sharing isn't working, maybe see if you have a backup tool or secure way to send them things. It can seem like overkill, but having tested troubleshooting options will help you stay calm when there are issues, find a solution quickly, and still be able to get valuable feedback out of your interview session. To that end, I also make sure that I clearly communicate a way for participants to reach out
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-logistics?u=76281980&t=94)** if they are running into any issues. This is personal preference, but I usually give them my email address and phone number and tell them to reach out however they feel most comfortable. The same is true for internal stakeholders or observers. You absolutely should not skip a pilot test for a remote interview. This is important both to test the technology and to test the length and flow of the conversation. I usually nominate a teammate, and even though I know it's not going to exactly represent the user base, it's easier to catch if the flow is off or if there's too much content to cover in the allotted amount of time. A few extra planning steps can help you feel more confident and prepared, allowing you to better focus on listening and probing.

#### [Remote moderation tips](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-moderation-tips?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-moderation-tips?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Instructor] While moderating interviews is a skill that takes time and practice to sharpen, even the best in-person moderators can struggle with remote sessions. There can be connection issues, audio issues, or any number of unforeseeable difficulties to overcome. The first absolutely crucial step to running a great remote interview sounds like a no brainer, but I highly recommend closing everything except the tool you're using to connect with your participant and your moderation guide. I can speak from personal experience the desire to multitask, but even the smallest amount of distraction can prevent you from being able to fully engage with your participant. So do your best to clear anything that might pull your attention away from what they're saying. No multitasking also means no note taking. It will be distracting for both you and the participant to hear you typing. You may lose eye contact or visibility of the participant, and you won't be able to listen as deeply. Get someone else to take notes if you can, and always record the session if possible. I usually ask for that permission to record when I recruit so that folks can think about whether or not they feel comfortable that when they sign up. Building rapport is important in every interview, but especially so in remote sessions. First things first, briefly introduce yourself and your goals. Remind participants that it's your job to learn about them without judgment and their job to be as honest and upfront as possible. Let them know that you may ask questions
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-moderation-tips?u=76281980&t=95)** a few different ways or ask for clarifications so that you aren't making any incorrect assumptions and getting the full picture. All of those things you normally do, but in particular, remind them that you'd like to record the session and describe how you'll use it. Hopefully, it's just for internal note taking purposes. Just really make sure that they feel comfortable with the setup and know what to expect from it. And don't forget to mention if there is anyone else already or going to be on the line. I prefer tools that allow you to control who the participant sees and allows you a way to communicate with internal folks separately. If you aren't able to use one of those tools, be sure that any other note-taker or listener is on mute and turns their camera off. This usually helps the participants feel a little bit less under the microscope and more comfortable to start talking. I also make sure that I allow remote participants to ask me questions before we get started and set expectations for how long we think we'll be, and give them opportunities to take breaks, if need be. With so many people working and connecting from so many different contexts, there are sometimes inevitable distractions. I'd much rather someone take a few minute break to answer their door, attend to their child, or respond to a message then have them attempt to multitask. That said, it's okay to acknowledge that distraction may be a part of their reality and actually incorporate whatever observations you have about that into your research. I've recently been working with an educational app for little kids and we've been [Interviewing](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Interviewing.md) parents.
>
> **[3:11](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-moderation-tips?u=76281980&t=191)** So many of them would apologize for interruptions from their children or be trying to shoo them off, but it was actually really insightful for us to see how they would distract their kids or try to keep them occupied when they're trying to focus. Either way, in these remote sessions, your ultimate goal is to help participants feel comfortable and try to get as an authentic contextual glimpse of their life as possible. Once you start the discussion, take extra care to speak clearly and allow the participant plenty of time to respond. Even with the best connection and best tools, there's a little bit of a cognitive delay when you are not physically with a person, so it's easier than usual to jump in or talk over people. The delay can also vary depending on your tools, so I am always deliberately extra quiet at the beginning of a session to feel out the cadence of that particular meeting. When I first started conducting remote research, I would often count to three in my head after thinking a person was finished speaking, just to make sure I didn't jump in. Over time, I've gotten better at learning the rhythm of remote conversations, but it's a great way to train yourself to give yourself a little extra time. Allowing that extra time will also create more opportunities for participants to go on tangents or bring up unexpected topics that may or may not be relevant to your research goals, but may be insightful. I never recommend following your moderation guide as an exact script, but I've found that it's even more
>
> **[4:45](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-moderation-tips?u=76281980&t=285)** important to focus on natural discussion flow in these remote sessions. I've also gotten even more into the habit of echoing or repeating back key insights I think that I've identified and asking for participants to verify what I've said. This helps make sure that I've interpreted what they're saying correctly and allows them the opportunity to clarify, expand, or confirm and help them feel confident that I'm following what they're saying. Again, it's really just all about helping participants feel comfortable and open. Make sure that your reactions reinforce that as well. Keeping neutral or a mildly pleasant expression, actively trying to make eye contact, asking follow ups that reinforce that you are listening and understanding, and providing your own physical cues that you're paying attention, such as nodding, will encourage remote participants. If you're not able to see the participant, it's especially important to provide verbal listening cues, such as repeating back what they've said and verbally acknowledging their points while they're talking. You know, the standard, "mhm", "of course", "yeah, that makes sense", all that sort of thing. Conducting a remote interview is a test of one's moderation skills. The same basic rules apply, but you need to pay even more attention to making your participant comfortable and drawing insights out.

Well it's always the case that you should let your research objectives drive your methods and therefore the kind of data you collect and how. I recommend really thinking through the specific logistics of what kind of data you're going to collect and how you're going to organize it when you're doing your remote interviews. As I mentioned in the planning stage, I recommend coming up with some sort of collaborative notes document. My personal preference is to give each participant a column and each row a core topic, allowing note takers to add rows as they go for any emergent themes. I also recommend pre agreeing on a place to store and watch the videos, the format for transcripts, what tools you might use to aid analysis, and how you'll start to record insights because you might have many options. >
> **[1:38](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/remote-analysis-and-synthesis?u=76281980&t=98)** organize tags, read transcripts, and create video clips. However, Zoom will create transcripts and sometimes I have clients who want assets in a shared drive. So I always set that up at the beginning and ask everyone to upload anything relevant to the proper place along the way. Then we can copy and paste into either a shared Google doc, slide deck, or occasionally another digital visualization tool like Miro. Being organized about how you'll collect the data makes the next step much easier. Once everything has been collected, you'll need to combine and collate all the raw data to start immersing yourself in it. Download video files and transcripts, ask everyone to enter their separate notes into one place and in one format and begin reviewing everything in its raw format. Again, having a preset digital place that everyone can access and having things in the same format from the start makes this collation process much simpler. Individual data immersion and initial data mining are both well suited for remote work. Each person can review the research goals and then dive into the data on their own listening, reading, and beginning to identify nuggets, then recording them in whatever format your team has agreed on like bullet points in a Google doc, notes in a spreadsheet, sticky notes, whatever works for you. Tagging and coding individual nuggets also works well remotely and asynchronously. I often like to create a unified bank of themes or tags
>

I include the tag and enough of a description to allow someone working alone to be able to make a judgment about their insight. Sometimes these tags are really obvious extensions of the goals, and sometimes they are new themes that emerge. When doing this sort of analysis remotely, I like to send out instructions for this data combination, cleaning, mining, and tagging to everyone involved and set a couple of meetings for folks to talk through what they find. Setting an initial joint meeting provides a specific timeframe for folks to enter all of their data and do all of their individual work. During the first collaborative session, I simply let everyone share what they've identified and start to combine findings that already naturally pair up. During the next session we begin to assess each theme deciding if it's distinct enough and how it's related to the others. I recommend a couple of these sessions spread over a few days to allow time to digest as you resort and reframe the data a few times to uncover the most authentic insights. Remember that it's often the collaborative discussion and reframing of things that ultimately drives the deepest shared insight. So don't rush yourself here. Unfortunately, there's no magic number of collaborative sessions. Though I found that my sweet spot for remote interviews tends to be three

> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Narrator] With all of these remote tools enabling us to continue conducting research and maybe even increasing the amount of research we're ultimately able to do, it's important to also know that just because we might be able to cram more sessions into a day it doesn't mean that we should. We've already talked about schedule a little bit, but [Zoom](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Zoom.md) fatigue is very real and conducting interviews is exhausting. Even under the best of circumstances. I often equate running remote interviews to remote speed dating. You want to provide a good enough impression but also jump into the nitty gritty. Sometimes very personal details of someone's life and you have limited time to do so. With that in mind, I strongly recommend limiting the schedule to no more than five sessions a day and making sure to include breaks in between. I know that's not always possible, but even a few minutes to process, get a drink, stretch and [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) your eyes can really help you stay sharp. Another tricky thing about conducting remote interviews is that one of the key causes for video conferencing fatigue is the high amount of very close face to eye contact. The perceived distance of another person's face tricks your brain into thinking that you're getting ready for a fight or other intense situation. So you become hyper alert and therefore burn out more quickly. You can arrange your screen view so that your mod guide is close to the camera and then be sure to reference it occasionally. This is also where integrating some
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980&t=94)** of those shared screen activities can be helpful too. You can still keep an eye on the participant but not feel quite so up close and personal to them. Another trick, maybe make sure that you use an external keyboard or even sit or stand a little bit back from your screen to give yourself a little bit more space. Another one of the reasons that video conferencing is so tiring is that you're constantly looking at yourself which causes you to constantly evaluate your appearance. It's not vanity, it's just human nature to self-assess. While it's important to make sure that you're framed somewhat well and you don't want to have anything that could distract the participant or be embarrassing on the recording, you don't actually need to stare at yourself during the session. So I usually view my image at first, just to do kind of a quick check of how I look. And then I minimize the view of myself. Not all platforms allow this, but I really recommend looking for that. All of the typical self-care things that you may do to keep yourself fresh are especially important when you're running remote interviews, things like making sure you're hydrated, getting enough sleep, having nourishing foods and moving your body are really crucial. We think of running research as a mental tax but it can be physically taxing also. Anything you can do to treat your body well will help you maintain energy and focus for the sessions. I also try hard to stagger my remote interview blocks so that I have at least a week or so in between wrapping up one set and starting the next.
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/conducting-remote-ux-research-interviews/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980&t=189)** This usually coincides well with dedicating time to synthesis. But I also like to think of it as a video break. I try hard to only conduct one type of interview at a time, that may not be an issue if you don't work on multiple projects at once like I do, but any additional context switching you might need to do between sessions increases mental drain. Burnout and Zoom fatigue are common problems for a lot of us working remotely anyway and conducting UX interviews can be especially intense and fatiguing. Make sure to take care of yourself. You'll be happier. You'll be better able to take care of your participants and ultimately your outcomes will be better too.
