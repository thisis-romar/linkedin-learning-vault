> **[2:04](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=124)** One of the most effective ways to share your research findings is to schedule a whole project team discussion of key takeaways and their implications.
>
> **[2:12](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=132)** Discuss what you observed, why it matters to the team, and what the team should do.
>
> **[2:18](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=138)** As an example, if you found a key usability issue of a checkout flow on an e-commerce page, it's tempting to say something generic like, "It's difficult for users to enter their credit card information correctly."
>
> **[2:30](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=150)** Giving additional context can be much more powerful.
>
> **[2:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=154)** Try something like, "It's difficult for customers to enter their credit card because we ask for the date in a format that does not match what they see on the card.
>
> **[2:44](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=164)** Many participants get frustrated at this point and drop out, which results in abandoned sales and a large potential loss of profit for the company.
>
> **[2:52](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=172)** We should update the form so that the date selector matches what customers see on their cards to maximize conversions."
>
> **[3:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=180)** Regardless of your final output or documentation format, you'll want to record key findings in a shared team wiki, research database, shared document, or even on a whiteboard.
>
> **[3:11](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=191)** Keep some sort of documentation of the main study details, such as what interface was tested or who was interviewed, so that you can look back at later times.
>
> **[3:21](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=201)** While it may not be necessary for sharing current findings, there are often times that you want to revisit previous research and it's much easier to record a small amount of data as you go, rather than try to remember or recreate after the fact.
>
> **[3:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-foundations-research-19417883/presenting-and-incorporating-results?u=76281980&t=214)** However you decide to document results, keep in mind that the most important thing you need to do is connect your data to the broader context of the business and help other people understand why it matters and what they should do about it.
