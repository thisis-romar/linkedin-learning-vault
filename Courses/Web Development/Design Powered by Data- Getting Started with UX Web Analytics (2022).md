However, in order to build great user experiences and create [[Microsoft Products|products]] that connect with your audience on a deeper level, we cannot look at design and data in silos. Throughout the course, you will learn why UX analysis sits at the intersection of data and design, and how the synergy between both fields is crucial when designing a meaningful [[User Experience (UX)|user experience]]. But first, let's talk about the definition of UX analysis. UX analysis is the process of collecting and evaluating data about how users interact with your product or service, then using these findings to enhance their overall experience. There are two sides to the UX analysis coin, a qualitative and quantitative one. Qualitative research deals with problems that are not clearly defined, and is used in the exploratory stages of research. For example, research methods such as focus groups, interviews, open-ended surveys, and behavioral observations are key techniques that can help you build up the context around your problem. As such, the results that qualitative research produces are often unstructured, and do not give you a definitive numeric answer. Quantitative research, on the other hand,
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-powered-by-data-getting-started-with-ux-web-analytics-2022/what-is-ux-analysis?u=76281980&t=93)** is all about measuring specific and usable metrics. This is done through research techniques like A/B testing, systematic observations, online surveys, and polls, all of which are used to quantify opinions, attitudes, behaviors, and other defined variables with a goal to support or refute the hypothesis. In this type of research, the results are numeric in nature and can be used as a benchmark. It is imperative that these two research modes work together and not against each other. It is only when you combine quantitative and qualitative data that you get a complete picture of the user experience.

Not communicating with your audience and prospective users can be detrimental to the UX design process. Remember, you're building something for an audience, not for yourself. In this lesson, you will learn how to design an A/B test to ensure you collect feedback and information from your users the right way. A/B testing, also known as split testing, has been a buzz [[Microsoft Word|word]] in a lot of industries, especially marketing. However, you shouldn't associate A/B testing with a particular tool or software, but rather treat it as a methodology that if executed correctly can produce an actionable insight. There are a few key steps in designing a meaningful A/B test. Let's go through them. Number one, identify your goal. This is the foundational step of your A/B test. So you want to make sure you get it right. In my experience, often people skip this step, assuming everybody understands the goal and purpose of the test, which is hardly ever the case. Amongst your team, ask yourself, what do we want to learn with this test? And would the expected result add any value to our business? For example, let's assume that you have a call to action image on your website, and you want to test whether having a person in the image, as opposed to an object,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-powered-by-data-getting-started-with-ux-web-analytics-2022/a-b-testing-and-experiments?u=76281980&t=94)** would resonate more with your audience. This [[Forms]] your hypothesis. If your hypothesis is true, then the call to action image with the person would yield a better engagement rate, and you would have an insight into how to build more appealing creatives. Hence creating a better user experience, and potentially attracting more business. Number two, select the right variables for the test. In research experiments, you would normally hear about an independent and a dependent variable. In essence, the independent variable is the lever you control throughout the experiment. To measure the impact, its movements are having on the dependent variable. I know it might sound confusing at the moment, but once you run a few tests, you'll get a better understanding of the mechanics. If we continue with our current example, the independent variable is the call to action image with the person or object. And the dependent variable is the engagement rate. You can see why, right? It's because the engagement rate is dependent on the results each call to action image is going to produce. Number three, create a control and test audience groups. This step can prove to be a bit tedious, especially if you were running a test for the first time. Luckily, a lot of the software and web applications these days have native A/B testing capabilities that can help you set up an experiment in no time.

In our scenario, the control group would include the users who will only see the call to action image with the object. It's important to note that you should test one variable at a time, whilst making sure all other conditions stay the same. This might sound silly, but I've witnessed companies wanting to change the KPI mid or post-test if the results did not look good. This of course defeats the entire purpose of the experiment, so don't fall for it. As you can see, A/B testing can be an incredibly powerful tool for collecting information and feedback.
