This additional contextual feedback will help you see a more realistic view of a person and the things that influence their behaviors.

#### [Reasons to choose the traditional research approach](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/reasons-to-choose-the-traditional-research-approach?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/reasons-to-choose-the-traditional-research-approach?u=76281980&t=0)** - While there are some real advantages to conducting research remotely, there's always going to be a place for face-to-face research. When you're sitting with someone, it's easier to build rapport, read somebody's face and body language, and understand when to probe appropriately. It might look a little different in a post-COVID world, but there's always going to be a place for face-to-face research. When the specific physical context or environment is a large component of the overall experience, you probably want to be in-person. For instance, let's say you're [Usability Testing](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Usability%20Testing.md) a mobile app for a construction project manager. You'll probably need to see what it's like for someone to be on a construction site with safety equipment, like gloves on, and dealing with the elements, like bright sun or a lot of dust. You may also need to observe a physical space to generate insights about a particular process, like you need to see how they file paperwork in their [office](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Microsoft%20Office.md) or store ingredients in their pantry. While you may be able to ask these questions directly or have participants try to describe or even take a picture, it's not the same thing as sharing the physical space for yourself. It may also be important for you to observe the physical interaction someone has, such as watching them connect pieces of a medical device together. It's very difficult for people to translate their physical experiences remotely. Physical presence could also be important if you need to share a low fidelity design prop, like a paper prototype or a set of simple wireframes so that you can walk through and show some context. Physically showing items,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/reasons-to-choose-the-traditional-research-approach?u=76281980&t=94)** whether a paper prototype or a test environment, also ensures greater security. You'll know for sure that participants won't have later access to what they saw and you have more control over what they see. So if you're in development stages or working on sensitive material, it can be risky to do remotely. Despite confidentiality or nondisclosure agreements, physically showing and then taking something away is the only way to really control access. The other situation that it may make sense to conduct research in-person is if you need to see how people interact together or observe the dynamics of a group of people. For instance, I once did a set of work on cruise ships. No, not the fun in the sun, laying on the pool deck kind of work, but deep in the depths with the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) of the crew. It turns out that a cruise ship is a place where rank and hierarchy are really important. People don't talk about it much, but it's really clear when you watch people's interactions. It's not something that I could've asked people to report back to me, but I really had to be there to see it for myself and see how people interacted. The key, just like with any research, is to match your approach to the context and the goals of your particular efforts. Remote research can be really great, but sometimes you're going to have to be in-person.

#### [Designing effective remote studies](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/designing-effective-remote-studies?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/designing-effective-remote-studies?u=76281980&t=0)** - Designing an effective remote research study begins just the same way that you design any research effort. You have to define your goals and pick the methods that will best answer your questions and collect the right kind of data. If you need a refresher or you're not yet familiar with this, I cover it in my [UX Research](../../Skills/Web%20Development/UX%20Research.md) Fundamentals class. So check that out. In short, you need to figure out your biggest open questions, turn those into specific research objectives, and then determine the method best suited to collect that sort of information. For instance, if you know you need to learn more about the daily habits and product usage of a target user group, you might choose a diary study or experience sample. You might choose a targeted usability test to assess a new onboarding flow or interaction. I typically recommend that you start by identifying the ideal method to answer your questions, and then look at how you can adjust to go remote. Some methods lend themselves particularly well to remote work. For instance, you can conduct an interview over a video conference, usability test by sharing a screen with a product you want to explore, or have people respond to a diary study trigger by completing a short survey or sending an email. You'll also want to consider whether you need to moderate and be present for the sessions, or if you can consider unmoderated studies. In an unmoderated study, you collect data asynchronously and you won't have direct contact with the participants. So it's best when you have clear specific questions to ask, because you won't have the chance to probe
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/designing-effective-remote-studies?u=76281980&t=95)** or follow different threads of conversation. Unmoderated research allows you to collect a large amount of data quickly, so it can be helpful when you need to reach a very large audience in a short amount of time. You'll also want to consider what you'll be missing by conducting sessions remotely or in an unmoderated way, and look for creative ways to fill gaps. For instance, if you're doing unmoderated card sorts, maybe you can run extra sessions with different types of users or conduct both an open and a closed sort, or you might decide to conduct [Usability Testing](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Usability%20Testing.md) on a high fidelity prototype instead of just on wireframes. Because conducting remote research is relatively fast, you can build in the flexibility to run additional rounds of similar or a complimentary methods. Overall, you'll learn to balance being rigorous with being flexible and pragmatic to best get at the information that you need. Keep your core goals in mind, and don't compromise on those, but be prepared to adapt for the context that you're in to get the best possible insights.

Make sure you schedule an extra person or two for every five or so scheduled and get ready to be flexible. I'd also suggest trying to have a little extra grace if someone misses a session, runs late or needs to reschedule at the last minute. Recruiting can be tough anyway, but especially with remote sessions, I found that you'll get the best insights from participants who feel comfortable and very valued.

> [!transcript]- Transcript

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/running-effective-remote-research-sessions?u=76281980&t=0)** - Running effective remote research sessions has the same basic rules as running any [UX Research](../../Skills/Web%20Development/UX%20Research.md) session. On top of the normal preparation, designing your study, crafting your test plan, pilot testing the plan attack, there are a few extra things to keep in mind of you're running remote sessions. If your sessions are unmoderated, good news, all the hard work in the preparation phase should set you up nicely. You may need to do things like send reminders or check on progress, but otherwise, your goal will be to just wait until you've collected all the data and focus on analysis. If you've chosen a moderated method, the same best practices for running sessions apply as ever. you need to set the tone appropriately, ask questions in a clear, easy to answer way, refrain from leading participants, give people plenty of time to respond, and probe as necessary. Remember, the start of a remote moderated session is a little different than a typical study. It might feel a little extra stressful for you or your participant. Always have backup plans to be connected and give yourselves a few extra minutes to get settled so that you can focus. Even a momentary pause to [compose](../../Glossary/Framework/Jetpack%20Compose.md) yourself will help set a relaxed tone. Tone is especially important remotely, where you don't have as much time to build rapport. Remain friendly, but neutral and focused. It helps to feel out the participant a bit and tailor your approach to them. I tend to ask neutral but relevant questions
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/running-effective-remote-research-sessions?u=76281980&t=95)** when they first join, like asking about their equipment setup, or if they've used a tool before. That sort of interaction gives me both a bit more insight about how savvy they are and to gauge their chattiness level. Some people just won't feel comfortable conversing until they've commented on the weather and others want to just jump right in. I recommend tuning into that and adjusting your approach to the participant. It's okay to be a little chattier or a little more straightforward, within reason. On top of that, I encourage you to pay extra attention to your participant's social cues and facial expressions throughout the remote study. It can be a little harder to read people when you can't see their body language, so err on the side of being even quieter and giving them even more time to speak before you move forward or ask a follow-up question. Because it's so much harder to read people when you're moderating remotely, I really never recommend that you try to take notes during your own sessions. If possible, I like to have my clients, teammates, or other stakeholders be the ones to take notes. That's actually always my recommendation and my preference. It builds deeper understanding and empathy, it's less work for you, and it drives engagement and understanding of the UX process. The thing to be especially careful about when including observers and note-takers is etiquette. Make sure that you give participants a heads up that you'll have note takers so they don't feel like they're under a microscope. If you can, consolidate observer views
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-remote-research/running-effective-remote-research-sessions?u=76281980&t=190)** and take advantage of any automuting or other observer roles your tool has. If not, definitely remind your teammates to remain quiet during the sessions. Automute is your friend. Once you've completed the sessions, you might have a different compilations of notes and raw data than in traditional studies, but you can approach the analysis much the same. For qualitative methods, that means you need to break down individual insights from each observer, then collaborate to identify patterns and key insights and takeaways. There are a lot of tools that will allow individual contributions and collaboration, from things like simple online text documents to more complex digital whiteboards or [Sketching](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Sketching.md) functions. Look for tools that your team is already familiar with and easy to use to encourage participation. Overall, running remote research is just like running in-person research. You have to follow the same best practices to get great results.
