#### [Use an accessibility-first workflow](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/use-an-accessibility-first-workflow?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/use-an-accessibility-first-workflow?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] The native web is a strongly accessible experience by default. At its core, the web is for delivering content as efficiently, widely and accessibly as possible. A website with only raw [HTML](../../Skills/Web%20Development/HTML.md) and no custom styles or functionality may not be the most visually exciting, but it'll load quickly, provide well-structured legible [Typography](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Typography.md), respect user preferences, and handle layout responsively across different screen sizes. Additionally, interactive elements will clearly communicate their state, including focus styles when using the keyboard to interact with content. As designers of digital experiences, it's our job and responsibility to preserve this accessibility as we evolve a product from an initial idea to the final [user experience](../../Skills/Web%20Development/User%20Experience%20(UX).md). Here's an overview of the accessibility-first workflow. First, plan for accessibility as your team develops the content for your website or app. Written content should be easy to understand and have an intuitive structure like a helpful book outline. If your product has images, video or audio, it's important to create equivalent content such as alternative text, transcriptions or captions, so all users can benefit from this information. Next, it's time to jump directly into code to get the content into the final medium as soon as possible. While most teams wait for a design to be final before beginning development, this is a major missed opportunity for creating accessible user experiences. By getting content into its final medium early in the process,
>
> **[1:31](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/use-an-accessibility-first-workflow?u=76281980&t=91)** we can ensure it has the proper structure, semantics and interactions without the risk of having to retrofit them later, or worse, forget them entirely. Once the content is live, we can now begin visual design. This is something I call just-in-time design because we wait to make aesthetic decisions until we have a solid accessible foundation to build upon. By waiting until this stage, our designs will be more resilient, accessible and quicker to implement. From here, visual design and remaining development can happen in parallel to introduce custom styling, functionality and final polish. This leverages the shared knowledge of all disciplines. Teams can surface the opportunities and requirements of design and development together to create a more robust user experience. Throughout this process, all disciplines perform various types of accessibility testing to ensure everything is in an always-working state. From [Content Creation](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Content%20Creation.md) to initial design and development through to the launched product, the team can identify and correct accessibility issues as soon as they arise, saving considerable time and effort. At a high level, an accessibility-first workflow is collaborative and iterative. It involves all disciplines working together and considering accessibility from the start to create the best user experience possible.

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/cross-discipline-collaboration-and-t-shaped-knowledge?u=76281980&t=1)** - Accessibility is determined by all stages and disciplines in [Product Development](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Product%20Development.md). An accessibility-first design workflow makes the most of the team's collective expertise to implement accessibility considerations throughout the process. In order to make the most of this collective expertise, it's important to have a combination of deep knowledge in your discipline and bridging context into the related areas of product work. This is known as T-shaped knowledge, and teams that have T-shaped knowledge are able to collaborate more effectively than team members with siloed experience. Cross-discipline collaboration throughout the process is the best way to build T-shaped knowledge in a team. This is because each member gains experience with the challenges and opportunities of each discipline. For example, when creating written content, it's important to be familiar with [Front-End Development](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Front-End%20Development.md) techniques like [Semantic Markup](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Semantic%20Markup.md) and heading levels. This allows you to properly structure meaningful content from the start. When designing the look of a button, it's important to know the native interactive states buttons provide and design visually-distinct versions for each state. These interactive states can then be conveniently styled in code. An effective way to foster a learning culture around accessibility is to involve everyone in accessibility testing throughout the whole product development process. As we'll explore, accessibility testing can and should be performed by all roles, technical and non-technical alike, and it's a great way to build shared experience and empathy.

#### [Work in parallel to reduce handoff](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/work-in-parallel-to-reduce-handoff?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/work-in-parallel-to-reduce-handoff?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Even in [agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md) teams, it's still common for design, [Content Creation](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Content%20Creation.md), development, and testing your QA to all happen in distinct waterfall phases, with formal handoff between disciplines at the end of each sprint, cycle, or iteration. Handoff introduces risk, especially for accessibility, with potential for lost context, miscommunication, and fewer opportunities for shared knowledge. Accessibility has many considerations and requirements that need to be predicted and preserved throughout [Product Development](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Product%20Development.md). When teams have distinct handoff, it can be difficult to clearly communicate all of these considerations, and any issues that arise later in the process often required decisions from earlier in the process to be reworked. This is inefficient, and it can lead to more accessibility failures, making it into the final product. Handoff requires each role to perfectly predict the needs, opportunities, and limitations of the next phase of product development. A content designer who is an expert in information design would also have to be an expert in visual design and coding to anticipate all of the considerations for creating accessible content. Likewise, a developer would need annotated visual design handoff that inventories every component state, semantics, keyboard navigation patterns, hidden text, and more. It's rare that every role has equal expertise in all other areas of product development, and handoff only further encourages siloed work, which makes shared knowledge less likely.
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/work-in-parallel-to-reduce-handoff?u=76281980&t=93)** Additionally, creating sufficiently detailed handoff to prevent any miscommunication is super tedious, time-consuming, and error prone. To reduce this risk and accessibility, first design workflow encourages teams to work in parallel across disciplines, eliminating the need for handoff. All other roles are involved throughout the process, offering their unique experience and expertise at each stage, and building bridging knowledge across team members. Working in parallel keeps the whole team engaged throughout the process, reduces thrashing from bursts of waiting and rushing, and makes for a more resilient, accessible, and successful product with less overall time and effort. To apply what you've learned in this chapter, map out your team's current product development process. With this map, note any distinct handoff steps between rules or phases. List any experiences you recall where questions or issues later in the process created rework earlier in the process. Lastly, map how your team could work in parallel to reduce handoff.

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/avoid-hidden-content?u=76281980&t=0)** - Accessibility can be daunting as a big topic with many considerations. In terms of users, it's our responsibility to consider and include a global population of users who have varying abilities, preferences, and circumstances. On the technology side, we must consider mini-devices, software, input modes, user interface patterns, and more. Ultimately, building an accessible experience relies on being familiar with many scenarios and approaches. As we introduce custom interactions, patterns, and functionality, the accessibility considerations and risk of excluding users increase considerably. The most effective way to manage all of this is to question complexity in our [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md) across content design, visual design, interaction, and functionality. According to the Annual WebAIM Million Study, which audits the top 1 million websites for accessibility errors, 96.5% of all errors detected fall into just six categories. Low contrast text, missing alternative texts for images, empty labels, missing form input labels, empty buttons, and missing document language. As the study says, addressing just these few types of issues would significantly improve accessibility across the web. As we'll explore these more common errors are easy to identify and prevent. So getting the fundamentals right and questioning complexity go a long way. Hidden content is actually at the core of nearly every complex interaction and feature. Think modal dialogues, accordions, tabs, disclosures,
>

All of these custom components solely exist to hide and reveal content dynamically. And introducing just one of these patterns significantly increases the complexity of the content, visual design, and code necessary to provide a well-supported, accessible experience. The most important way to question complexity is to avoid hidden content. First, ask if the hidden content itself is necessary. If this information is hidden, isn't important enough to keep? If it is truly important, can it always be shown? If it's not very relevant to the current page, does it deserve its own page? If the content still needs to be dynamically hidden and shown, it must follow established guidelines to ensure a resilient and accessible [user experience](../../Skills/Web%20Development/User%20Experience%20(UX).md). This requires special consideration from all roles to be successful, and the complexity of the product suddenly goes up. For example, the team will need to design visuals and content for all interactive states. The underlying information and code will need to properly support assistive technology and account for various devices and input modes. And most importantly, the team will need to thoroughly test all custom functionality, ideally with an inclusive group of real users who have varying accessibility needs. If hidden content or unique functionality is truly necessary for the product, be ready to follow the established patterns we explore

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/respect-established-patterns?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] When it comes to user interfaces it's best to follow established patterns to avoid confusing or frustrating users. #### [Semantics and document structure](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/semantics-and-document-structure?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/semantics-and-document-structure?u=76281980&t=1)** - The internet was originally created by academics to make their research globally accessible. As the web has evolved in the decades since, content, especially written content, has remained foundational to creating an accessible internet. Content design is where accessibility truly starts. The practice of accessibility-first design prioritizes creating accessible content as the foundation for all successful product work. In fact, it's best to implement content into the final medium, such as the web browser, as soon as possible to test and improve it for proper accessibility. This provides a useful and accessible baseline to preserve throughout the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) of the [Product Development](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Product%20Development.md) process. Even without any visual styling or custom functionality, a website with well-structured, semantic, accessible content can provide value to users. It's our job to preserve this accessibility as we layer in remaining design and development. With written content, proper semantics and document structure are essential. Semantics is the meaning of language. In websites, semantics refers to the contextual meaning conveyed by the underlying code used to structure information. [HTML](../../Skills/Web%20Development/HTML.md), also referred to as markup, is the language we use to structure information on the web. It offers a selection of semantic elements that are used to provide special meaning to the text and other [Forms](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Forms.md) of media they contain. Examples of semantic elements include headings, paragraphs, block quotes, emphasis elements, lists, form field labels,
>

But automated testing offers valuable reinforcement and convenience with routine feedback throughout the [Product Development](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Product%20Development.md) process. Automated accessibility testing detects issues such as color contrast, heading levels, document structure and semantics, accessible naming and property use of links and buttons. These tools come in a few different [Forms](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Forms.md). First, there are web browser developer tools. In Chromium-based browsers such as Chrome and [Microsoft](../../Instructors/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Microsoft.md) Edge, the developer tools provide Lighthouse which can run checks for accessibility, performance, best practices, SEO, and more. This is a quick way to evaluate work at any stage of development. The accessibility checks report any successes and failures with helpful descriptions and links to further documentation on how to correct any issues. In Firefox, the developer Tools accessibility panel provides a detailed structure that demonstrates how the website or web app is understood by assistive technology. This is helpful for understanding the overall structure of content and markup, as well as confirming the accessible names of various interactive elements. Next are web browser extensions. Popular options include Microsoft Accessibility Insights for Web, WebAIM's WAVE tool, and the axe Auditor. These tools provide accessibility reports and can provide even more detailed reports
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/automated-accessibility-testing?u=76281980&t=94)** and overlays with color contrast, heading structure, document outlines, accessible naming and other categories. There are also tools that are offered as code linting or [continuous integration](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Continuous%20Integration%20(CI).md) services. As developers write code, accessible code linters can surface warnings or errors with code that cause accessibility issues. Continuous integration services can check for required accessibility techniques every time code is updated and can even prevent deploying code to users if accessibility checks don't pass. All of these tools can be used in combination, run manually or on an automated basis and should be used throughout the design and development of a product. With an accessibility first design approach, content can be coded in the browser as early as possible allowing automated accessibility testing tools to provide immediate feedback. These tools can then provide feedback as new elements of visual design and functionality are layered in. The main downsides of automated accessibility testing are that it can only catch detectable issues which are about 30% of common accessibility issues and they can generate large reports of issues which can be overwhelming and result in issues being neglected. But if you understand their limitations, automated accessibility tools have a useful role in supporting other forms of testing with quick reports that confirm your findings and help maintain quality over time. > **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/accessible-design-handoff?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Presenter} Accessibility first design is most successful when all disciplines collaborate in content design, visual design, development, and accessibility testing. However, not all teams can work this way meaning formal design handoff may still be necessary. If design handoff is truly needed, all accessibility consideration should be well documented to set up all remaining work. [Accessible Design](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Accessible%20Design.md) handoff relies heavily on shared understanding, great documentation, and annotations. With handoff, each discipline has to anticipate the needs of other disciplines, and have bridging knowledge into the opportunities and challenges of each phase of work. This is especially true when it comes to accessibility. For example, with design handoff, visual designers will need to have full understanding of accessible interaction patterns to properly design all the necessary states. They'll also have to coordinate with content designers to give proper accessible names to any controls, and account for proper keyboard navigation support. With a distinct handoff phase, all of this has to be considered upfront before the design is built in code. Design handoff should offer documentation for the full color palette. Complete with approved foreground and background color combinations, and their approved color contrast ratios. There should be documentation for all [Typography](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Typography.md) with an established type scale that notes each style's font size, font weight, line height, letter spacing, and intended use. Designs for all interactive elements need
>
> **[1:32](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/accessibility-first-design/accessible-design-handoff?u=76281980&t=92)** to include a full inventory of all interactive states, which may include default, hover, active, focus, visited, disabled, invalid, valid, collapsed, expanded, or others depending on the element and the interaction pattern. Annotating designs for accessibility strengthens documentation with finer details and considerations. Accessibility annotations can be layered with design annotations that note visual details such as color, dimensions, border radii, and more. Accessibility annotations often note instances of visually hidden text indicating keyboard tap sequences, supported keyboard interactions, names of landmark regions, and more. This helps ensure that accessibility is properly preserved and implemented when visual designs are built in code. Beyond design file documentation, accessible design handoff may require further communication and coordination. Teams that rely on handoff may benefit from detailed explanations, instructions, and walkthrough recordings. While it's best to eliminate a waterfall handoff process, if it's unavoidable these techniques will empower other disciplines to incorporate accessible visual design into the final product.
