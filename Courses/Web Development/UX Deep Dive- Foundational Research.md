#### [What is foundational research and why does it matter?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/what-is-foundational-research-and-why-does-it-matter?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/what-is-foundational-research-and-why-does-it-matter?u=76281980&t=1)** - Most of us know that [user experience](../../Skills/Web%20Development/User%20Experience%20(UX).md) is made up of a wide range of disciplines, with a major delineation between those who focus more on creation and design and those that focus more on research. Within research, there are even more distinctions, and one of the most important to understand is that between the kind of research that evaluates solutions and the kind of research that helps us understand the users, context, and possibilities. That first type of research is usually referred to as evaluative research, and is often thought of as a UX researcher's bread and butter. Have solution, assess how well it does. This is usually covered by things like usability tests, tree tests, that kind of thing. And no doubt it's incredibly important to be able to assess solutions, whether they're existing [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md), competitor solutions, or something you've just come up with. It's just that you need to know more than just evaluation techniques to be a truly impactful UX researcher. That's where foundational research comes in. We use foundational research to understand contexts and people, generate ideas, to find meaning and definition out of unknown spaces. You might also hear this research be called exploratory, generative, or discovery research. They're all typically used pretty interchangeably, but for the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) of the course, I'll use the term foundational. To remember, you can think of this kind of work as that that helps us set the foundation
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/what-is-foundational-research-and-why-does-it-matter?u=76281980&t=96)** for what we're going to build, for whom, and why. Deeply understanding people and their contexts helps you see what could be better in their lives, or how you could build or provide something of value, which leads you to opportunities. This is the sort of research that leads us to aha moments about new features or services to offer, ways to change your current offerings or pivot to a different model altogether. The shift is to think about people and what they need, rather than just thinking about your product. Ultimately, understanding people's lives will help you craft your product, but you'll be able to paint a much fuller picture. Foundational research helps you understand what you should be building and for whom, and evaluative research helps you understand how well those solutions are doing. Evaluative research is more tactical, typically guiding individual design decisions, whereas foundational research is more strategic, and helps guide overall product or direction decisions. You might use foundational research to learn about the types of users you serve or should serve, understand new services, offerings, or features to provide, help you decide what things not to work on, prioritize currently understood work, or any other kind of adaption your teams might need to make. Of course, there isn't always a cut and dry delineation between evaluative and foundational research. For instance, you might conduct a series
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/what-is-foundational-research-and-why-does-it-matter?u=76281980&t=189)** of moderated usability studies, where users lead you through their individual processes, where you both assess how well the tools they have are working for them and learn about their context and the gaps in their current experience. You don't need to think of a clear distinction between the two categories, and only use specific methods for one type of an effort or another. But there are typically different research objectives and scopes for the two kinds of research. Thinking through your goals and considering these different sorts of efforts will help you frame your research activities and craft the right kind of study to answer your biggest open questions. If you need a refresher on refining your research goal and choosing appropriate methods, or any other research logistics, you can check out my Research Fundamentals course.

>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/foundational-research-cadence?u=76281980&t=1)** - Finding the right cadence for conducting both evaluation and foundational research can be tricky and will really depend on your specific team and circumstances. Foundational research is often thought of as coming first or early on in a process to set the stage. And while I absolutely agree that you ought to conduct foundational research early, I also recommend regularly conducting this kind of research to understand the changing contexts and needs of your users. Doing foundational research regularly unlocks the real power, which is to uncover new opportunities to fill a gap that has recently emerged. I want you to pause and think about your mobile phone. What does it look like? What is it capable of? Are you using the same process to connect to people that you were five years ago? One year ago? I doubt it. Changing contexts mean that there are new challenges and new opportunities, new ways that you need to serve people. I also think that relegating foundational research to only the beginning of a project or process is part of what gives research a bad reputation for being time-consuming and out of touch with technical or business feasibility. In traditional waterfall-type projects, there's a big chunk of time spent upfront to discover what needs to happen, and there's an incredible amount of plan detail the team is responsible for fleshing out, which means that they often push to have more research time. But no matter how much time you have upfront,
>
> **[1:38](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/foundational-research-cadence?u=76281980&t=98)** there's no possible way that a team can anticipate every potential question that will come up during the course of implementation. And by the time things are being worked on, context has probably already shifted. Those kinds of situations make all that upfront research time feel wasted. With that in mind, I typically recommend that there be a small amount of foundational work done at the beginning of an effort and also some sort of regular cadence for both evaluation and foundational research. I recommend keeping regularly scheduled research regardless of the type of development environment you work in or the stage of the project or product maturity. That's not to say that you shouldn't be able to add either type of effort as ad hoc needs arise, but setting a cadence holds the teams responsible for conducting the work and gets your partners used to integrating research findings regularly. That particular cadence will depend on your team, but I've seen teams commit to conducting at least one small bit of research every two weeks with great success. That might be too much for your teams, but I'd try to have something on the books at least once a month or so. Foundational work doesn't have to be quite as often, but I still like to set a regular, fairly steady cadence
>

That said, foundational type of work can also be more time-intensive in planning, conducting, and especially analyzing and synthesizing than evaluative work. You might have a broader scope. You'll probably have to include more sessions. They're more likely to be moderated, and you'll have to work harder to make sense of the data that you collect. So don't try to jam a foundational effort into the same amount of time as, say, a rapid usability test or something like that. There is no exact formula for how long you should give yourself to run research or exactly how often to do so. The right flow is greatly going to depend
>
> **[4:46](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/foundational-research-cadence?u=76281980&t=286)** on your particular team's needs and resources, but it's important to balance tactical research needs while looking at the bigger picture and making sure that you don't over-index on either side of the spectrum.

> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/using-longitudinal-studies?u=76281980&t=1)** - While [Interviewing](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Interviewing.md) and going onsite are both wonderful, you may want to learn about your users' behaviors and experiences over time, which means you'll need to run some sort of longitudinal study. There are a few common methods, but basically a longitudinal study is any in which you collect information over a period of time. It could be as short as a few days or up to several months, but I would caution that the longer the study, the harder it is to keep participants engaged, and the more complex the reporting becomes. Longitudinal studies can be especially helpful when you're looking to see what motivates or triggers someone to take action, what habits exist around a specific behavior, the fullest context of use and changes to any of these over time. The most common longitudinal method used for UX work is called a diary study, where users record data entries based on a specific protocol you set up and explain at the beginning of the study. It could be based on a specific time, like they need to reflect at the end of every day, a specific event, like every time they complete an online purchase. Or you could have them respond to prompts that you send throughout the course of the study. You could also do any combination of those. When you ask the same questions to the participants at regular time intervals, we call this an experience sample. You might analyze that data a little differently but the planning and execution are very similar. I often end up designing longitudinal studies with some combination of response triggers.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/using-longitudinal-studies?u=76281980&t=95)** But of course, this depends greatly on the kind of behavior you're looking to study, and how that fits into users' lives. It also depends whether you're looking to learn more about the context of a specific behavior or the changes in a behavior over time. Regardless of the prompts, engaging participants and making sure you get participation is critical to the success of longitudinal studies. So carefully think through the best way to get in touch with your respondent base. You also have a choice whether you want to strictly structure the questions, or leave the responses more open-ended. But be sure to set expectations clearly for all participants and build in time to send reminders and answer questions. I usually aim for a hybrid approach where I have some clearly defined questions, and sample answers, but I also leave participants the freedom to leave other feedback. Again, think through your specific goals and be clear about what you want to learn and what information you need from participants, whatever format that takes us. I also typically like to schedule an interview, but at least select participants at the end to discuss their responses and dig into any areas that were interesting or I need more information. This also gives you a chance to get feedback on the process itself, so that you can be sure that you learn from their experiences to make it easier for participation in the future. Although you won't usually be able to get as rich a context because you won't be in field, longitudinal studies can provide a remote way to build deep understanding of processes or behaviors over time.

> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/focus-on-ideation-exercises?u=76281980&t=1)** - You might also want to put some focus on ideation exercises or cocreation workshops within your foundational work. Cocreating and ideating are just fancy ways for saying that you're going to involve both users and stakeholders in your process, and focus on ways to extract new ideas and get immediate feedback on them. You may want to work generational types of exercises into existing interview sessions, or dedicate a whole session to generating ideas and creating together, which often includes multiple users and stakeholders. There are tons of different structures to explore, but the idea is to provide a framework for people to come up with their own ideas, then build off of others and refine things as a group so that the best bits of each idea are incorporated. While there is no one right structure, I typically suggest that people format these sessions or activities to be individual at first so that the ideas generated by the loudest or most senior people in the room don't immediately take precedence. Even if that group is just you and a single participant, it can be intimidating for people to get started. Look for individual divergent-thinking exercises to just start generating without judgment, such as mind mapping with specific prompt words or listing out opposites to assumptions. Match the kind of exercise to the kind of thing you're trying to generate. Then, look for ways to build off of each idea. For example, if you first did a mind mapping exercise,
>
> **[1:37](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/focus-on-ideation-exercises?u=76281980&t=97)** you can then do a round robin type of thing where everyone passes their mind map to the person next to them and chooses the [word](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Microsoft%20Word.md) that resonates most. Continuing passing around until everyone has voted and everyone can see what stood out most. There are heaps of activities and exercises for you to try. So just make sure that you look for one that will be both easy to participate in and draws out ideas. For more information, check out my colleague Chris Nodder's excellent course on ideation.

[↑ Back to Table of Contents](#table-of-contents)

#### [Analysis and integration](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/analysis-and-integration?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/analysis-and-integration?u=76281980&t=1)** - Analyzing and synthesizing foundational research data is of course dependent on the goals of the study and type of method that you used. When you gather quantitative data, analysis is fairly straightforward, but qualitative data is usually a bit more complex. I go over this in depth in my Analyzing Data course, so check that out if you need some guidance on how to tackle analysis and synthesis. However, with foundational research in particular, you need to do more than just draw insights from the work. You need to help put what you've learned in context for your team members and stakeholders, and ultimately drive change based on what you've found. Regardless of the context, the first thing you need to think about is the goals of your stakeholder or audience base. Think about what's important to them, and craft stories that speak to what they care about. For instance, if you're working with a marketing team, it might be most important to highlight branding considerations or impact on content needs. Business sponsors might be more interested in revenue or support costs. Each team is going to have a slightly different focus, because they're responsible for different pieces and they will need to take different actions. It's likely that you'll have different kinds of audiences that all need to connect to your data, so consider making slightly different versions of documents, or at least make presentations easy to scan and dissect to focus only on relevant portions.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/analysis-and-integration?u=76281980&t=96)** To that end, carefully choose the ways that you document and types of artifacts you create to describe your findings. I'm no fan of documentation just for documentation's sake, but visualizations can help you tell stories in a compelling way, and formulating your findings into frameworks, like personas or journey maps, might help frame what you've found. Your goal is to pick artifacts that'll help your audience understand and connect to what you've found, and help them take whatever actions they need to next. I go over different types of UX maps in more detail in my UX Mapping course, and Chris Nodder has a great course on creating personas, so check those out for more specific instruction on those types of artifacts. I've also found it useful to keep track of foundational research findings with a framework that helps people see how research impacts decisions and follows the thread from initial effort to eventual outcome and impact. I particularly like the Atomic Research Framework, which allows you to organize individual research efforts together into consolidated conclusions. The Atomic Framework goes like this. There are experiments, which describe the individual research efforts, where we've collected data. From the experiments, you find indisputable facts, like how many people approach a problem in a certain way. These facts are the pure observations from your experiments, without any meaning or interpretation layered on top. When you synthesize together facts, you can draw insights.
>
> **[3:13](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ux-deep-dive-foundational-research/analysis-and-integration?u=76281980&t=193)** These are combinations of facts with the context and meaning layered on, rather than just the pure observations. You may synthesize insights from many different facts and experiments. Finally, you draw conclusions from the various different insights, and identify what you'll do with that information. Conclusions are the "so what" of research, leading you back to create more hypotheses about next steps or new experiments to run. This sort of framework helps you draw together pieces of data collected across different research efforts into unified insights or conclusions, and can help you keep holistic context in mind as you learn new things. It can help you and your team see how each individual effort leads to conclusions, and therefore impacts overall direction. Remember, foundational research helps you understand people and context, and is used to explore problem areas or uncover new opportunities. Linking together what you learn about people into what actionable steps you should do next will help your teams glean the most benefit out of your work and help your [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md) improve.
