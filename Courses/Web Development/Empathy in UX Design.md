#### Example: Personas
> [LinkedIn Learning](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=0)** As a user researcher, I supply the information I gather to the product team.
>
> **[0:05](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=5)** In my dream world, every stakeholder would attend all the research sessions.
>
> **[0:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=9)** We'd have lots of discussion time to talk about what we learned in the research, and each stakeholder would carefully and meticulously read every word I wrote, absorb every highlights video I made, and take everything we learned to heart, supporting all necessary changes to meet user needs if only.
>
> **[0:28](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=28)** In addition to the deliverables we've talked about so far, such as research findings, mockups, and wireframes, another good way to represent users for whom stakeholders can feel empathy is through the use of personas.
>
> **[0:41](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=41)** Personas are based on data from user research and perhaps some other sources too, such as market research or even web analytics.
>
> **[0:49](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=49)** But instead of a diverse collection of data points, personas pull everything together into a composite person.
>
> **[0:56](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=56)** A profile is created for that person as if it's someone real, even though it's ultimately a representation of a whole user group.
>
> **[1:04](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=64)** The intent of the personas represents something tangible for design teams to grab onto and feel for.
>
> **[1:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=69)** The personas' needs are needs that a product should aim to meet, and the personas' frustrations are frustrations that real users may frequently experience with a current product or perhaps competing products.
>
> **[1:21](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=81)** Over the course of my career, I've come across all sorts of personas.
>
> **[1:26](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=86)** First, let's talk about the commonalities.
>
> **[1:29](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=89)** Invariably, personas have a picture attached to them to represent a product user.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=94)** They also have a random name attached and some basic attributes intended to help the team feel for them and their needs.
>
> **[1:41](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=101)** There should be one persona for each category of user of the product.
>
> **[1:46](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=106)** Sometimes I've seen those categories broken out into such little tidbits that a huge number of personas becomes overwhelming.
>
> **[1:54](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=114)** It's hard to feel empathy, much less designed for too many people at once.
>
> **[1:58](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=118)** Making this huge number of personas not as useful as they could be.
>
> **[2:04](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=124)** There is no exact science to how many personas need to be created, but consider the smallest number that legitimately synthesizes and represents the various types of people that would be using a product.
>
> **[2:15](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=135)** Just like everything else we've talked about, personas should tell an emotional story that will carry through and properly synthesize the joys, frustrations, and desires of each type of user.
>
> **[2:27](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=147)** Sometimes I've seen personas that start with a photo and a name, but then, instead of aggregating in an emotive sense, devolve into a collection of tallied attributes.
>
> **[2:37](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=157)** For example, in one case, I remember seeing personas that tallied actual percentages of software usage among each audience group.
>
> **[2:45](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=165)** While it's absolutely fine and often valuable to have backup data that can be referenced when needed, it's hard to feel for a series of tallied numbers.
>
> **[2:54](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=174)** It often makes sense to start with essentially proto-personas based on information from stakeholder assumptions, but these should never be circulated as the actual personas.
>
> **[3:04](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=184)** Rather, they should be framed as temporary placeholders with qualitative and perhaps quantitative research designed to either validate or update what stakeholders think they know about their intended users.
>
> **[3:16](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=196)** As a researcher, when I'm asked to design research that will be used to build personas, I often find it helpful to first develop these proto-personas, then I can more easily develop a recruiting and research strategy to fill in the gaps and validate or modify from this starting point.
>
> **[3:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=213)** This research could involve some combination of user interviews, contextual inquiry, usability testing, and survey research.
>
> **[3:40](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=220)** Sometimes the user research also involves a secondary analysis of the prior research that is recent enough to still be valid.
>
> **[3:49](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=229)** With respect to persona development, I should also mention empathy maps.
>
> **[3:54](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=234)** Empathy maps represent a cluster of attributes for each persona that are grouped into what personas would say, think, do, and feel.
>
> **[4:03](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=243)** The "Says" bucket may include quotes pulled right from a qualitative research study. What real people actually said.
>
> **[4:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=250)** The "Thinks" bucket may include what it turns out those real people might have been thinking along the way, which may come out during a session as they think aloud, or perhaps in a debrief.
>
> **[4:21](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=261)** The "Feels" bucket is the most emotive and represents what people might be feeling, often based on verbal or written cues, and perhaps body language as well.
>
> **[4:31](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=271)** The "Does" bucket captures behavioral data, perhaps in usability testing or maybe from a survey of reported behaviors.
>
> **[4:39](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=279)** Overall, an empathy map is a persona add-on that presents a valuable clustering of information and perhaps can be generated through a group exercise that involves and engages stakeholders.
>
> **[4:52](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=292)** Before we close out our discussion on personas, I do want to mention a pet peeve of mine when I see stakeholder desire for too much empathy with personas.

> **[5:11](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=311)** While this may work for some people, others, and I count myself here, feel like this can come across as uncomfortable and unnecessary.
>
> **[5:19](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=319)** Create and distribute a slide deck of personas and even tape large printouts to the wall if you want.
>
> **[5:24](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=324)** Just remember that generating that necessary empathy means walking a fine line of legitimate authenticity.
>
> **[5:31](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-personas?u=76281980&t=331)** Not too little, not too much, but just right.

>
> **[1:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=69)** A journey map will often highlight the flow of the journey for a particular audience group or persona from left to right.

> **[2:05](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=125)** Sometimes, in addition to the short blurbs, there may be additional callouts and even supplemental information below, detailing a bit more of the story.

> **[2:15](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=135)** Unlike a persona that will often fit on a screen or a single page, a journey map is often densely packed with valuable information which may not easily fit in a limited space.

> **[2:26](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=146)** Therefore, as you create a journey map, I caution that you want to think about how it's going to be viewed.

> **[2:50](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=170)** And of course, every time they zoomed in, the big picture disappeared and the magic of the story was broken, so it was harder to convey the inherent empathy.

> **[3:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=181)** And even printing didn't help as the font size ended up printing at a 7.5 even on large poster-sized paper.

> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/empathy-in-ux-design-25118320/example-customer-journey-maps?u=76281980&t=189)** My takeaway here is that as you produce a journey map, think about what you want to do with it and how it's going to be used to cultivate that necessary and critical stakeholder empathy.
