Schematics can be small and simple circuit diagrams like this one or large, complex designs that can span multiple pages. Regardless of their size, the way that you read them is the same. Electronic schematics use symbols to represent the components in a circuit. And those components are connected to each other with lines that represent electrical connections. It's important to understand that the relative location of components in an electronic schematic does not necessarily represent their physical location within a circuit. #### [Capacitors vs. inductors](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/electronics-foundations-basic-circuits/capacitors-vs-inductors-14155507?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/electronics-foundations-basic-circuits/capacitors-vs-inductors-14155507?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Since capacitors and inductors are both reactive components that store energy and oppose change, there are times when a circuit can be designed to use one or the other to do the same thing. However, capacitors and inductors are not direct replacements for each other because they work in very different ways. For starters, the way that capacitors and inductors store their energy is different. Capacitors store energy in the electric field that exists between two charged plates. Inductors, on the other hand, store energy in the magnetic field surrounding a coil of wire. In regards to how capacitors and inductors [react](../../Skills/Web%20Development/React.js.md) to change, capacitors resist sudden changes in voltage by absorbing and releasing stored charge, attempting to maintain a constant voltage across the capacitor. Inductors resist change in current by expanding and collapsing the magnetic field in the coils. And they attempt to maintain a constant current through the inductor. The way that capacitors and resistors respond to different frequencies is also different. Capacitors have a low impedance against high-frequency signals, allowing them to pass through, but capacitors act like an open circuit to block low-frequency and DC signals. Inductors, on the other hand, act like a short circuit to allow low-frequency and DC signals to easily pass through, but they have a high impedance against high-frequency signals, so those get blocked. This mirror-like response to frequency
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/electronics-foundations-basic-circuits/capacitors-vs-inductors-14155507?u=76281980&t=94)** allows us to build similar low and high-pass filters using either inductors or capacitors. The capacitor acts like a short circuit to high frequencies and drains them out of the input signal, leaving just the low-frequency components at the output of the filter. I can build a similar low-pass filter using an inductor and a resistor in series, as shown here. Notice that they're arranged in a different order. In this circuit, the inductor acts like a wire to the low frequencies, allowing them to pass through, but the inductor will block any high-frequency components in the signals. Both of these circuits are low-pass filters, so the way they respond to different frequencies will be similar. The equations to calculate the cutoff frequency for each one is different, but if I choose my component values right, I can design these two filters to have the exact same frequency response. And because these two filters have the same frequency response, it's a common mistake to think that they're completely interchangeable with each other, but they're not because they're different circuits that work in different ways. >
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/electronics-foundations-basic-circuits/capacitors-vs-inductors-14155507?u=76281980&t=189)** the input impedance of the filter circuit against high frequencies is relatively small, basically just the resistance through the resistor. In this LR circuit, on the other hand, the high frequencies get blocked right away by the inductor at the front of the circuit, which gives this circuit a very large impedance against high frequencies. That means if the signal being filtered consists of mostly high-frequency content, this filter circuit won't draw much current from the signal source or have to dissipate much high-frequency energy, so it'll be relatively efficient. But for DC energy, it will have a much lower input impedance because the inductor lets that signal pass through, so some of that DC energy will get dissipated through the resistor to ground. When designing a circuit to use one of these filters, it's important to consider the purpose of the circuit, so you can choose the right filter for the job. > **[4:42](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/electronics-foundations-basic-circuits/capacitors-vs-inductors-14155507?u=76281980&t=282)** then I would place a decoupling capacitor between the signal and ground, which is basically creating an RC low-pass filter. A capacitor would not work for that application because a capacitor can never create more voltage than was used to charge it. Charging a capacitor with the low-voltage battery would not be able to produce the high-voltage surge required by the spark plugs.
