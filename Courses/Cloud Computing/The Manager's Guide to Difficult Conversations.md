For some reason, Patty has been allowed to come in late over and over again. Let's suppose you're the boss, and it's your director report who's coming in late.

> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/the-manager-s-guide-to-difficult-conversations/discerning-the-real-communication-issue?u=76281980&t=93)** The second step is to initiate a conversation and find out why Patty is coming in late instead of assuming it's due to a poor work ethic. It goes something like this. "Patty, I'd like to meet with you and talk about the schedule. I've observed that last week you came in at 9:30 instead of 8:30. Walk me through what's happening." Third, when Patty talks, listen for four areas, skill, clarity, priority, and resources. If Patty says, "Well, you know, I've really never learned how to tell time," well then the issue is a matter of skill. If Patty says, "I thought we had flexibility. #### [Listen to own the conversation](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/the-manager-s-guide-to-difficult-conversations/listen-to-own-the-conversation-19248783?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/the-manager-s-guide-to-difficult-conversations/listen-to-own-the-conversation-19248783?u=76281980&t=0)** - Listening is probably one of the most difficult skills to practice. It's an ongoing discipline that includes staying present and avoiding the temptation to multitask, interrupt, and argue. Listening is also about noticing nuances in body language or facial expressions and it's about asking relevant questions but in highly charged, difficult conversations, listening is not enough. You need radical listening. Radical listening is the ability to own the conversation by controlling your emotional urges when you don't like what you're hearing, and most of us need a lot of practice. Just go to a social media site and look at a couple of political threads. You won't see much radical listening. You'll see personal attacks, name calling, labeling, and a strong desire to convince the other person how wrong they are. None of these tactics create conversations, promote understanding, or convince others. Now, think about your own urges when strong emotions are stirred up. Do you roll your eyes, raise your voice? Do you get irritated at their misinformation? Do you use facts to prove your point? Do you try to win at all costs? Do you avoid? Not if you're a radical listener. That's because the one who listens first owns the conversation and doesn't respond based on emotion.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/the-manager-s-guide-to-difficult-conversations/listen-to-own-the-conversation-19248783?u=76281980&t=96)** When you listen first and the other person feels truly heard, they're often willing to listen to you in return and that's often what we're after in a difficult conversation. Here are some guidelines for radical listening. When something triggers you, build some space between stimulus and response. Take a deep breath. Welcome the silence. Next, simply acknowledge their reality. You don't have to agree. Just say something like, "I see," or, "I hear you," or "That's interesting." Then build another space. Don't speak yet. Give them more room to expand. Say something like, "I didn't know you felt that way." Or simply use curiosity without judgment by saying, "Say more about that." Stay silent, but attentive, being mindful of your expressions until they start to wind down. Here's the point. Until someone feels heard, they won't consider another point of view. Instead, they'll feel threatened. They'll argue, withdraw, and they'll make you wrong. But notice the shift in them once they feel heard. Their demeanor will soften. They'll seem more open to consider other viewpoints. Either they will invite you to share your point of view or they won't, but you control when to share. For example, if the conversation still seems heated,
>
> **[3:12](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/the-manager-s-guide-to-difficult-conversations/listen-to-own-the-conversation-19248783?u=76281980&t=192)** you can say, "I'd like to think about what you said and later I'd like to share my own thoughts." Or you can decide to strategically present your point of view. Remember this, when you talk, you know what you know, but when you listen, you know what you know and you know what they know. Radical listening builds bridges and helps you control the direction of difficult conversations.

Accountability and responsibility go together in creating a culture of accountability, but it's easier when you understand their individual components. Accountability is about measurement and numbers. I think of accountability as being logical and not emotional. It's about the head, while responsibility is about ownership, more emotional, and therefore, it's more about the heart. To create a culture of accountability, the head and the heart must work together. When employees really enjoy their work and they want to achieve, they crave the kind of accountability I'm talking about. They want feedback from the boss. They want to be noticed when they do a good job. They want new tips and skills when they miss the mark. Accountability is a manager's best friend when used properly.
