Engineers tend to focus on shipping features and building [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md). Engineers too often embrace the CTO part of their job but fail to make time for the CEO part of their job, unfortunately. The reality is, just like you, everyone else is busy. This includes your manager, your manager's manager, your [human resources](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Human%20Resources%20(HR).md) business partner, your colleagues, your internal and external customers, just about everyone. What this means is that no one has the time to make your case for you. Too many engineers think that if they just do their job and ship products, the results will automatically follow. And then if results don't automatically follow, engineers get discouraged and they lose faith in the process. This then leads to low morale and attrition. That is a lose-lose proposition both for the engineer and the company.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/how-promotion-cycles-work?u=76281980&t=95)** The promo process has four key components. First, frequency. Second, competencies. Third, stakeholders. And fourth, key decision-makers. Understanding these components will help you as an engineer to create the necessary artifacts, build the relationships, and get the timing right so you can make the best possible case for yourself and your career. Well, let's look at these one by one. First, frequency. In some companies, whether an engineer gets promoted or not solely depends upon the manager. The manager decides when the time is right based on the direct knowledge of the work done by the engineer and also input from stakeholders. That is not, however, the case in every company. As companies get larger, they tend to have annual promotion cycles with the possibility of an extra mid-year cycle as well. So you might have two cycles every year that are roughly six months apart. During those six months, you have three responsibilities as you explore your case for promotion, besides, of course, doing your job. First, assess the areas that you need to grow in. That is your competencies. Ensure that you are setting, meeting, and exceeding your stakeholders' expectations. Continually build a resume to make your case for promotion just as you would to get a new job. Remember, asking for a promotion is like asking for a new job in a sense, which brings us to the second component, competencies. When you are asking for a promotion, you are stating that you exceed the qualifications of your current role,
>
> **[3:07](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/how-promotion-cycles-work?u=76281980&t=187)** you have been performing at the next level, and, this is critical, you have been operating at that next level for a sustained period in multiple contexts. Especially for tech jobs, companies set up measurable attributes, behaviors, and achievements that engineers can be measured against. These objective metrics are known as competencies. You will want to build your case by mapping your achievements against these competencies. The third component is stakeholders. These include your manager, internal and external customers, as well as your colleagues. These would be folks who can add color and context to your work. You will need to manage these relationships, check in with them on an ongoing basis, and make sure that they will back your case for promotion. The fourth component of the promotion process is the decision-makers. These may include your manager, others in the company who are at or above the level that you seek to be promoted to, also folks from other departments who will ensure that the project is objective, and possibly somebody from human resources to follow legal guidelines. Now that you understand how the promotion process work, let's start preparing for it.

#### [Promotion ladders, competencies, and levels](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-ladders-competencies-and-levels?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-ladders-competencies-and-levels?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Presenter] What are ladders, levels, and competencies, and how do they affect the promotion process? It is critical for companies to set up a promotion process that is objective, fair, quantifiable, and scalable. These four attributes are critical because they help ensure fairness and efficiency. Every engineer has a different set of skills. Each product is different. The priority on deliverables tends to vary from time to time. How much visibility a specific project gets depends upon its stakeholders, business value, and urgency. It is therefore important for companies to set up a level playing field and a common set of standards. This is why, as companies grow, they use concepts like ladders, levels, and competencies. Let's see what they mean now. Ladders correspond with the different types of jobs that exist within a company. For example, a tech company could have a software engineering ladder, a data scientist ladder, a [Security Engineering](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Security%20Engineering.md) ladder, and many others. Now, these are just examples, but the fact is these are all different kinds of jobs as well. The skills it takes to perform a software engineering job are very different from the skills it takes to perform, for example, a data scientist job. When it comes time to assess people's performance and promote them, it makes no sense to compare a data scientist to a software engineer. This is where the ladders are helpful. Creating a ladder, for example, is like creating a job description or a resume. It helps clarify what somebody must do to count as having succeeded or met expectations. For example, people on the software engineering ladder
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-ladders-competencies-and-levels?u=76281980&t=93)** write code, design architectures, create [Microservices](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microservices.md), et cetera. People on the [Data Science](../../Topics/Data%20Science.md) ladder, for example, run queries against data stores, analyze the data, and make projections for the business. Now that we have the differences between two ladders clearly understood, let's look at how these ladders operate individually. This is especially critical when it comes to the promotion process. In order to get promoted on a ladder, you need to make the argument that you are performing above the level that you are currently at, which brings us to the next concept, which is levels. Each level comes with specific expectations. In the case of software engineers, to go from level X to level X plus one, you may need to demonstrate increased throughput in terms of services shipped, increased scale in terms of number of internal or external customers assisted, improved code quality, part leadership where you're seen as a leader within the company, each of these criteria when measured numerically or objectively could help prove whether or not a given engineer is ready to perform at the next level and therefore should officially be promoted to the next level. But what distinguishes one level from the level above it or the level beneath it? This is where competencies come into play. At each level, as I mentioned before, you need attributes that can either be measured numerically or substantiated by way of feedback. These attributes define performance at that level. Depending about how well you fulfill these attributes, you might get a rating like successful, exceeds expectations, or needs improvement.
>
> **[3:07](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-ladders-competencies-and-levels?u=76281980&t=187)** If you exceed expectations in those competencies for a sustained period of time on your ladder, you might be ready for promotion to the next rung on the ladder. So as a promotion candidate, you need to make the case that you have a clear understanding of, first, the ladder you're on, second, the level you are on within that ladder, and third, the competencies that correspond to your level and the level you seek to be promoted to. It is vital that you understand this so that you can set expectations with your manager, cultivate skills, identify projects, and track your performance on an ongoing basis. Too many engineers make the mistake of looking at all of this right when the promo cycle begins. To be successful, this preparation must be ongoing throughout the year so that you and your manager can make a successful case for your promotion.

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-committees?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Another key component of the promotion process are the committees. As I've said before, companies need to make sure that the promotion process is equitable and consistent. As a result, rather than allowing just a manager to make a decision around the promotion process, companies are increasingly using a committee process. So for a software engineer promotion process, what does a typical committee look like? In my mind, a committee must have four key attributes. First, it must include technologists who understand the specific engineer's ladder, level, and competencies. That is, other software engineers. It must also include people who have gotten the promotion that the specific engineer is seeking. That is, engineers at least one level above the engineer under consideration for promotion. Third, in order to ensure that the candidate, that is the engineer up for promotion, has their point of view represented on the committee, the committee should have folks with at least some context about the candidate's work, or at least the work done by the candidate's team. Finally, in order to make sure that the promotion process is objective, the committee must include engineers from completely different teams so that the work done by the promotion candidate can be compared to the work done by other candidates as well. Given that engineers are often disconnected from the administrative processes of the company, they may wonder, "Why do companies go through this extremely complicated process?" Also, the fact of the matter is, the people on the committee are also the same people who have existing busy day jobs at the same company. As such, building this committee
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-committees?u=76281980&t=94)** is an extremely time-consuming process, especially in larger companies, you may need multiple committees for the same ladder. Given the number of ladders at most companies, that further increases the number of committees as well. So here's why more and more companies still stick with the committee process. A committee with this diverse composition has a better chance of being objective and comprehensive. This would reduce the chance that favoritism and recency bias could sway the decision one way or the other. This is important for engineers to understand because your promo packets, your code examples, your [Microservices](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microservices.md), your other tools and other artifacts, all of which you put together will be heavily and seriously scrutinized. Your company invests a lot in the promotion process, it is vital therefore, that you take this process seriously. Think of the promotion process as your code in production, failures are hard and take time to undo. You may get a second chance to deploy code in production, and you may get a second chance to go for the promotion process as well, but just as either your code works or it doesn't, either the committee says yes to the promotion or it doesn't. Here are some other factors to consider when it comes to committees. First, they usually include five to six people and are headed by one person known as a committee chairperson. Second, the committee members usually review the promotion packet before they meet. Every member is expected to have an opinion on whether or not the engineer should get promoted. The committee then meets, deliberates and debates
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/promotion-committees?u=76281980&t=188)** with the intention of ending up with an up or down vote. If the committee wants to be especially thorough and fair, they may request additional evidence from either the candidate or the manager. It is therefore helpful to keep evidence at the ready in case there are follow-up questions. Again, too many engineers mistakenly assume that their work speaks for itself, but remember, the committee will include people who have no understanding of the candidate's work, but may have done similar work. The more evidence you can provide them and the more context you can provide them, the higher the chances are of you getting a favorable verdict. Committee members are extremely busy. I have served on committees where you sometimes had more than a dozen candidates to review in a given day. Your promotion packets need to be on point, free of grammatical or factual errors and in line with the format the committee has prescribed. Finally, engineers must need to work with their managers to make sure the right committee reviews their packet. During a busy promotion cycle, things often fall through the cracks, so being extra careful, never hurts.

#### [What the committee is looking for by way of impact](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/what-the-committee-is-looking-for-by-way-of-impact?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/what-the-committee-is-looking-for-by-way-of-impact?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] What does the promotion committee mean when it comes to judging your impact as a criteria for promotion? What are they exactly looking for? Every role has a key metric that determines success. It is not the only metric, but without this specific metric, it is hard to make the case for promotion. I went to undergrad school in the state of Missouri where the state motto is show me, since according to unofficial legend, Missourians tend to be skeptical and need evidence before they believe you. Software engineering promotion committees tend to work the same way. For software engineers, and especially for engineers who are up for promotion, the ask is from the committee to answer one simple question, what have you delivered lately? To be more descriptive, the promotion committee is looking for evidence that a software engineer has actually built and deployed a product. This is critical since this evidence answers several key questions. First, it proves that the engineer can take an idea from zero to one, that is, the engineer can take something that was a design or an idea and converted that to a fully functional product. Second, it provides a detailed code repository that the committee can then look at for [Scalability](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Scalability.md), code quality, complexity, et cetera. These are criteria that are objective and provide a clear signal for an engineer's readiness to operate at the next level. It could also showcase examples of collaboration. Engineering is no longer about just writing good code. It requires imagination, persuasion, attention to detail, response to constructive feedback.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/tech-career-skills-getting-promoted/what-the-committee-is-looking-for-by-way-of-impact?u=76281980&t=94)** Engineers need to be able to convince others with their point of view, listen to other points of view, make constructive and decisive judgments about the way forward and make sure that the eventual outcome captures the best of all available options. Persistent and continuous examples of completed [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md) allows for examples of collaboration to shine through. It helps demonstrate an engineer's growth. Any complex and high-impact [Software Deployment](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Software%20Deployment.md) tends to have peaks and valleys, advances and retreats. An engineer then has a chance to show the mistakes they made, the feedback they received, the course corrections they delivered, and the impact upon the end result because of all of those cumulative learnings over time. It is very difficult to get all these signals from an engineer unless there are sustained examples of delivering products and functionality.
