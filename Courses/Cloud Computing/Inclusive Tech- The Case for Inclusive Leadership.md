In the same way that you include diversity and inclusion goals alongside your core business goals, you're going to want to build that time to reach these goals into your roadmapping. Setting goals without time budgeted to work towards them isn't going to result in success. Goals without the means to reach them are just good intentions that you've written down. Make sure that your inclusion goals are budgeted for in planning, in roadmapping, and in your delivery estimates. If you're working in an [[Agile Development|agile]] environment, make sure to include work towards these goals in individual sprints. While you may already have clear ideas of how long these different activities will take, be sure to include your team in the planning process. This will help make sure that the projections for your goals are more realistic. Not just for inclusion goals, but for all of the work you do. It also helps your team build knowledge of inclusion planning into their own skillsets. As you work to plan and make time for your team to meet these goals, make sure that you're also allowing enough time for the documentation of this work. Many activities around diversity are human-focused
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/inclusive-tech-the-case-for-inclusive-leadership/balancing-time?u=76281980&t=94)** and can be more difficult to report out than bugs fixed or features added. Many teams and leaders fail to record the inclusion work that they've done, meaning that that time and the resources needed to complete future work is difficult to advocate for. Better documenting this work also allows you to better reward excellent work done in the inclusion space. We can broadly look at work activities as either promotable or non-promotable. Promotable activities are the things that buy your employees work rewards they seek. This could be a raise or a promotion, or merely professional recognition. Non-promotable work are activities that don't deliver these rewards in a specific workplace. One of the challenges of inclusion work is that activities meeting inclusion goals are less often seen as promotable than purely technical tasks. As a leader, it's your responsibility to make sure that inclusion work is being properly managed and that appropriate rewards for this work are being allocated. You need to do more than make sure your whole team has time to do the inclusion work. As a leader, you also need to make sure that this work is equitably distributed throughout your team and equitably rewarded as it's completed.
