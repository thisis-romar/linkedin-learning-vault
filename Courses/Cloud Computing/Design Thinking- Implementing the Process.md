#### [Identify the pain points: Watch real users](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/identify-the-pain-points-watch-real-users?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/identify-the-pain-points-watch-real-users?u=76281980&t=1)** - You might have an idea for a product you want to build, but how do you know if it's the right idea? Maybe you identified an issue based on your own experiences. Maybe you've been tracking customer feedback on an earlier release of your product, or maybe you have a project roadmap and it's time to implement the next phase of the project. Before you go ahead and start building, it's essential that you go and watch some people doing the activities you want to make a solution for. Why? Because it allows you to see the true issues and what people do to get around those issues today. When you read or listen to product feedback, people give you their solutions, not their problems. Because they're caught up inside the process, their solution might not be the best one for the real underlying issue. It's the same if you want to build a product based on an issue you experience. Remember though, that your assumptions are based on a sample of one person, you. Before you go any further, take the time to work out whether other people have the same issue, and what it is that they do to work around it today. In either case, whether you're starting from product feedback or from your own idea, you'll get better insight into the true problem by watching other people working through the tasks that you think need to be improved. What if there isn't existing software out there for the task you want to observe? Well, there's probably a manual way that people perform the task today. In fact, if you can't find that manual task, I'd question how valuable your solution is going to be.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/identify-the-pain-points-watch-real-users?u=76281980&t=96)** People's tasks don't change very much over time. Just the way they perform them changes. If there isn't existing software, find the manual tasks that people are performing and watch that instead. It's essential that you go to the place where the activity happens. Just bringing people to your [[Microsoft Office|office]] to ask them questions doesn't give you the background information or the understanding of the little steps that they might forget to tell you about. Those nuances in the interaction could be the pieces that actually make the biggest difference in the whole process. It's also really important to go in with an open mind. Rather than asking people how wonderful the solution you're already thinking about is, take the time to listen to and understand their true problems. You'll have to tease out those problems from some of the solutions they offer up to you. But if you do your job well and get to the true root cause of the issues they face, you'll be in a position to generate a truly useful solution that people want to use. For our example project, the project leader is a mountain biker. He identified the problem of making donations to the crews that perform voluntary maintenance on mountain bike trails. His initial idea was to build an app that let trail users buy the trail maintenance crews a virtual drink, as a way of saying thanks. In order to check that this assumption is true, we're going go to where these trail maintainers currently do their work and their fundraising. We'll watch them in action, watch their interaction with trail users, talk to some of these trail users too,
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/identify-the-pain-points-watch-real-users?u=76281980&t=189)** and use those observations to extract the pain points that exist in the current way of raising funds.

#### [Extracting the pain points](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/extracting-the-pain-points?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/extracting-the-pain-points?u=76281980&t=1)** - I already mentioned that your experience map shows you what the experience looks like today and where the problems or pain points are. The next step is to highlight those pain points and work out which are the most important ones to fix. Then you can prioritize the list of issues and turn those issues into goals for the project. I suggest that as a team, you dot vote directly on the experience map to draw out the most important pain points. Just give every member the same number of sticky dots, maybe four or five depending on the size of your experience map, then everyone on the team gets to stick their dots on the areas they think are the biggest issues. The only rule here is that you can only stick dots on the observations, not the themes or process steps. In our map, that means just the yellow stickies, not the green or blue ones. Once everyone has stuck their dots up, tally them, pull out a list of the biggest pain points, starting with the ones that got the most dots. You may find that multiple issues can be grouped into the same pain point. This is a powerful exercise because it's data driven. You observed these issues. Now you as a team have decided which ones were causing your customers the most problems. Rather than basing your future design on one team members' opinions, or worse still,

#### [Pain points to goals](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/pain-points-to-goals?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/pain-points-to-goals?u=76281980&t=1)** - It's great to have the list of pain points, but now it's time to work out what it looks like to fix them. Take each pain point in turn and invert it. This gives you a list of goals to work towards. For our example project, the top two pain points were that riders don't know who to report trail issues to, and they don't know how to report those issues. The team decided that solving these two pain points involves goals of not only knowing who to report to but when, as well as being able to easily and successfully report those issues. You can see the direct relationship between the pain points and the goals. This way, we're still staying true to the user data we gathered. Interestingly, the goals are different from the initial idea that the team leader came up with. He started with the idea of a drink token app as a way of saying thanks to the trail workers. From our list of pain points and goals, it looks like we're actually better off looking at a solution which helps trail users report issues, and in the process, makes them aware of the trail work so they either donate money or volunteer time. All too often teams deliver a solution but then have no way of measuring how well the solution actually works. Even at this early stage, it's worth taking the time to give each of your goals some metrics. We can measure customers' efficiency, effectiveness, and satisfaction. Efficiency is a measure of how long a task takes. Effectiveness is how well it removes the potential
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/design-thinking-implementing-the-process/pain-points-to-goals?u=76281980&t=96)** to make errors. Satisfaction is, obviously, how much the people using the solution you develop like it. You can give a measure of efficiency, effectiveness, or satisfaction to each of the goals you've come up with. In this instance, the project team came up with efficiency metrics around how many issues are reported, effectiveness metrics around reduction in number of abandoned reports, and satisfaction metrics around both the app and the trail conditions. You'll notice that this team didn't add actual quantifiable numbers to their metrics. They were so early in the process, they didn't know what the current measures are, so they don't know how much they should aim to improve them by. For your team, you might be able to put real numbers against these goals, for instance, raising awareness by 25%, or raising the number of reported issues by 50%. These goals and metrics are built from the pain points, and so they're completely in line with what the team heard while they were out talking to people in the field.

The design thinking process can be seen as a sprint zero type of activity or customer development and validated learning. Of course, the information you get from the design thinking process, doesn't lose its value once you move forward into code. Customers' pain points remain the same. Your product goals remain the same. You'll still run frequent validation exercises like user testing or metrics analysis. In other words, design thinking sets the scene for how the team will approach the whole development process. They'll have a set of shared experiences where they learn about who their customers really are. They'll have a common understanding of why the product is being built the way it is. And they'll be much more confident in making design decisions as the product matures.
