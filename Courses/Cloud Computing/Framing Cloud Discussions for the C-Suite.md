That is infrastructures that can be updated and changed dynamically, programmatically and automatically change the cost of operating that infrastructure, to match updated configurations. This is hugely valuable for building modern applications for three main reasons. First, the ability to adjust the number and size of servers and the other infrastructure components, allows you to quickly change the capacity of an application. This allows you to support changing application loads. If an application has higher usage, say during daylight hours and lower usage at night, then you can add additional infrastructure components during the day and remove the excess at night. Traditional infrastructure designed would require statically allocating resources that remain 24 by seven and have those resources simply lying around when not needed, which is a waste of resources. Second, dynamic infrastructure improves [Resiliency](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Resiliency.md) and availability. Typically static infrastructure operation centers require spare resources to be lying around that can be turned on and operated when needed. These initial resources are costly and do not contribute to the normal operation of the application in any meaningful way. Yet removing them dramatically increases the risk of an availability outage. In dynamic infrastructures, these resources are not required at all, because additional resources
>
> **[1:32](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/framing-cloud-discussions-for-the-c-suite/discussing-dynamic-application-infrastructures?u=76281980&t=92)** can be added in seconds or minutes, rather than days or weeks. Finally, dynamic infrastructure allows quick replacement of problematic pieces of infrastructure, whether it's software or [Hardware](../../Topics/Hardware.md) problems. If an application has a service instance that is starting to operate in correctly, rather than spending resources trying to fix the instance, you can simply terminate the instance and replace it with a brand new infrastructure component quickly and efficiently. That's not to say this isn't without its issues, though. When it comes to dynamic infrastructures, cost is an issue that is both an advantage and a disadvantage. Since you can add and remove resources dynamically as needed, you can reduce the number of spares we have lying around, which reduces costs. This flexibility is incredible. This isn't really an issue for components that you operate for relatively short periods of time, such as to handle scaling needs. Let's talk about the three basic types of expenditures a typical company might make. The first is a capital expenditure. A capital expenditure is the purchase of equipment that is expected to be used over and over again, presumably for multiple years. The single purchase buys something that is used over and over again. Capital expenditures are a fact of life for most companies and keeping them as low as possible helps to keep companies profitable. Managing capital costs is critical. The next type of expenditure a company makes is a fixed expense expenditure. A fixed expense is a purchase of an item that is used or consumed by the business over a relatively short period of time. If you construct your application to use a variable, dynamic number of servers based on the application traffic requirements, well, it's clear that as your application scales up, you can usually scale up the number of servers and other resources needed to meet that need. And if you hit a sudden traffic spike,
>

The sudden increase in traffic to a specific part of your application is a common problem in most web applications. When I was working at Amazon, we called this the Johnny Carson effect. The name comes from the sudden increase in traffic Amazon received to the video store on the day that Johnny Carson, a very famous American television host, had died. There was a sudden and huge interest in tapes of Johnny's old TV shows, so much interest that it impacted the performance of the entire website. From that point on, the impact of a sudden traffic demands on our website was called the Johnny Carson effect. The dynamic [Cloud Infrastructure](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Cloud%20Infrastructure.md) helps improve scalability and that helps improves availability. The worst thing to happen to you is to receive sudden interest in your website, lots of people head over to your website to take a look at it, but because the load is too high, the website goes down or it becomes unresponsive. Just as you get successful, you fail. This is why dynamic cloud infrastructure is so important to application availability as well as scalability, but the cloud also helps with availability in another way. What happens to your application if a data center goes down? If you have a true high availability application,
>
> **[3:06](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/framing-cloud-discussions-for-the-c-suite/discussing-cloud-scaling-and-availability?u=76281980&t=186)** your application isn't operating in just one data center. If one data center goes down, you can move traffic over to other data centers. And assuming you have the capacity, the scalability, to handle the extra load at the alternate data centers, you can keep your application running. Some applications simply have spare data center capacity that sits, mostly idle, just to handle large scale outages such as this. When using the cloud, sending up additional data centers is easy to do and you can do so in direct response to the needs of an individual incident. If a data center goes down, you can architect your system in such a way that you can easily spin up an entirely new data center worth of resources for your application in a very short matter of time, dramatically improving your measured application availability.
