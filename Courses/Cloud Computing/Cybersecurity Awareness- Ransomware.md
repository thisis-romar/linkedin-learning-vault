Once the conference attendees inserted the disks into their personal computers, they were surprised to find that the disk didn't just contain a questionnaire, as they had been made to believe, but also a ransom demand of $189. And because cell phones at that time didn't have instant money transfer apps like we do today, the targets were told to mail a check to the cyber criminal's post [office](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Microsoft%20Office.md) box. As interconnectivity, technology, and the internet continued to evolve, ransomware attacks did, too, and as the attacks grew in complexity, the ransom demands grew just as quickly.

Many of them require payments be made using [Cryptocurrency](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Cryptocurrency.md) to maintain the cyber criminal's anonymity because it's untraceable. Clearly, ransomware is a problem. That's why it's important to know how to identify it to prevent these types of attacks, and that's the topic of our next video.

First is the malware used to encrypt files so that any other files or systems that rely on these are unstable. Second, cyber criminals demand a ransom payment to remove the involuntary encryption and give their targets access to the files, therefore, its name, ransom ware, but it doesn't stop there. It's common for cyber criminals to threaten to leak or sell the information they've retrieved and encrypted if the ransom isn't paid. But how does that malware make its way to someone's computer? Ransomware efforts usually start with social engineering attacks that use psychological manipulation tactics like [Phishing](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Phishing.md). Phishing is a cyber attack that sends its targets emails that appear to be from a legitimate party but uses links to spoof sites or attachments to install malicious software. Once the malicious software is installed, users can access files, data, and sometimes systems until the ransom is paid. There are three key types of ransomware, locker ransomware, crypto ransomware, and scareware. Locker ransomware locks the user from accessing basic computer functionality until the ransom is paid. In some cases, the user may not be able to access emails or the internet while functionality is limited to the window that contains the ransom demand. The screen usually displays a locked screen when its user attempts access. Crypto ransomware encrypts local files

Affected users may also see a countdown to the ransom demand to scare them into compliance. Scareware claims that there's an issue on your computer like a virus, and request that you pay or purchase a software solution that will resolve the problem. Scareware is typically enabled by clicking unsafe links or visiting malicious websites. Users often report an exponential increase in popups. Though ransomware attacks are attempted often, many of them are unsuccessful, but when they are, the impact of ransomware can be brutally crippling for an unprepared organization. In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack infected over 200,000 computers, and over 150 countries, resulting in a global data breach that has an estimated impact of $4 billion.
