#### [What is a business information security officer (BISO)?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/what-is-a-business-information-security-officer-biso?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/what-is-a-business-information-security-officer-biso?u=76281980&t=1)** - The role of the BISO, short for business information security officer, is rapidly growing in popularity. BISOs are part of an organization's information or [Cybersecurity](../../Topics/Cybersecurity.md) function, and usually report directly to the chief information security officer or their chosen delegate. Each BISO is assigned to a particular division region or business unit across the organization where they help drive security into [Business Operations](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Business%20Operations.md). While that sounds simple BISOs have to be intentional about how they meet their goals. First it's imperative that they develop a deep understanding of the businesses they support. In fact, the BISO should be one of if not the most well versed security practitioner in the company, they should be familiar with the business' [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md) and services, customer profiles, regulatory requirements, revenues, opportunities, challenges, and strategic initiatives. Second, the BISO should work across the domains within the security function to have ongoing insight into the organization's incident history and trends, tools, vulnerabilities, threat intelligence, infrastructure, vendor risk management, and associate awareness levels. BISOs must understand what each information security team is responsible for. So they can partner and recommend enhancements and best practices to the businesses they support. This role advocates about security
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/what-is-a-business-information-security-officer-biso?u=76281980&t=94)** to the business side, but also needs to educate their fellow security practitioners about the businesses they partner with. By creating opportunities for security and business functions to collaborate, the BISO ensures the security team recommends products, best practices and services that enable the business to be safe and innovative in the most secure way possible. And when you educate the business on services performed by security business leaders are more likely to incorporate security proactively in their plans and into their operational culture. Third, the BISO must establish relationships with leaders, managers and associates across the businesses within their portfolio. This facilitates learning, builds rapport and creates a channel of communication between the business and security. The BISO can then identify key partners across the business that they can rely on, learn from and partner with to enhance the maturity of that business' security strategy. This is key as one of the most challenging aspects of the BISO role is that they must lead through influence as opposed to authority. It's rare that the information security function can dictate to the business what actions they have to take. Sure, the company has policies, procedures and standards that the business must adhere to but in no way is the BISO empowered to change a business unit strategy and operations or deploy controls.
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/what-is-a-business-information-security-officer-biso?u=76281980&t=190)** So by having an understanding of the business, knowing services provided by security and being well versed on the company's policies, procedures, and standards while [Building Trust](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Building%20Trust.md) with leaders across the teams they support, the BISO is well equipped to leverage trust and influence to partner with business leaders to drive change. Oftentimes a cyber security function as seen as a detractors from progress but the role of the BISO helps transform the perception of security from the department of no by identifying secure, thoughtful yeses.

#### [Organizational architecture](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/organizational-architecture?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/organizational-architecture?u=76281980&t=1)** - Business information security officers usually report to the chief information security officer or chief business security officer depending on their company structure, which makes sense, because the function of the role is to carry out the CISO's vision across the business units that they support. Typically, BISOs are assigned one or a set of business units that they're dedicated to. These assignments are thoughtful and strategic and can be based on anything, from domain expertise, career history, existing business relationships, or even something as simple as bandwidth. It's not uncommon for BISOs to act as many chief information security officers in the business units they work with. This means they're often consulted as a senior leader and sometimes final decision-maker on security-related business matters. This requires BISOs to be aware and familiar with the goals of the CISO, and have the autonomy to make important decisions on behalf of the information security function. It also means that BISOs are familiar with each information security domain within their group, and will pull in domain experts to support [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md) as needed. Business units are often not sure what teams to engage from within the security function when they want to roll out technology-backed [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md). This is where the BISO can help. BISOs operate as a liaison or one-stop shop for the business when it needs information security support.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/organizational-architecture?u=76281980&t=95)** However, this does not interrupt any relationships that exist between business leaders and other security teams. In fact, the BISO role was put in place to add to and enhance the connections between the security function and broader [Business Operations](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Business%20Operations.md). While it's important that BISOs work closely with chief information security officers, they also have to work with leaders across the business units that they support. The BISO functionally reports into the security organization, but it's crucial that the business knows the BISO is on their side, campaigning for them across information security, or infosec, just as they campaign for infosec with the same business partners. Take a look at your company's information security function and see if they have a BISO team. And if so, who leads that team and who their peers are. This will help you understand their level of influence, autonomy, and how they're positioned for success.

That's good news for folks interested in becoming a BISO because that broad range of skills can come from any number of professional roles. In other words, you don't have to take the traditional career path through tech to succeed as a BISO. Instead, think back on the core skills you've developed in various roles throughout your career and consider how they'll map to those required for a BISO. Let me use my own atypical path as an example. I started my career on Wall Street at an investment bank, managing relationships with other banks and monitoring account activity. That role taught me to always consider the financial impact of business decisions and the importance of building relationships. Next, I moved into a management consulting role at a Big Four firm where the work was project-based and I frequently faced a new team, new clients, and new challenges. I had to learn quickly, solve problems, present to executives, and deliver value, all skills I continue to use daily as a BISO. Then I became a certified internal auditor at a video game company where I learned about risk, controls, governance, and how to influence and negotiate. The BISO role is always tasks with balancing the priorities of information security and [Business Operations](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Business%20Operations.md).
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/the-instructor-s-journey-to-becoming-a-biso?u=76281980&t=96)** In fact, a lot of BISO successes come from understanding how to manage risk and influencing business leaders to adopt secure practices while not disrupting innovation. Later, I got certified in risk management assurance and began working in project and program management for a team that built, staffed, and managed international offices. Working on a global team showed me how culture impacts the way teams work and that people are a company's greatest asset. Most of the challenges we face in information security are rooted in human behavior, not technology. This last role helped me learn strategies to improve collaboration and understand what teams need to thrive, because teamwork really does make the dream work. So as you can see, my path to being a BISO was not traditional. And in fact, there is no single path to becoming a BISO. What's more important is being able to assess your career history and identify the relevant skills, both technical and soft, that you've built and strengthened along the way. Take a moment now to write down some of the valuable skills you've learned along your professional journey. Chances are they may serve you well in your pursuit of the BISO role.

People bring their problems to the BSO in the hopes of finding creative and secure solutions. Often these problems are new and challenging, and bring a lot of inconveniences to business leaders and InfoSec teams alike. But with an innovative and optimistic mindset, BSOs will quickly realize that they're uniquely positioned to help their business partners and InfoSec peers navigate and overcome these challenges. Let's walk through some strategies BSOs can use to effectively and efficiently address problems. First, get to the root cause of the issue. When our business partners approach us with challenges, more likely than not, they're frustrated by the symptoms they experience, like increased client complaints, unnecessarily long process times, spikes in fraud activity, and product failures, and solving for symptoms is not where you want to start. Yes, let your business partner vent, and you can even ask them what downstream impact those symptoms are creating across various parts of their organization and for their customers. But this is where active listening comes in. Ask your business partner what caused the symptoms, or try The Five Whys methodology. This is an interrogative technique that helps you explore underlying cause and effect relationships by asking why something is happening,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-careers-become-a-business-information-security-officer-biso/innovative-problem-solving?u=76281980&t=94)** then inquiring, "Why?" when you get the answer to that question, and, "Why?" of the next answer, until you've asked five why questions to drill down to the root of the problem. If you want to learn more about The Five Whys technique, you can do that in the Critical Thinking course right here on [LinkedIn](../../Skills/Web%20Development/LinkedIn.md) learning. Once you understand what's causing the issue, you can partner with your information security or business teams to resolve it. Finally, determine what additional risk exists. You can now transform the, "No," to, "Yes, and," improving the experience of your business teams and managing the identified risk. I encourage all BSOs to not run away from problems but to run toward them. Finding opportunities to develop solutions builds trust, optimizes business and customer experiences, and creates space that balances risk and innovation.
