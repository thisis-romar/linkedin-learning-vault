#### [Weighted shortest job first](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-development-in-the-new-world-of-work/weighted-shortest-job-first-27893110?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-development-in-the-new-world-of-work/weighted-shortest-job-first-27893110?u=76281980&t=1)** - Let's talk about a backlog refinement technique known as weighted shortest job first or WSJF. It's a prioritization tool that teams use to prioritize and sequence which work items should be delivered from a large backlog to provide maximum benefit. We first calculate the cost of waiting on each deliverable in our backlog, or in [Agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md), It is known as calculating the cost of delay. We then apply the level of effort to deliver the work to ensure we are working on the items that would create higher value if they were delivered sooner. When I was a member of a development team tasked with delivery of the company's first mobile application, using WSJF made it possible. In our retrospective, we discovered that not all small items are equal in value delivery, and some could wait longer than others to be delivered. We also noticed that there were other items that happened to be time-sensitive and would cost us time and value if we waited longer to complete them. We reviewed the backlog and we scored each item around key criteria. By using the scoring system of one for small, two for medium, and three for large, we assigned a score for value, which spoke to how much value the work would provide when we completed it. Value is provided by the stakeholders and the team collectively. This is the value score. We then calculated a score based on the time pressure for the delivery of the work. We used the calendar and discussed
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-development-in-the-new-world-of-work/weighted-shortest-job-first-27893110?u=76281980&t=94)** when each item would need to be delivered by. The items that could be delivered the latest were scored with a one, and the items that needed to be delivered as soon as possible were given a score of three, and we aligned all the work in between. The next step is to add those two numbers together. The total is the cost of delay. Once each backlog item has a cost of delay calculated, we then divide the cost of delay by the level of effort for each deliverable. So if we have an app feature scored as a three on time pressure, and scored as a three on value, the total cost of delay to deliver this app feature would be six. We scored a value of two for level of effort, so we took the cost of delay score of six, and divided it by the level of effort score of two. The result is the WSJF score of three. We then calculated the WSJF score for all the other items in the backlog. The items with the highest WSJF score became the first items we would pull into sprints for delivery and provided the most value. Remember, teamwork makes the dream work, and this is a team effort. Ensure that all your team members and stakeholders have a voice in prioritizing and sequencing your backlog. The weighted shortest job first calculation is one of the best ways to do this as a team.

We created a set of two primary objectives for the app we were developing that the team would rally around. [User experience](../../Skills/Web%20Development/User%20Experience%20(UX).md) and security. We wanted to make sure that we always took into account the users having a wonderful experience. We then detailed a measurable key result that we could use as a rallying point and a way to demonstrate progress. We measured the number of pain points a user may experience and works to deliver removing each pain point. Before starting the project, we were provided with a list of pain points that should be addressed with the final delivery. In our sprint planning we were sure to reference these user experience frictions and the security standards that should be met in our sprint. In the sprint review, we updated our work towards delivering key results.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-development-in-the-new-world-of-work/objective-and-key-results?u=76281980&t=94)** We removed pain points with the work delivered in this particular sprint. As we delivered work we were sure to indicate which pain points was resolved. For example, if we delivered work that reduced one of our pain points, we would reduce our count of open pain points. The goal of objectives and key results was to have a way to define and track our progress through concrete, specific and measurable actions and to ensure everyone was continuing in the same direction with clear priorities and a constant rhythm. In the end we deliver and our application. And while we did not deliver all the features from the backlog, we delivered on our objectives and key results. Objectives and key results help the team focus on the key features for success as opposed to focusing on just all of the features.
