This training course focuses on these oft-overlooked topics, providing the critical knowledge to take you to next level in DevOps. Instructor Michael Jenkin

Use virtual meeting spaces to allow everyone to benefit from the knowledge sharing. #### [Avoiding burnout](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Instructor] Working long hours under stressful conditions day after day after day will only lead to one thing, burnout. Let's talk about burnout and go over some ways we can avoid it. The Mayo Clinic describes burnout as work-related stress that results in physical or emotional exhaustion. In extreme cases, burnout can affect physical and mental health. We can expect to have some stress in our work and our life but when stress goes on for long periods of times, we can burn out. At work, some of these stresses include a lack of control over things like schedules and workload, a disorganized or constantly changing work environment or the opposite of that, having a work routine that's the same day after day. Signs of burnout in yourself or your team include reduced productivity, difficulty concentrating, an increase in irritability or impatience or at the most extremes, problems with mental or physical health. Fortunately, there are steps you can take to prevent burnout. The most important step you can take is opening up. Communicate with your team and management. Let them know how you're feeling and see if anything can be done to minimize the stress you or your team is experiencing. Use these conversations to set realistic goals for what needs to be done right away
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/avoiding-burnout?u=76281980&t=94)** and what can be put on pause for a bit. This can help set expectations for everyone. Also, get support from family, close friends or health professionals. Outside of work, you'll need to prioritize your health. Regular physical activity can help improve your mental state, increase concentration and reduce anxiety. You'll get additional benefits from exercise by also choosing to eat healthy foods. This could simply mean minimizing processed foods and avoiding sugary foods and alcoholic beverages. Taking breaks during work will also help with preventing burnout. Even just a few moments diverted from the task at hand will keep your mind fresh and help with focus when you return to work and of course, a little bit of R and R is great for reducing stress. Make time for your hobbies and interests outside of work and also make time for seven to eight hours of sleep if you can get it and for the ultimate break, take a vacation. Use that time to really disconnect from work and stress for an extended period.

If you're curious about ChatOps but haven't taken the leap, getting started might be easier than you think. Here are a few ideas to help you get started. One of the easiest ways to start with ChatOps is to centralize your communication in one place. Many of the tools and applications used by DevOps teams have integrations with popular chat clients that allow them to send notifications and alerts directly into chats. Additionally, service providers provide status updates via RSS that can be pulled into chats as well. Having information from applications and services automatically provided and readily at hand helps with the flow of conversation around development and operational capabilities. It also keeps team members from having to track down the details from an email, websites,
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/using-chatops-for-automation?u=76281980&t=96)** or other remote sources of information. If you're overwhelmed by team members asking you if the code has been pushed or if the last build was successful, ChatOps can help. Down the pipeline, CI/CD tools like [[Jenkins]], GitHub Actions and Travis CI can report into chats when builds or deployments start, and then update with the status when the job is complete. #### [Applying DevOps to boring technology](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/applying-devops-to-boring-technology?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/applying-devops-to-boring-technology?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Instructor] Engineers and developers love to create and work with the latest technology. After all, we were trained to use tech to solve new and challenging problems. However, we also spend a lot of time maintaining systems that some might consider boring. Perhaps changes to the system are rare or maybe when those changes are made, they're done manually. Yikes. Or maybe the technology being used is what you would call classic. You know, it's not running the latest and greatest tech from the last year or two or maybe even 10. But even in these cases, we should still be using [[DevOps]] principles and practices. Let's take a look. Honestly, I like it when a system doesn't change too often. That usually means things are stable and less likely to break. But what if something does break? In these cases, one of the most important things to have in place is a disaster recovery plan. You should have backups in place for critical data and configuration information along with clear instructions for how to do a restore. You should take this one step further by having regularly scheduled exercises where you do a dry run with your disaster recovery plan in a sandbox environment. And if you're working with a team, make sure everyone participates to prevent tribal knowledge. This is especially true if you're the one person who knows how everything works.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/applying-devops-to-boring-technology?u=76281980&t=96)** Any system where all changes are made manually is ripe for automation. If you can, develop a process to automate any changes made to the system. I'll acknowledge that automation can be difficult if you're working with a technology that can be accessed using an API or a command line interface, for example. If change automation is impossible or not worth the effort, you can try to automate testing. A detailed test suite would be ideal and can be easily implemented if the system has a web interface. And if nothing else, document the procedure for making changes. You can create a detailed checklist for signing off on changes and, much like disaster recovery, have the entire team get familiar with the process and practice it. When I'm working with technology that I call classic, my biggest concern is security. As software and operating systems mature, bad actors find ways to take advantage of security holes. Don't fall prey to unpatched vulnerabilities or insecure configurations for the sake of running a stable system. And while you're at it, start looking for a modern equivalent. New solutions are always being developed and there's likely a more efficient and more secure solution available. If a modern equivalent is available, develop a proof of concept and share the results with your team and stakeholders. Use this as an opportunity to collaborate and build partnerships.
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devops-tips/applying-devops-to-boring-technology?u=76281980&t=190)** And I'll admit, rolling out a new solution for a tried and true system might be a hard pitch to sell. But in the end, your entire team and maybe even your entire company will benefit from it.

Microservices communicate with each other using HTTP requests, or some other common protocol like message queues. Additionally, each service is developed, tested and deployed independently. This makes for fast iterations when it comes to developing new features. Let's see how our monolithic web application might be broken up into microservices. On top of that, I keep the messages sorted into a channel specifically for blog updates. That way, conversations in other channels aren't disrupted and I can scan the blog channel at my leisure. For some time now, I've also been getting industry updates from podcasts. I like podcasts because I can listen to them passively on my commute or while I'm doing something else like exercising. Podcasts are also shared on RSS feeds so it's easy to follow them on computers and smartphones. I especially like listening to podcasts produced by cloud providers. I like the way they share new features, product updates, and interviews with customers using their [[Microsoft Products|products]] to solve real problems. All of the major cloud providers have their own podcasts

Twitch is one of the newer outlets that I've been following for devops news. During live streams, engineers and programmers demonstrate their skills during live coding sessions. What's really cool is that Twitch makes these [[Real-Time]] sessions interactive through a chat interface. One of my favorite Twitch streams is produced by [[Amazon Web Services (AWS)|Amazon Web Services]]. I'm really hooked on the live demonstrations showing new and innovative ways to use the AWS cloud platform. So these are a few different ways I keep up with the latest DevOps news: blogs, which continue to be a great source of information, podcasts that are portable and keep my hands free, and Twitch streams that deliver demonstrations that are in depth, real time, and interactive.
