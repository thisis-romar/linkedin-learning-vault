This was a secret oath based organization of English textile workers that destroyed textile machinery as a form of protest. They were upset at the use of mechanical devices, and what they considered to be a fraudulent and deceitful manner being used to get around standard labor practices of the day. So here we are, over 200 years later. And people have similar fears about the impact of emerging technologies like robots and AI on the workplace. Some things never change.

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/future-proofing-your-data-science-career/data-throughout-history?u=76281980&t=0)** - A good place to start when considering the history of [Data Science](../../Topics/Data%20Science.md) is in the early part of the 19th century. An English inventor and mechanical engineer named Charles Babbage designed a machine to mimic human learning and problem solving. In June of 1822, he submitted a paper to the Royal Society in England with the title "Note on the Application of Machinery to the Computation "of Very Big Mathematical Tables." Quite a title. Babbage proposed building an automated mechanical calculator called the difference engine. It was designed to be really the first calculator. A few years later, he proposed what he called the analytical engine to provide a fully-fledged general purpose computation device. Unfortunately, a working version was never completed in his lifetime, but in 1991, a version of the difference engine was built at the London Science Museum. It consists of 8000 parts. It weighs five tons, and it measures 11 feet long and seven feet high, and it actually works. Babbage also wrote what could be viewed as the first proposal for how to apply data to business. His paper published in 1832 was called "On the Economy of Machinery and Manufactures." In it, he focused on how man-made tools could help drive business models using mathematics. Looking back, we've gone from Babbage's difference engine to, today, Amazon's recommendation engine. There are lots of other interesting examples
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/future-proofing-your-data-science-career/data-throughout-history?u=76281980&t=94)** of the people and tools that have advanced the art of data science. One of my personal favorite examples of what might be called the initial data science tool is called the Antikythera mechanism, an ancient Greek mechanical device. It's manufacture is currently dated to around 100 B.C., and speculation is that the famed Greek mathematician and astronomer Archimedes had a hand in its design, but no one really knows for sure who built it. This mechanism could predict astronomical positions of the four planets closest to the sun, as well as solar eclipses decades in advance. It could also be used to track the four-year cycle of the ancient Olympic Games. Talk about a breakthrough data science application, wow. Fast forward to 1969 and the creation of the ARPANET, which was the first computer to computer link, and it was designed to connect government and academic institutions. This innovation laid the groundwork for the Internet as we know it today. These devices are simply examples of humans making complex tools that manipulate data to help us live better and make our work easier. The really exciting news about all this is that data science is about to enter an entirely new era. Always keep that in mind when thinking about what's next on your career path. I urge you to discover and embrace the changes and tools and processes that will transform the data science discipline. Remember, years ago, computer scientists used to write code in the assembly language. Then it was Fortran, then COBOL.
