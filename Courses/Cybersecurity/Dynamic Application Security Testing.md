The big thing to look for when testing for SSRF flaws is URL validation. Again, apps should not assume that every URL can be trusted as is. You should also take a look at the app architecture to determine whether segmentation has been implemented. If an SSRF flaw slips through the cracks, you can proactively minimize the exposure and the damage by limiting the internal network traffic to only those ports and services that are required for business use. And with the ever-increasing adoption of [Cloud Services](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Cloud%20Services.md) the presence of defined and enforced [Cloud Security](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Cloud%20Security.md) configuration standards is a pretty big deal.
>
> **[3:11](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/dynamic-application-security-testing/a10-server-side-request-forgery-ssrf?u=76281980&t=191)** An oversight that leaves a [Cloud Storage](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Cloud%20Storage.md) bucket overexposed results in an SSRF vulnerability that's exceedingly simple to exploit. Unfortunately, we've seen some prominent data breaches in recent years for this very reason. Input sanitation and validation are at the top of the remediation list for SSRF vulnerabilities. As long as the app doesn't assume it can trust user supplied URLs, attackers are going to have a much harder time finding and exploiting SSRF vulnerabilities. You should also consider disabling HTTP redirects. Those redirects might be a convenient way to route users to the right location, but they can also be exploited by attackers to force one of your servers to access a malicious URL that the attacker controls. One other control you should consider is allow lists. If you know what normal app traffic looks like, you could put rules in place that say, anything outside of that known good pattern should fail. OWASP strongly encourages a allow list over deny list. Since attackers have multiple ways to circumvent deny list controls. The OWASP Server Side Request Forgery Prevention Cheat Sheet is a terrific resource to build into your developer security training. It provides some clear examples of these types of attacks including a reminder that protocols
>
> **[4:44](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/dynamic-application-security-testing/a10-server-side-request-forgery-ssrf?u=76281980&t=284)** other than HTTP are just as vulnerable. For a more detailed analysis of SSRF attack prevention you could jump straight to the SSRF Bible from the Wallarm research team.
