#### [Joint controllers and third-party partners](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/gdpr-compliance-essential-training-14328961/joint-controllers-and-third-party-partners?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/gdpr-compliance-essential-training-14328961/joint-controllers-and-third-party-partners?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Earlier we spoke about data controllers and processors. There are two other groups of organizations you need to consider: joint controllers and third parties. Joint controllers, also known as co-controllers, are defined in Article 26 of the GDPR, which is a situation where two or more controllers jointly determine why and how to process personal data. Joint controllers usually have shared purposes. An example of this may be an organization that creates a [[Facebook]] fan page that people can like or follow. Facebook has responsibility for protecting the data, as does the organization collecting information on their specific page. Under these arrangements, each controller remains responsible for complying to the GDPR. Joint controllers must enter into an agreement setting out their respective responsibilities for complying to the different obligations of GDPR. Additionally, it is important that the components of this agreement are clearly communicated to individuals whose data is being processed, so they have a clear ability to exercise their rights with each controller. GDPR also extends to third parties. While it may not be obvious, there is a difference between processors and third parties. Processors process personal data based on a written contract.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/gdpr-compliance-essential-training-14328961/joint-controllers-and-third-party-partners?u=76281980&t=94)** Third parties are all other parties with whom you share personal data. The difference is that third parties can process personal data for their own purposes. Let's break it down with an example. If you sign a processing agreement with [[Google]] Analytics and the data gathered via cookies on your domain, then Google would be a processor for you. You are responsible for that data. However, if Google is receiving data from your website visitors for their own purposes, then they would be considered a third-party service. Seeing how many data breaches happen through third-party relationships, it is important for organizations to ensure that their third parties are handling [[Data Privacy]] and security in a compliant way as well. Some methods to do that include providing minimum security requirements or frameworks, vendor risk assessments during contracting and then on a regular basis for any changes, self-certifications and acknowledgements by the third party, and independent audits. Understanding joint controllers and third parties creates visibility into your third-party risks and can ensure that the parties you do business with and share data with remain compliant with GDPR.

Finally, in the case of an investigation,

> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/gdpr-compliance-essential-training-14328961/data-protection-officer-dpo?u=76281980&t=189)** the data protection officer must help with the cooperation between their organization and the governing agency.
