>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-at-work-22609530/social-engineering-overview?u=76281980&t=0)** - I talk to people every day about security vulnerabilities in [Hardware](../../Topics/Hardware.md) and software, but there's also a social component that is really important for all of us to understand. Here's how the U.S. [Cybersecurity](../../Topics/Cybersecurity.md) and Infrastructure Security Agency describes social engineering. "In a social engineering attack, an attacker uses human interaction to obtain or compromise information about an organization or its computer systems." An attacker may seem unassuming and respectable, possibly claiming to be a new employee, repair person, or researcher, and even offer credentials to support that identity. However, by asking questions, he or she may be able to piece together enough information to infiltrate an organization's network. If an attacker is not able to gather enough information from one source, he or she may contact another source within the same organization and rely on the information from the first source to add to his or her credibility. Basically, what this means is that while some cybersecurity attacks exploit technical vulnerabilities in software, others exploit psychological vulnerabilities in human behavior. The most effective cybersecurity attacks often use both. We're going to look at a few examples of social engineering attacks, and some tips on how to avoid them.

You might be working at a coffee shop, a conference center, a coworking space, or an airport. Public wifi is not inherently secure. It's important to understand that when you're using public wifi, you may be inadvertently broadcasting information that anyone can eavesdrop on. To understand why public wifi is less secure, let's discuss a few of the fundamental concepts of [Wireless Networking](../../Skills/Network%20and%20System%20Administration/Wireless%20Networking.md). Wireless networks allow communication between devices without the use of a physical connection like an ethernet cable. Wifi was invented in 1997 and became popular in the early 2000s. Today, laptops and smartphones connect to wifi pretty seamlessly. When your phone, tablet, or laptop connects to a wifi router, it sends and receives information using little digital packages called frames. The information sent from your device to a wifi router is unencrypted unless you take steps to secure it. You can think about data on an insecure wireless network kind of like sending postcards in the mail. If you can get a hold of a postcard, you can read the message. It's fairly easy for a hacker to purchase a $20 device that will allow them to sniff any and all unencrypted traffic on a wireless network that is not secured using WPA2 and a reasonably strong password.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-at-work-22609530/public-wi-fi?u=76281980&t=96)** HTTPS encryption, that little padlock in the corner by a website's address does not guarantee security. It's also fairly easy for a hacker to set up their own malicious wifi network, pretending to be a legitimate one. Unsuspecting wifi users might connect to a wifi network that's actually hosted by a hacker, and inadvertently send over some information that wasn't intended to be shared. While some of these attacks have fun names, like Pineapple or person-in-the-middle, they're actually dangerous security risks. So what's the best way to proceed? First, if your company requires the use of a VPN to connect to the company's internal network, connect to the VPN immediately upon joining a public wifi network. Second, take a risk-based approach to the types of online activity that you do while using a public wifi network, for example, you might be comfortable reading news articles on a public wifi network, but choose to wait until you're on a secured network to conduct online [Banking](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Banking.md) activity or handle other sensitive information. Third, if available, use your own personal hotspot, a wifi connection to your cell phone. Make sure to enforce a strong password so that not just anyone can connect to your network. Work that you do on a public wifi network is not necessarily private. It may seem like it's not a big deal,
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/cybersecurity-at-work-22609530/public-wi-fi?u=76281980&t=188)** especially when it looks like everyone else casually uses public wifi to do everything, but threats are real. Hackers rely on the fact that most people assume public wifi is generally safe, and they specifically target heavily used networks to conduct their attacks.
