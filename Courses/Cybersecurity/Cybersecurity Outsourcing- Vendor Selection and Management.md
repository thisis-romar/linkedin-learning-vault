But first, we need to define a key term, MSSP. It means managed security service provider. You can outsource many security functions to an MSSP, such as [[Intrusion Detection]] [[System Monitoring]], 24 by seven [[Security Operations]] center, and vulnerability testing. When you work with an MSSP, there are two common types of business arrangements, outsourcing, where the work happens at the MSSP offices, using their equipment, and co-sourcing, a combination of outsourcing and insourcing that lets the MSSP use your equipment installed in your building. A good example is security event collection and analysis, although many security [[Microsoft Products|products]] could be sold this way. Now, there are three major differences between managing work that is insourced versus outsourced. First, there's control. With insourcing, you have daily control over your staff, including work schedule and visibility into their work. When you outsource, there's a lot more distance between you and the people doing the work. The outsourcer gets to decide job assignments
>

With insourcing, you can move people to tasks as needed. At a minimum, be sure to require your MSSP to protect your sensitive data and allow you to terminate the contract if they do not, and build a financial firewall in your MSSP contract by requiring them to pay for the costs of any data breach that they cause. One final tip, you can't transfer all your cybersecurity risk to the MSSP, so don't lose your sense of ownership along the way.
