I recognize that my code will be attacked by talented and persistent adversaries who threaten our physical, economic and national security. I recognize these things, and I choose to be rugged. I am rugged because I refuse to be a source of vulnerability or weakness.

> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/devsecops-tips-for-success/find-a-rugged-path-for-software?u=76281980&t=93)** I am rugged because I assure my code will support its mission. I am rugged because my code can face these challenges and persist in spite of them. I am rugged, not because it is easy, but because it is necessary, and I am up to the challenge. This approach to building software really struck a chord with me. It became part of my guiding principles to how I build and create systems and security defenses. I encourage you to adopt this as well. In many ways, I feel rugged was a precursor to [DevSecOps](../../Skills/DevOps/DevSecOps.md). It addresses the same values we have in DevOps, but from an angle of more secure software, software that is meant to last, software that is safe. Now, here we are in the present. What do we see as important pieces of rugged in leading DevSecOps teams today? First, we need to produce a Software Bill of Materials. Do we know all the software and all the versions that we have running? Do we know what dependencies and libraries our software uses? Rugged means knowing all your component parts. An SBOM, or a Software Bill of Materials, includes all the component parts that built into your software. It lets you know really what you have running. Another piece of rugged software is favoring short-lived systems over long-running systems. Think farms over gardens. Farms turn over quickly. They're flexible to adding new crops, and farmers don't have favorite plants. If crops are destroyed, the farm can just keep on going. But a gardener at home builds a garden as a passion project.

He has an acrostic that he wrote that surfaces the three most important characteristics that provide a more secure system. It is called DIE, and it stands for Distributed, Immutable and Ephemeral. The research shows that if your system can exhibit any of these three, but preferably all three, you'll have a provably more secure system. As we build systems, look for ways to make them distributed, immutable and ephemeral. These may not be the first properties you would think of, but that's part of the rugged paradigm. Rugged doesn't mean one thing for everyone, much like the "[Agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md) Manifesto," rugged is loose so that you can take it and use it and make decisions for your software and your organization. Rugged can be part of the path to building a more secure system. It's not an end, but it's a journey.
