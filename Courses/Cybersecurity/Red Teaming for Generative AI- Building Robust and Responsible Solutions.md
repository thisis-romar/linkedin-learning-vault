Before we proceed, let's clarify the difference between Gen AI systems and Gen AI solutions. While Gen AI solutions refer to the applications or implementations of [Generative AI](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Generative%20AI.md) technology to solve specific problems, Gen AI systems encompass broader infrastructure, architecture, and ecosystem. Training data attacks include dataset poisoning and hyperparameter tempering. Dataset poisoning is when bad actors inject biased or malicious data into the training dataset of a generative AI model. This can lead to model learning incorrect patterns, or making biased predictions, compromising its integrity and reliability. Attackers could use deepfakes to prompt Gen AI systems to generate realistic, but fabricated content such as fake news articles or videos featuring manipulated political speeches. Hyperparameter tempering refers to manipulating hyperparameters, such as learning rate and batch size to significantly compromise performance, functionality, or integrity. [Red Teaming](../../Skills/Cybersecurity/Red%20Teaming.md) being one of them. Organizations can use red teaming for several specific purposes, including identifying vulnerabilities, ensuring ethical compliance, and improving the robustness and fairness of the system. Adversarial prompting is a specific red teaming technique where the attacker focuses on crafting prompts and cautions that exploit the weakness in the GenAI model. This technique is great for testing biases and finding security holes. You can also use red teaming to simulate data poisoning attacks that manipulate the training data to introduce biases or steer the model towards unintended outputs. It reveals weaknesses in the model's ability to learn from clean data and avoid manipulation. This knowledge helps build more robust models that are less susceptible to real world attacks. Fuzzing is another red teaming technique that identifies bugs in the model's code that might not be caught with the traditional testing methods. These bugs could potentially be exploited by attackers to manipulate the model's output. Depending on your GenAI system and solution, you can pick a combination of red teaming techniques to help you detect vulnerabilities and enhance the robustness.

When building the red team, focus on three aspects, experience, mindset, and features to be tested. Different from building a red team that comprises [Cybersecurity](../../Topics/Cybersecurity.md) experts exclusively, you need a diverse team. As you select the team members, include individuals with diverse backgrounds and expertise, such as AI specialists, social scientists, end users, and security experts. For instance, if you're developing a chat bot for education providers, include education experts who can identify domain specific risks and end users who can identify prompt issues. Also, including members from different demographics, such as age, will help you identify biases. The user behavior evolves with the evolution in the digital landscape. Including team members from various age groups can bring a fresh perspective on how people interact with technology, helping to identify biases related to the modes of communication. Now, let's talk about mindset. You need people with both adversarial and benign mindsets to look for possible issues. Create a team that is a mix of security professionals and users. Team members with [Security Testing](../../Skills/Cybersecurity/Security%20Testing.md) experience and a knack for thinking like attackers will help identify security vulnerabilities. On your team, also include astute, benign users. These people will help highlight potential harms and usability issues that users might face.

Data augmentation involves expanding the training data set by generating new data points from existing data sets using a variety of techniques, such as rotation, translation, or flipping. These techniques help improve the model's generalization capabilities by exposing it to a wider range of variations and scenarios during training. You might find vulnerabilities in models architecture itself during red teaming. Techniques like adversarial training can address these issues. In adversarial training, the Gen AI model is deliberately exposed to malicious inputs, specifically designed to trick the model. By constantly being challenged with these examples during training, the model becomes more robust and less susceptible to manipulation attempts in real world scenarios.
