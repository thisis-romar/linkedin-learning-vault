#### [Consider a disaster recovery plan](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ubuntu-linux-operating-system-basics/consider-a-disaster-recovery-plan?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ubuntu-linux-operating-system-basics/consider-a-disaster-recovery-plan?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] We don't often want to think about a disaster impacting our systems. But it's something a responsible administrator needs to consider. A disaster isn't necessarily just a huge weather event or an earthquake that takes out power to your data center. A disaster can be something as seemingly small as a server outage, or a bad configuration, or another system administrator maliciously deleting data, or even an outage of your internet service provider. Disaster recovery is a whole topic unto itself and it's important to consider what it would take to get your services back up and running in a minimum amount of time. Large enterprises often have whole data centers online and ready to go in the event that their primary systems have problems. If you're working in a small business, having that kind of ready redundancy may be impractical. But if your ability to keep your systems up and going is the lifeline of your business, there are a few levels of protection you should consider. At the absolute minimum, you should have backups of all of your critical information - [Databases](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Databases.md), applications, configurations, documents, and documentation should be backed up regularly. And your backups should be tested regularly as well. You certainly want to be sure that anything backed up can be restored. Don't just trust that it'll work when it's needed. You should also consider how your backups are stored. Having your only backup on the same network means the backups are probably in the same physical place, and would be vulnerable to disasters that affect that space - a fire, a power outage, or even ransomware could compromise your backups and your live data at the same time. For this reason, you should consider keeping
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ubuntu-linux-operating-system-basics/consider-a-disaster-recovery-plan?u=76281980&t=93)** a copy of your backups disconnected from the network and the internet, and in a separate physical location. Some vendors offer this service, and will pickup and store backup tapes for you. Many business will keep a local, online copy of backups and send offline backups to a remote storage facility to allow them to do minor recoveries quickly, and still have a safety layer of offsite backups, too. Depending on the setup of your systems, and how critical they are to your business, you may not be able to tolerate a local [Hardware](../../Topics/Hardware.md) failure in the event of a disaster. You may need to setup a remote site that has a mirror, or enough of a mirror, of your system to operate your services temporarily. It can be a good practice to build a duplicate of your production and testing systems on a cloud provider, or in shared or dedicated data center space, in a different location or region. The extent to which this secondary system is online, up-to-date, and ready to go, will be a business decision your leadership team will need to make. In perfect world, we'd just have a big button on the wall that would instantly switch traffic, app hosting, [Data Management](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Data%20Management.md), and everything else to a secondary system that's powered on, configured, and up-to-date with the latest data. But for most organizations, that's impractical. Disaster planning doesn't have to only involve setting up a remote instance of your platform. Sometimes hardware dies, and it may be worth having a replacement server, or disks, or network switch, or so on, available in a box or already in the rack, so you don't have to wait to order a replacement when there's a failure. You need to work with your organization's leadership team and stakeholders to define what the parameters
>
> **[3:05](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/ubuntu-linux-operating-system-basics/consider-a-disaster-recovery-plan?u=76281980&t=185)** of disaster recovery are for your business. What front-end and back-end systems need to be available if the primary data center goes offline? How often is customer data fed into the backup system? Who has the authority to decide when to switch to and from the backup site? And how often will you test the processes that switch your operations from your primary hardware to the backups? And what are the criteria for success? All of your procedures, policies, and decisions should be documented clearly, and in a way that's accessible to the people who need access to them. And you need to plan for interruptions in the ability of your team to execute that plan as well. A major disaster may make it impossible for the database administrator to get to the backup site, or for the network administrator to make changes to the network policies that redirect traffic. An ongoing disaster will require you to consider the welfare of your workers as well, who may be dealing with their own problems as a result of the disaster. While you should know your system well enough to know how to rebuild it if the need arises, it's often recommended to seek out a professional in the area of data recovery to help you establish your policies. They'll be able to look at your system through a different lens, and will likely be able to help identify issues you may not have thought of.

You and your team may need to support other teams during an outage or some other kind of trouble. Be sure to find out what your expected role is and what policies are in place that you'll need to follow. To facilitate communication, businesses usually have a cross-team communication platform already determined and set up for use.
