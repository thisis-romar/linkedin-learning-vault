#### [Continue to evaluate after a training](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/crisis-communication-planning-after-a-ransomware-or-other-cybersecurity-attack/continue-to-evaluate-after-a-training?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/crisis-communication-planning-after-a-ransomware-or-other-cybersecurity-attack/continue-to-evaluate-after-a-training?u=76281980&t=1)** - [Narrator] Once the crisis ends, you'll want to assess what aspects of your communication went well, what parts did not, and what opportunities exist for improvement? It's all about [Resiliency](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Resiliency.md). I've seen too often when companies create a new strategy, only to have the plan filed in the appropriate virtual folder and never seen again. Don't do this. The first thing you should do after a crisis is canvas your communications team and ask the following. How well did we prepare for this event? What tactics met or exceeded expectations? How should we include additional tactics in future incidents like this one? Who are the best spokespeople and who needed improvement? Who else should we put on the crisis communication team? How well was our messaging received, and how quickly were inquiries routed, and how fast did we respond? It's essential to evaluate performance when it's fresh in your mind. When the crisis ebbs, catch your breath, get a good night's sleep, and then begin the evaluation. Your fresh memory will help capture all the nuances of the activities and strategies that the team undertook. It will help make your plan much better. Also get candid feedback through one-on-one discussions, group meetings, and online surveys from your counterparts in IT, HR, legal, customer service, and other departments. They will provide invaluable insight into where your plan succeeded and fell short. Many of these groups dealt directly with customers and other audiences and will obtain more candid feedback on how they felt they were kept informed on the cyber attack, than they may be when they're talking to you. They won't feel so pressured to essentially say what you want to hear.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/crisis-communication-planning-after-a-ransomware-or-other-cybersecurity-attack/continue-to-evaluate-after-a-training?u=76281980&t=94)** Getting their help to improve your readiness for another crisis is critical. Once you obtain the relevant information, update the communication plan's messaging, tactics, timelines, and gotcha questions accordingly, and then send it around again for review. Be sure folks see what will be expected, if a cyber breach happens again. This is important, so that you get people generally familiar with the changes in your communications approach during a ransomware or other cyber attack. Doing so, will ensure that your customers, employees, investors, and partners know you didn't just move on, and that your organization is now moving in one direction together. Even if you might feel exhausted after a crisis ends and the last thing you want to do is revisit it, it's really important to do so. Taking time to evaluate and update your plan at the end of a cyber event, and then at least semi-annually afterwards, will reduce your chances of going through something like that again, or at least do an even better job next time.

Doing so offers a practical approach to training the plan in a way that will ensure your team retains the information better. Here are a couple of examples of cases I recommend you use.
