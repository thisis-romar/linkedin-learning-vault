Listen out for statements that give a false sense of security, such as, "That couldn't happen to us." Second, rationale. Our brains need to resolve discrepancies when there's cognitive dissonance. If we're not willing to be wrong, then we'll invent reasons that we're right. Listen for statements that dismiss further investigation
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-creating-an-agile-culture/avoiding-groupthink-30025004?u=76281980&t=190)** and offer easy solutions, such as, "It's probably nothing, you're just worrying too much." Third, morality. When group members believe strongly in the inherent morality of their group, individuals are more likely to dismiss consequences that don't align with their beliefs. Listen out for statements that dismiss evidence that contradicts established viewpoints, such as, "It doesn't matter if the study had a small sample size. We know we're right." Fourth, stereotypes. When team members are similar, it's easy to make false assumptions about people who are seen to be in different groups. Be aware of decisions that are rooted in group differences. For example, "You know what? He wouldn't understand anyway. He's part of the business." Fifth, pressure. In groupthink situations, dissent is quickly squashed by direct pressure to conform to group norms. Listen for statements that discourage individuals from sharing their concerns, such as, "Stop being so negative." Sixth, self-censorship. According to Janis, victims of groupthink don't want to rock the boat, so they're more likely to, quote, "keep silent about their misgivings," and even minimize to themselves the importance of their doubts. If you notice individuals aren't speaking up, you'll need to work on creating an environment
>
> **[4:43](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-creating-an-agile-culture/avoiding-groupthink-30025004?u=76281980&t=283)** where it's safe to share. Seventh, unanimous decisions. Clear-cut disagreements among the members of a group is evidence that each person is thinking critically and reality-testing their ideas. If you notice people agreeing without much pushback, that's often not a sign of an effective team. It's a signal of groupthink. Look out for comment patterns like, "Looks good to me." Eighth, mind guards. Groups that have fallen prey to groupthink will often have an individual who acts as a gatekeeper who prevents individuals from their group from interacting with members of other groups. If individual members aren't allowed to speak for themselves or listen to criticism, that's an indication that the group is susceptible to groupthink. An example might be something like, "Engineers don't have the social skills to interact with clients, so all communication has to go through this project manager." Here's the paradox. When you have a culture that's rooted in compassion, kindness, appreciation, and respect, that can make you more susceptible to conflict avoidance. Groupthink can sneak up on you. Conflict isn't always comfortable, but making harmony the goal isn't always helpful and can be downright dangerous. When it comes to complex decisions, remember, candor is just as important as comradery.
