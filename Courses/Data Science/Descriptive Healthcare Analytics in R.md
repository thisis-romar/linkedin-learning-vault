So what is risk factor surveillance? Well, it's keeping track of the rates of risk factors which are things we do or states we are in that confer risk to our health. Imagine you hear on the radio or read in the newspaper that 14.7% of Massachusetts adults smoked in 2014. Where do you think this number comes from? How do we know this? The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey, or NHANES, is conducted by the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, or CDC, which is a federal agency. In the NHANES, neighborhoods, households, and individuals are sampled and data collectors visit respondents in person using a mobile examination center, or MEC. These respondents provide answers to questionnaires but also undergo clinical measurements such as weight, height, and blood pressure. The NHANES is done every two years. Many researchers express interest in the NHANES dataset which is publicly available because they are impressed with the inclusion of clinical measurements, but there are a lot of challenges to the use

> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/descriptive-healthcare-analytics-in-r/us-risk-factors?u=76281980&t=93)** of the NHANES dataset. Next, compared to the BRFSS, there are relatively fewer records, around 10 thousand total. And these may not all be usable due to low participation rates and high refusal rates. And therefore, you have to wonder if there is selection bias at play. So as you probably guessed, I'm not a big fan of the NHANES. Even though it has clinically-measured variables, my opinion is that the complexity and bias offset that advantage. Hence, I'm a much bigger fan of the second main [[Public Health]] surveillance effort in the US, the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System, or BRFSS, which is done on a yearly cycle. You'll see the differences between BRFSS and NHANES. First, while the CDC organizes the BRFSS and provides supports to states doing the BRFSS, the [[Data Collection]] is actually run at the state level. Next, instead of running around neighborhoods in a MEC, the BRFSS does anonymous phone surveys by simply randomly generating phone numbers, calling them, and doing the survey. Admittedly, BRFSS was a little late to the gate in getting cell phone numbers into the sampling frame, but they worked very hard on it. And now their sampling appears to be fairly robust. Also, they compile the dataset annually instead of every two years like NHANES. Next, there is this concept in the BRFSS of core questions and questions from modules.
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/descriptive-healthcare-analytics-in-r/us-risk-factors?u=76281980&t=190)** Because the feds organize the BRFSS, there are certain questions they insist are asked pretty much the same way every year as these are part of the core questions every year that states are obligated to ask. For example, some states have cities with smog problems, and those states may choose to include the asthma / adult asthma history module one year. Or states experiencing a spike in racially-motivated violence might choose to include the reactions to race module that year. The answers to the core questions are available publicly in a huge dataset you can download. Some researchers don't trust the BRFSS because people are expected to self-report conditions such as having diabetes. Unlike in NHANES, there is a lack of clinical confirmation of self-reported conditions in other metrics in BRFSS. But many analyses have shown that the BRFSS is pretty accurate. Some, such as myself, believe that the reduced selection bias in a large sample associated with BRFSS helps overcome the possible misclassification problems introduced by the phone versus in-person survey and that these biases are preferable to the more severe selection bias present in the smaller NHANES sample.

> **[4:45](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/descriptive-healthcare-analytics-in-r/us-risk-factors?u=76281980&t=285)** But there is no right answer, whatever floats your boat. Unfortunately, only the results of the core questions are available publicly with documentation. If you want state module questions, you'll have to contact that state's public health department and see if they're willing to share their module data with you. So now, I'm done with our quick review of US risk factors surveillance systems: the NHANES and the BRFSS.
