> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/strategic-financial-intelligence-for-business-leaders/the-balance-sheet?u=76281980&t=1)** - The balance sheet shows the cumulative result of all prior period income statements at a particular point in time, since the business activity is always ongoing. #### [The cash flow statement](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/strategic-financial-intelligence-for-business-leaders/the-cash-flow-statement?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/strategic-financial-intelligence-for-business-leaders/the-cash-flow-statement?u=76281980&t=0)** - All right, we've made it to the cash flow statement, which is my favorite one of all. This one shows how much cash actually moved in and out of the business during the period. It separates cash activity into three areas: operating, your day-to-day activity, investing, your long-term asset transactions, and financing, debt and equity capital transactions. And here's the breakdown for you. Operating activities generate cash in from customers and out to suppliers for payroll and for day-to-day functions. Investing activities generate cash in and out for sales and purchases of long-term assets, like equipment, property, or acquisitions. And finally, financing activities generate cash in and out from borrowing, repaying debt, issuing shares, or returning capital to owners. The total cash movement across those three categories added to your starting cash balance gives you the ending cash balance, and that is your true liquidity position. The cash flow statement answers critical questions about the business. Are you funding yourself through operating cash flow, or debt? Or worse, with asset sales? Is your profitability converting into actual liquidity? Are you investing in future growth, or are you just draining cash balances to stay afloat? You can have growing profits and still run out of cash.
>
> **[1:37](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/strategic-financial-intelligence-for-business-leaders/the-cash-flow-statement?u=76281980&t=97)** It happens all the time. And that is where many leaders get caught off guard. When cashflow from operations is negative, but profit is positive, you need to know that something is off. It usually means you're growing, but you're forced to grow your working capital to fund that growth. You're building up receivables and stockpiling inventory, and all the profit is getting reinvested just to keep the wheels turning. Picture this: You report a million in profit, but you've locked up a million and a half in working capital. You're pulling from reserves, you're tapping credit lines just to stay liquid. And what happens next year? More growth demands even more working capital, but your borrowing capacity doesn't scale at the same pace. At some point, you will hit a ceiling, and that's how risk builds up. Your cash tightens, your debt increases, and your resilience disappears. And before you know it, you find yourself in survival mode. Here's the hard rule you need to know: You can't grow faster than your ability to fund that working capital. If every dollar of profit gets sucked into accounts receivable and inventory, you need to rethink the business model. That's where many leaders get caught, so don't make that same mistake. And remember, profit doesn't equal cash, and cash is what keeps you in the game. This is why financial intelligence isn't about looking back;
