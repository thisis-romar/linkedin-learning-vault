#### [Why visualize data?](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/learning-data-visualization-15572314/why-visualize-data?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/learning-data-visualization-15572314/why-visualize-data?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] There are a lot of really good reasons to visualize data, but there are three that rise above all the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md). First of all, humans are visual creatures. Literally, about half of your brain is devoted to processing the information that comes in through your eyeballs. Your eyeballs, by the way, have 70% of all the sensory receptors in your body. Humans experience the world visually, first and foremost. Second, we're visual learners. There's a well known psychological phenomenon called the Picture Superiority Effect, which has shown via a variety of experiments that humans remember pictures better than words. So if you want to teach someone something about your data and you want them to remember it, a picture will outperform a description of the data and a table of numbers. Third, we can see things in visualizations of data that we would otherwise miss. The most famous historical example of this is called Anscombe's Quartet. Francis Anscombe was an English statistician and he created these four data sets, which looked pretty similar when you look at the table of numbers. Maybe you can detect a pattern or two, but most people don't notice anything without staring at them for quite a while. If you analyze them with [Statistics](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Statistics.md), you find that they are in fact pretty much identical. They all have the same average of both X and Y values, the same variance of X and Y, the same regression, the same correlation, and the same correlation coefficient. They're the same, except that they're not, they're actually very different.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/learning-data-visualization-15572314/why-visualize-data?u=76281980&t=94)** Only the visual reveals this, so we visualize our data because without the visual we might misunderstand our data. It's not just window dressing, it's not just making it pretty, it's not just giving busy executives a fast route to enable decision making, visualization does this too, but it's critical, because without visuals you just might be wrong about what your data even means. We'll talk about some more of the advantages of visualization throughout this course, but these three stand on their own to make the case that really you must visualize your data to most effectively reach your audience, and when you're analyzing the data in the first place.

You're very likely not including every single bit of detail in your data set in your visualization. This is about the nature of the numbers themselves, which falls in the category of [Data Literacy](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data%20Literacy.md). And then when you visualize it, think about how relatable your visuals are. Using a football stadium to explain a hundred thousand people should be relatable for some.
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/learning-data-visualization-15572314/making-numbers-relatable?u=76281980&t=188)** Using 63 football stadiums may make it hard to really understand exactly what 6.3 million looks like but just the sheer magnitude and overwhelming nature of it might still get across the point in this case. Make your numbers relatable and then visualize them in a relatable way. If you don't, then your numbers can lose all meaning which wouldn't be very good for data visualization that's meant to mean something.
