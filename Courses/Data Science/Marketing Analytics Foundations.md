#### [Selecting the most relevant metrics for your organization](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/marketing-analytics-foundations/selecting-the-most-relevant-metrics-for-your-organization?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/marketing-analytics-foundations/selecting-the-most-relevant-metrics-for-your-organization?u=76281980&t=1)** - Click through rates, open rates, reach, conversions, qualified leads, shares, reactions, visitors. Seems like there are endless metrics to measure. Marketers often struggle with identifying what to focus on. The good news is that we can set guidelines for what metrics are really important. The key is to use metrics that indicate whether you're really making progress on these big goals. These are the metrics that are KPIs, key performance indicators. For a key performance indicator to be effective, it must measure significant outcomes that matter to an organization. But there's more that makes a metric a KPI and not just another number. Here are five ways to tell if a metric is a potential KPI. First, it tracks performance, not status or activity. You want to track what your marketing is accomplishing, not just how popular your message is. For example, let's say you're running two ad campaigns for a new ice cream chain. One gets lots of likes and comments while the other gets more clicks resulting in web traffic, including visits to your hours and locations page. Which do you think is the more successful ad? It's probably the one that drove tangible results. Second, it focuses on the most integral kinds of performance. Not all [Performance Metrics](../../Skills/Cybersecurity/Performance%20Metrics.md) are equal. Let's go back to our ice cream store example. While overall traffic, for instance,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/marketing-analytics-foundations/selecting-the-most-relevant-metrics-for-your-organization?u=76281980&t=94)** is an indicator of performance, other metrics might be even stronger indicators that an ad is effective. For example, one ad might result in more email signups or bring more repeat customers to the company. This is stronger performance than simple web traffic, since it more clearly leads to revenue and growth. Third, it's actionable within existing resources and workflows. You also need to be able to use metrics to improve your marketing. That means that metrics need to be actionable. They need to not only show what is working, but also show us how to do more of what works. For instance, we can run A/B tests pinpointing the types of creative that convert audiences and what factors lead to conversions. Then we can actually act on the data to design the right ads. Fourth, it's reliable and measurable over time. A metric has to be consistent from period to period. For example, let's say a company is trying out two different email subject lines for their newsletter. The first shows significant lift in opens and click through rates. Great. However, when they try the same thing again next month with a similar subject line, open rates are back down. What went wrong? Maybe nothing. We need to keep measuring the same data consistently so that we can see what is a pattern. And finally, and most importantly,
>
> **[3:07](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/marketing-analytics-foundations/selecting-the-most-relevant-metrics-for-your-organization?u=76281980&t=187)** a KPI needs to align with the overall goals of an organization. This means that it should directly contribute to the growth and success of the company. By considering factors, such as performance tracking, focusing on integral types of performance, actionable metrics, feasibility, reliability, and alignment with organizational objectives, a company can identify the most effective KPIs to track and use them to guide their marketing efforts towards success.

> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/marketing-analytics-foundations/analyzing-effective-kpis-examples?u=76281980&t=1)** - Let's discuss some examples of KPIs that help drive greater success through marketing. We'll start with a brief overview of three key marketing KPIs. Number one is customer acquisition cost, or CAC. Number two is return on advertising spend, or ROAS. And the third one is conversion rate. Let's start with customer acquisition cost, or CAC. This measures the cost of acquiring a new customer. It includes all marketing expenses divided by the number of new customers acquired during a specific period. For instance, if you spent $100,000 marketing your business during the year and acquired 1,000 new customers over that year, your CAC would be 100,000 divided by 1,000, or $100. Understanding CAC helps you evaluate the efficiency and ROI of your customer acquisition strategies. When PayPal implemented a basic referral program where existing customers were rewarded for referring new customers, they became the big company they are today. Their referral strategy led to 10% growth per day. Their cost per acquisition fell to a little over $5 per new customer. By leveraging their existing user base, they sustainably grew their customer base at a minimal cost. Next is return on advertising spend, or ROAS.

With your data and communication gaps identified and your tool set evaluated, it's time to come up with a timeline, budget, and resources to bridge the gaps. Plan how long it will take to start collecting all the data you need. Assign staff to oversee onboarding technologies, setting up reporting, and communicating with your team. Schedule a check-in to assess your new data and see how reporting is working. Concurrently, design new dashboards to integrate the new data for decision making and awareness. Having the data you need is only half the challenge, however. You also need to build a data-driven culture where team members understand the data they can access, how to interpret it, and how to make decisions based on data. A marketing analytics plan needs training to ensure that team members put new metrics and technologies to use. Make them aware of the data specifically relevant to their work. Ensure that dashboards take user needs into account. Measuring marketing is a lifelong endeavor. Companies need to see analytics not just as something to do when marketing needs support, but as a regular part of marketing operations. Implementing a robust marketing analytics plan ensures that everyone on your team integrates analytics
