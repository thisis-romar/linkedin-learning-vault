So, two great facts to share, Humans can only hold around four items in short-term memory for 20 to 30 seconds. This is scientifically tested psychology. So a chart with dozens of labels and callouts and details is really not useful from a presentation standpoint. One of the most common reasons we forget things is because of something called interference. The competition between different ideas struggling to be captured in our brains. A busy chart creates more interference, reducing understanding and memory. Now, another approach is you can always blame kids these days. Meaning you can point out that as time rolls on where more and more distracted by data, by demands on our time, by our electronic devices, by advertisers, people are exposed to 15.5 hours of consumable media per day. You want to show one thing in your chart, then add more pages to your presentation to layer more details on top of that. It's also proven by all kinds of research that humans are wired for visual communication. A million text labels and call outs and a lack of whitespace isn't just ugly, it's bad communications. This is all heavily researched and backed by brain science and studies galore. There's a reason that books have margins, and that newspaper visuals are extremely simple, and that USA today began printing simple charts and graphs back in the '80s. We're wired to see data, not to read it, so a simpler graph with less detail will do a better job communicating than a table full of numbers, or even a heavily labeled chart. And never forget to keep pushing to force answers to the KWYs. What are you really trying to say? What is the data saying and what does your audience need to hear? Your boss or client is always trying to say something. Even though they may not consciously feel that way

You can usually whittle just about anything down to one key idea or point. And don't forget to offer to do more than one version, one for the leave-behind and one for the presentation. If and when you lose this argument, and you will, produce a version of the chart that you think is right and show them anyway. Do it on your own time. No boss or client likes to be ignored.
