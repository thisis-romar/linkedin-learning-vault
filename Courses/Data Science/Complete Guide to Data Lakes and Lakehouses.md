But what exactly is a data lake? A data lake is a centralized repository that allows you to store all of your structured and [Unstructured Data](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Unstructured%20Data.md) at any scale. That means however much data you have, whether it is gigabytes or petabytes, you can add it all to the centralized repository. In a data lake, you can store data as is without having to first structure it and run different types of analytics, from [Dashboards](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Dashboards.md) and visualizations to [Real-Time](../../Skills/Database%20Management/Real-Time.md) analytics, machine learning, [artificial intelligence](../../Topics/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI).md), and more. Now, let's take a look at the specific key characteristics of a data lake. First is [Scalability](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Scalability.md). [Data Lakes](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data%20Lakes.md) can store petabytes of data and scale as needed without significant performance degradation. This capability is essential in today's data-driven world, where the volume of data we generate and analyze continues to grow. Next is cost-effectiveness. Data lakes often use object storage that costs less than traditional storage, making it a cost-effective solution for storing huge amounts of data. Also, flexibility. Unlike traditional [Databases](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Databases.md) that require data to be formatted and structured in a predefined way, data lakes can hold data in diverse formats, including logs, XML, [JSON](../../Skills/Web%20Development/JSON.md), images, and more. > **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/complete-guide-to-data-lakes-and-lakehouses/origins-and-evolution-24326594?u=76281980&t=0)** - Imagine stepping back in time to the early days of the internet, a world blooming with data, but confined by the limited storage and processing power of traditional systems. How did we transition from there to today's sophisticated [Data Lakes](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data%20Lakes.md)? In the late 1990s and early 2000s, the digital universe began to expand at an unprecedented rate. With the rise of the internet, [E-Commerce](../../Skills/Web%20Development/E-Commerce.md), and social media, traditional [Data Storage](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data%20Storage.md) and processing systems were overwhelmed. The need for a technology that could handle not only the volume, but also the variety and velocity of data became obvious, but there were significant challenges behind it. Traditional [Databases](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Databases.md) and data warehouses were structured for consistent, predictable loads of structured data, things like customer names and contact information, financial transactions, and stock information, et cetera. However, the new types of data, like emails, videos, social media posts, sensor data, et cetera were unstructured and didn't really fit into rows and columns. We needed a solution that was [agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md) and diverse as the data itself. Enter Apache [Hadoop](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Hadoop.md). Inspired by the [Google](../../Glossary/Service/Google.md) MapReduce and Google File System papers, Hadoop was designed to store and process huge amounts of data across clusters of commodity servers. This was a conceptual beginning of what we will soon call a data lake. The term data lake itself was coined by James Dixon, CTO of Pentaho, around 2010. He used it to describe a large repository of raw data held in its native format until it was needed. The evolution of data lakes is also closely tied to the rapid advancement of [Cloud Computing](../../Topics/Cloud%20Computing.md). As [Cloud Services](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Cloud%20Services.md) like [Amazon Web Services](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Amazon%20Web%20Services%20(AWS).md), [Microsoft Azure](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Microsoft%20Azure.md), and [Google Cloud](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Google%20Cloud%20Platform%20(GCP).md) became more robust, they provided the perfect ecosystem for data lakes. These platforms offer scalable, secure, and cost-effective storage solutions, allowing companies and teams of all sizes to set up data lakes without the need for expensive on-premise infrastructure. The concept of data democratization, making data accessible to everyone without gatekeepers, is another key factor in the rise of data lakes. By allowing data to be stored in a central location that is accessible to different stakeholders within a company, data lakes enable a collaborative environment where insights and [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md)
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/complete-guide-to-data-lakes-and-lakehouses/origins-and-evolution-24326594?u=76281980&t=190)** are available across departments. So there you have it, a little bit of history to understand how we arrived here and maybe even think about what the future may hold.

#### [Architecture core components](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/complete-guide-to-data-lakes-and-lakehouses/architecture-core-components-24327500?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/complete-guide-to-data-lakes-and-lakehouses/architecture-core-components-24327500?u=76281980&t=0)** - Ever wonder what lies beneath the surface of a data lake? Let's take a high level overview to their main architectural components, or layers, and explore their main characteristics. These ledges go from the least actionable at the bottom to the most actionable at the top. This is so important that I actually have structured the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) of the course to go in this order. The storage layer is a foundation of a data lake. It is designed to hold an enormous amount of raw data in various formats, from structured to unstructured. This layer must be highly scalable to accommodate growth in data volume, without degrading performance. There are three main characteristics that a storage layer should have. It should be able to expand easily to storage mass data as you need, and that could be petabytes or even exabytes of data. It should be optimized to keep the cost manageable, especially important as data volumes grow. And lastly, it should be able to support multiple data formats and structures. Next, the ingestion layer handles the intake of data from various sources, whether it is streaming real time or in batches. It's fundamental for preparing and moving data into the lake in a consistent and reliable manner. What are they and how are they implemented? ACID is an acronym that stands for atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability. It describes a set of properties that guarantee database transactions are processed reliably, despite errors, power failures, or other unexpected conditions. Here is a closer look at each one of these properties. Atomicity guarantees that each transaction is treated as a single unit, which is either fully completed or not executed at all. If any part of the transaction fails, then the entire transaction fails and the database state is left unchanged. This property is important for preventing partial data operations that could lead to data corruption. So how is atomicity implemented? Transaction management systems are one of the options for implementing atomicity. These systems manage the state of transactions, making sure that they are complete fully, or revert back to their initial state in case of failure. These systems sometimes use logging and rollback, which means every transaction is logged before execution. If a transaction is interrupted, the system uses log to undo any changes reverting to the original state. Consistency guarantees that every transaction brings a database from one valid state to another,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/complete-guide-to-data-lakes-and-lakehouses/acid-transactions-24323780?u=76281980&t=94)** maintaining all predefined rules and constraints. For example, in a sales data set, this would mean making sure each sale is associated with a valid product and customer. Some of the ways consistency is implemented are using constraints like foreign keys or unique keys that are strictly enforced during transaction and implementing [Data Validation](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Data%20Validation.md) rules, defined according to business logic to make sure that all transformations meet specific criteria before being committed. Isolation provides each transaction with the illusion that it is the only operation being executed in the system.
