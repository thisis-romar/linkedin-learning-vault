So keep things accurate, but don't let perfect get in the way of helpful.

#### [Admit what you don't know](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/lessons-from-data-scientists-27890200/admit-what-you-don-t-know?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/lessons-from-data-scientists-27890200/admit-what-you-don-t-know?u=76281980&t=0)** Ever had someone nod along in a meeting like they understood everywhere you were saying, but you know they had no idea what was going on? If your audience is non-technical, your message needs to be non-technical. Decision makers don't want code, they need context. Your job as a data scientist is to adapt your message to your audience's level of technical fluency. Because you're not a gatekeeper, you're the bridge between data and action. A lot of data scientists revert to jargon because they think it makes them sound smart, or that it makes their work sound more impressive. But that only really creates confusion. Jargon doesn't make your work more impressive. In fact, it's the opposite. Jargon makes your work harder to use, and if people don't understand your work, they'll dismiss it out of fear, frustration, or uncertainty. I’ve learned the hard way to never assume that somebody knows the terms “log loss” or “feature importance.” When I talk to stakeholders now, I reframe everything in terms of impact. “Here's what I found. Here's why it matters. And here's what I would recommend.” Even simple [word](../../Skills/Cloud%20Computing/Microsoft%20Word.md) swaps make a huge difference. Like saying, “We predict this group is likely to churn” instead of “The model output a [Probability](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Probability.md) over 0.75.” This lands better and actually drives action. So before your next meeting, ask yourself, “What does this person need to know to actually make a decision?” Then practice explaining your work in two ways: one to a technical coworker and the other to a VP who hasn't touched code in, say, 15 years. If your message leads to clarity and competence, not confusion, you've done your job.

They have systems that make them faster and more reliable. So stop relying on your memory. Start building systems for yourself now.
