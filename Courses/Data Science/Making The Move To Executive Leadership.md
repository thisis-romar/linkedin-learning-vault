"It means saying no to the hundreds of other good ideas." Executives must scale to lead hundreds or thousands of employees while staying in touch with the important details of the work in their teams. There's a seeming contradiction between high level strategic direction setting and being down in the details of dozens of teams. The executive role is to get the right things done quickly and well, not to get everything done. At Amazon, I managed 800 people, far too many to know personally. Yet the leaders above me expected me to know the critical details of their work. The only way this is possible is by training your leaders to recognize and funnel the information you need into your hands. To scale, you must develop the individuals and leaders under you to work with you to rapidly move information up, down, and across your organization. I explained to my team members that I was expected to know the key details of features, timelines, problems, and budgets for each of their areas. They needed to provide that information to me,
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/scaled-deep-leadership?u=76281980&t=94)** both as a weekly status and as an immediate update if something major went wrong. Once you've taught your team to always bring key information to you, the next step is to know when to audit personally. There's a time to dive in yourself, and it's when the information you're getting indicates that there's a problem in an area of your team that's not getting solved. At that point, you dive in deeply, gather the team, and use your greater experience, personal skills and positional resources to resolve the problem. Then you extract yourself and go back to strategic work. The final step is prioritization. As Jobs said, "Focus is about saying no, "as well as saying yes." Your job is to get the most valuable high impact work done. This means identifying what is the most important, and then having the courage to say no to other work. Since what you say no to will still be good work, it will have advocates and saying no to it will both disappoint people and have a business cost. As a leader, though, there is no reward for getting many projects partly finished. It's better to get your key projects completely done,

Now, this behavior can become a trap. If you jump in to fix it yourself, you limit your ability to scale across many large teams. You also teach your leaders to depend on you for rescue rather than figuring out their own issues. Though it's difficult, you have to reserve your personal engagement for rare critical situations. To learn more about prioritization, I recommend the book, "Eat That Frog" by Brian Tracy. This book led to a huge improvement in my executive career by getting me to identify and complete my most important high impact tasks each day.

#### [Invention](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/invention?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/invention?u=76281980&t=0)** - While it's important to be an inventive leader yourself, you must teach your entire team to find creative solutions to customer needs. Once you master the three-step process I've explained to unlock your own inner inventor, you can teach this method to your team members as well. However, there's more you need to do in order to get your team really engaged in both adapting to surprising changes and recognizing new opportunities. First, you must make invention part of their jobs. In many companies, teams are too busy working on the old way, to stop and think about a new approach. Toyota famously addressed this problem in two ways. They created the Andon Cord, which any employee could pull when they saw a problem. This cord would stop the assembly line and bring managers and team members rushing to the site of the problem to fix it. The Andon Cord approach created a highly visible culture of addressing problems when they're seen. As a result, Toyota vehicles enjoy a worldwide reputation for quality. Second, Toyota harnessed the Japanese idea of Kaizens, which translates roughly as improvements. Each employee was expected to come up with two Kaizen ideas for the company every month, and given the time to do so.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/invention?u=76281980&t=94)** Your team can only invent for you if you both set the expectation that this is part of their job, and give them the time to think. A high-tech example of this approach is the famous 20% time policy at [[Google]] where employees can spend up to 20% of their work time on a project of their own choosing. Perhaps you can't grant your team a day every week to work on whatever they want. There are many other ways to demonstrate support for creativity and innovation. You can hold periodic hackathons or other invention contests. You can solicit new ideas as a part of your annual planning cycle and help employees propose them for management support. You can create a culture of moving quickly on reversible projects, as we discussed with your own decision making. allow your team members to experiment with changes that can be undone if they don't have good outcomes. Not all employees will embrace invention, but if you do these things, you'll be flooded with more suggestions than you can handle. The last important step is to tell employees that there will only be time and resources for the best ideas. At the same time, set their expectations that a good idea is only the beginning, and that turning it into a working result should be their ultimate goal.

#### [Trusted decision maker](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/trusted-decision-maker?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/trusted-decision-maker?u=76281980&t=1)** - When I was a director at Amazon, I owned and built our first app store. Our CEO, Jeff Bezos, was personally interested in the product launch. When the product failed under load on release, he lost faith in my judgment. Trust is built by consistent performance over time, and it took me two years to restore that trust before I was promoted to vice president. Trust is essential to land an executive role because no higher leader can afford the time to second guess everything you are doing. Your influence with other executives is also built on trust. Simply explained, trust comes from making wise choices and following through until leaders come to rely on you. Executives make high-dollar strategic decisions all the time. While experience helps us make good decisions, there is also a simple process to follow when you're faced with a new challenge. Step one is to widen your lens and gather all the options. Don't jump to the first possible choice, but rather ask, "What else could work here?" This is the time to get input from a wide range of experts and advisors on possibilities. Step two is then to evaluate your options and narrow them down. Assign your team to do research, gather data,
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/trusted-decision-maker?u=76281980&t=95)** and have your strongest team members help you evaluate all the information. Step three is where you make the choice. This is where many executives stumble, delaying or avoiding a decision for fear of being wrong. While it is important to be right most of the time, the pace of business keeps accelerating. If you wait too long, a competitor will most likely rush past you and be first. One key idea is to consider if a decision is reversible. Many decisions can be changed or modified after you learn more. Building software is one example where features can be added, changed, or removed along the way. By comparison, letting go of a key employee or supplier is much harder to undo. If a decision is easily reversed or modified, you can make it sooner with less information and learn as you go. This allows you to move faster. Most decisions are actually reversible, particularly with planning. For example, you can try a new product, either in a small market or by building a prototype before investing in the final version. Only when a decision has no options to test the outcome or change course does a good executive slow down
>
> **[3:13](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/trusted-decision-maker?u=76281980&t=193)** and make a very careful choice because it must be right the first time. Step four is where you evaluate and adjust. Once you make the decision and start acting, you will start learning. A common mistake is to stick to a plan even in the face of obvious problems. Choose the metrics you will use to judge your progress when you will assess the data and adjust course. Establishing these measurements and dates upfront will protect you from getting sucked into execution and missing the big picture. By following these steps, you can make good decisions quickly, earning trust while you drive forward. Finally, I want to share my number one book recommendation, "Decisive" by Chip and Dan Heath. This book is an easy read that will help you make better decisions in both your professional and personal life.

#### [How to: Build and demonstrate executive presence](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-build-and-demonstrate-executive-presence?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-build-and-demonstrate-executive-presence?u=76281980&t=0)** - I want to share a story about my client, Sam. Sam worked hard and knew his material, but in big meetings, he rarely jumped into the conversation, either speaking later or not at all. When put on the spot with a tough question, he would either ramble or sound uncertain in his responses. Sam and his team delivered their projects, and his team members liked him. He got good ratings every year but was never promoted past senior manager. Sam lacked executive presence. He couldn't command a room, hold attention, and appear to be a decisive leader who is quick on his feet. Aspiring executives are often told that they lack executive presence, but then given no specific definition of what this means to the reviewer. In this video, we will define executive presence, discuss how to pinpoint where to improve, and give specific techniques to do so. Executive presence is tough for leaders to define because it's actually composed of many skills. Often, people think of public speaking, a leader giving a rousing speech in front of a crowd, but there's much more. Author Sylvia Ann Hewlett breaks down executive presence into three categories,
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-build-and-demonstrate-executive-presence?u=76281980&t=93)** defining it as a mix of 60% gravitas, 30% communication, and 10% appearance. Gravitas is composed of the things you do that make you a serious leader worthy of attention and respect. The two dominant traits are confidence and decisiveness. People follow leaders who seem composed and sure of themselves. This includes remaining calm in a crisis or answering tough questions in a meeting. Communication skills are often the first thing people think of but are less important than gravitas. This category includes public speaking as well as your ability to hold attention and manage a room, read an audience, and present your authentic self. Least important is appearance, but it does matter in two key ways. People form their first impression based on your appearance. Luckily, appearance is not about being attractive but rather showing attention to your dress and grooming. It shows professionalism and gives you a chance to display your gravitas and communication skills. To improve your executive presence, you must first identify which specific pieces you need to hone, then make a plan to learn about them
>
> **[3:08](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-build-and-demonstrate-executive-presence?u=76281980&t=188)** and practice those skills. Here are a few areas to consider. For public speaking, find small, safe audiences to practice on or join a Toastmasters club. To learn to read a room, partner with a friend and discuss what each of you saw in the meeting after the fact. For displaying calm and practicing emotional control, try meditation or building other emotional intelligence skills to allow you to remain calm in crisis situations. To increase your influence, read "How to Win Friends and Influence People" and then also prepare your arguments in advance rather than on the fly. Finally, for appearance, consider working with a professional stylist, like a Nordstrom personal shopper, to define and sharpen your personal style. To go deeper, there are two good books, both called "Executive Presence." One is by Sylvia Ann Hewlett and is particularly good for women and those who'd like to overcome an accent. The second is by Harrison Monarth and has chapters on each component of executive presence, making it easy to focus on the skill you want to target.

>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-know-where-you-stand?u=76281980&t=1)** - Many leaders are working hard and getting good reviews, but are not advancing in their careers. They are stuck and don't understand why. To hone top performance, you must seek out feedback using a wide range of methods. Once you know what the organization is rewarding and how you are viewed, you can take action and move up. There are four main ways to get feedback and know where you stand. The first is to ask your manager, but there's a right way to do it. Whenever you ask someone for feedback, it is best to give them warning a few days ahead so that you get their real thoughts, not whatever pops into their head at the moment. Tell them that you would like to get feedback in your next meeting, and that you are telling them now so that they have time to think about it. At the meeting, be sure to say that you would like to understand what you're doing well along with where you can grow or improve. Most people are promoted for their strengths, not their absence of weaknesses. So learning what your manager values is as important as what you need to change. Always say thank you for feedback, even if you disagree. If you argue, it will teach people to just not tell you what they believe.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-know-where-you-stand?u=76281980&t=94)** Instead, if you disagree, ask for specific examples so that you can better understand, and then ask what they wanted instead so that you are clear on how they define good performance. If your manager only says, "You're doing great," thank them, but then ask for specifics, explaining that you need details in order to improve. The second way to get feedback is from your and your manager's peers. 360-degree feedback gives you a full picture of how you're doing. While your manager is the most important, corporate promotions have input from many people, and your manager can also have blind spots. If you disagree with feedback from your manager, but you hear the same thing from a few peers, it indicates that you would better take it seriously. Use the same process of telling people in advance you want feedback, saying thank you, and asking for examples in all cases. The third way to know where you stand is to observe others. Who is succeeding in your organization? What are they doing to get that success? Who is struggling and not progressing? Why? By observing those around you, it becomes easier

> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-know-where-you-stand?u=76281980&t=189)** to see what your manager and other leaders value. Are you getting the budget you need and a good share of any increase in headcount? Are you getting training and travel opportunities? If not, who is and why? Finally, if you struggle talking about sensitive topics with your manager, the book "Radical Candor" by Kim Scott or "Crucial Conversations" by Joseph Grenney and others can really help you.

#### [How to: Work around poor managers](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-work-around-poor-managers?u=76281980)

> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-work-around-poor-managers?u=76281980&t=1)** - It's really hard to get promoted without support from your direct manager. Companies trust their managers and if the person who knows you best doesn't support your promotion, it usually doesn't happen, but managers leave, reorgs happen. New managers don't know you and lack the experience to advocate for you. At Amazon, after hundreds of thousands of reviews, we had the data to know that having more than one manager during a year lowered the average performance review for those employees. You may also have a poor manager, one who doesn't care about your development, who's too busy to invest in you or just lacks the knowledge to navigate the promotion process. The best way to avoid these problems is to try to find a stable veteran manager when you are nearing promotion. Veterans at the company know the system and have already shown stability at the company. While they may leave, your odds are better. Given that you need manager support, here is how to build it quickly and how to mitigate the damage if your manager does leave. First, you must be a solid performer. Establish this by directly asking your manager where you stand. Seek feedback from your manager and key stakeholders. Then address any big gaps.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/making-the-move-to-executive-leadership/how-to-work-around-poor-managers?u=76281980&t=96)** If your manager gives little feedback, try observing others. Who is really succeeding in your team or working well with your manager? What are they doing that you can learn from? Get what support you can from a poor manager. Then reach out to your skip level manager, your manager's peers, and any mentors to form a support network. Such a network minimizes your dependence on your manager to be a strong advocate for you. If you have a bad manager who can't get you promoted or seems likely to leave, it may make sense to change jobs yourself. Just be careful to honestly consider if you have performance issues of your own. If you do, it's likely that they'll be noticed in the new company and hold you back there as well. Finally, most promotion documents require a number of higher level peers to give feedback. I've seen many promotions derailed by the unexpected departure of an essential feedback provider. Given this risk, invest in cultivating extra potential supporters. In a large group of feedback providers, some turnover is almost certain.
