#### [Use the first hour for focus and impact](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/use-the-first-hour-for-focus-and-impact?u=76281980)

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/use-the-first-hour-for-focus-and-impact?u=76281980&t=0)** What you do in your first hour doesn't just set the tone. It shapes what actually gets done. Most new data scientists start the day by checking [Slack](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Slack.md), scrolling through emails, and getting pulled into threads. Suddenly, it's noon and you haven't touched the real work. Here's a better approach: block the first hour for thinking and not reacting. Before opening your inbox, look at your task list and ask yourself, “What's the one thing that I need to do to move forward today? What is urgent versus what’s just noise? And what do I need to input before I can proceed?” Write it down. Not in your head-- not scattered across stamps-- but one clear list. This reduces context switching and helps you focus before everyone else priorities become yours. And here's a real example. One analyst I worked with spent 15 minutes every morning reviewing her active projects and jotting down one goal per project. Just one. She never looked busy, but she always delivered what mattered. And that habit made her the person leadership trusted when it counted. A pro tip is keeping a “waiting on” list. If you’re blocked, track who or what you're waiting for. It keeps you proactive instead of stuck in a limbo. So the first hour is not about getting everything done. It's about deciding what's worth doing today and starting with that.

#### [Prioritize without overloading your day](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/prioritize-without-overloading-your-day?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/prioritize-without-overloading-your-day?u=76281980&t=0)** A long to-do list feels productive until it isn't. When everything is important, nothing moves. The real skill for a data scientist is knowing what not to work on today. The first group is tasks that drive decisions or unblock others, and the second group is tasks that feel productive but don't create momentum. For example, tweaking a dashboard layout might be satisfying, but if a stakeholder is waiting on insights to make a pricing code, that's the higher value task. Don't confuse effort with impact. Each morning, pick one to three things that actually matter. That's your focus list. Everything else goes on a secondary list: Visible but not allowed to hijack your day. Here's a trick that helps-- write your task as outcomes, not activities. Instead of saying “Pull user data,” write “Identify top user segments for the Q1 campaign.” It keeps your goal clear and makes it easier to know when you're done. And one more thing-- protect your focus list from last-minute requests. If something new comes in, ask yourself, “Does this replace something on today's list or can it wait?” If you're always shifting, you're not prioritizing. You are literally reacting only. Clarity beats volume here, a short, focused plan that drives progress will always beat a packed list that goes nowhere.

#### [Adapt to delays and disruptions](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/adapt-to-delays-and-disruptions?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/adapt-to-delays-and-disruptions?u=76281980&t=0)** Disruptions are not the exceptions. They are the job. In a data role, you will depend on input from others, review cycles, approvals, data access-- and those timelines will shift. If you don't plan for that, you will constantly feel behind. The key here is to design your day with buffers. Don't block out eight hours assuming nothing will move. Instead, leave room between tasks and expect that some of them will stall. Use a two-track system of one track for deep work that requires focus and another for shallow task-- quick updates, follow-ups, cleaning datasets, or reviewing scripts. That way, when you're blocked on the big stuff, you’ve got smaller pieces ready to go. And here's an example: one analyst may keep a separate list of five-minute tasks-- things like checking query results, replying to open questions, and every time a meeting ran late or a dataset wasn't ready, this person dipped into the list and it kept the product. And this keeps you productive without switching to busywork. Another tip is document what you're waiting on. Literally write, you know, “Waiting for review from Sarah” or “Access pending from IT.” It reduces mental load and gives you a clear way to follow up without losing track. Being flexible doesn't mean being passive. You can plan for uncertainty, just don't overbook yourself like everything will go perfectly. Resilience is not about staying on schedule. It's about staying useful even when the schedule breaks.

#### [Conduct exploratory data analysis](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/conduct-exploratory-data-analysis?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/conduct-exploratory-data-analysis?u=76281980&t=0)** When I first started doing exploratory analysis, I thought my job was to confirm what my team already believed. Pull some charts, run a few summaries, and make the data say what they expected. I was definitely wrong. Exploratory is not about proving assumptions-- it's about testing them. And sometimes it’s about challenging them completely. The first step is to stop looking for answers and start looking for patterns. What is queued? What's missing? What looks too clean? I always start with distributions. Before touching averages or correlations, I check how the data’s shaped. You would be surprised how often the mean hides outliers or weird groupings that completely change the story. Then I ask simple, pointed questions: who are the edge cases and why? Are we segmenting by the right things? And does the data match what the team thinks is happening? One of the most useful habits I've built is writing down observations before trying to explain them. That gives me the room to explore without forcing a narrative too early. Exploration should feel a bit messy. That's how you know you're actually thinking and not just confirming. If you already know what you're going to say before you open the notebook, you are not doing EDA-- you are doing reporting. So the point here is to treat exploration as its own phase with its own value.

#### [Evaluate data bias before conclusions](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/evaluate-data-bias-before-conclusions?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/evaluate-data-bias-before-conclusions?u=76281980&t=0)** Some of the most confident presentations I've seen were built on completely flawed data, not because the person wasn’t smart, but because they trusted the dataset without questioning it. Before you draw conclusions, ask “What might this data be hiding?” Bias shows up in a lot of ways: missing values, sampling issues, or just poorly defined metrics. And if you're not looking for it, you will miss it. One classic example is a Simpson paradox. It's when a trend appears in individual groups but reverses when you combine the data. And here's a practical example. So you're analyzing two marketing campaigns: campaign A and campaign B. You see that A has a high conversion rate overall. So you recommend scaling it. But when you're breaking it down by channel, let's say mobile and desktop, you find that B actually performs better in both channels. So what has happened? Campaign A had more mobile traffic, which converts lower overall. When grouped together, it looks better, but that just because how the data is distributed. If you had skipped the breakdown, you would have pushed for the wrong campaign and stakeholders would have acted on a flawed recommendation. And this is why I always ask, you know, “What segments might be making the true pattern? Are we combining things that shouldn't be grouped?” A good analysis starts with good data awareness. If the foundation is shaky, nothing built on top will hold up. So check the bias. It's not just a quality check. It's a trust check.

#### [Recognize data limits and insights](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/recognize-data-limits-and-insights?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/recognize-data-limits-and-insights?u=76281980&t=0)** No dataset tells you the full story. The danger comes when we act like it does. It's easy to forget what's not in the table: missing columns unchecked behaviors, outdated definitions. But decisions get made based on what we show. So if there are gaps, they need to be called out clearly. And here's a practical example. Let's say you're analyzing customer churn. And you notice that churn is highest among users who haven’t logged in for 30 days. It seems obvious: Log-ins drop and so churn goes up. But then someone ask, “Why did they stop logging in?” If your dataset only includes product usage and ignores some support tickets, some pricing changes, or external factors like a competitor new offer, you don't actually have the full picture. You can describe what's happening, but not fully explain why. That's not a failure. It just means you need to be clear about the limits of your data. When I'm writing up results, I often include a simple line. “This analysis is based on X and does not account for Y. These results should be interpreted with that in mind.” Stakeholders appreciate it. It doesn't make you look unsure. It makes you look honest. The best analysis don't overstate what the data shows. They recognize its blind spots and help the team make better decision anyway.

#### [Decide when analysis is ready](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/decide-when-analysis-is-ready?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/decide-when-analysis-is-ready?u=76281980&t=0)** The hardest part of any analysis is not starting: It's knowing when to stop. There's always another chart to tweak, another filter to test, or another edge cases to chase. But analysis is a research paper. It’s a tool for making a decision, and decision need to happen on time. I used to keep polishing work until I felt it was airtight. Then I would send it, only to realize the team had already moved on or made a call without it. Here is how I decide now. If the analysis answers the core question clearly, highlights any major caveats, and is actionable within the timeline, then it's ready. That doesn't mean it's perfect. It means it's fit for purpose. So ask yourself, you know, “Is this good enough to help someone make a decision today?” Or, “Are the limitations clear and documented?” “Would extra polish meaningfully change the outcome?” If the answer to the last one is no, then just ship it. And here's another quick example: I once spent hours fine-tuning a model that predicted customer upgrades, only to find that the marketing team just needed a simple list of accounts to target that week. They didn't need probabilities, they needed a direction. And so your job is not to impress with that-- it’s to deliver value when it's needed. The right timing beats extra polish every single time.

#### [Translate data into clear decisions](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/translate-data-into-clear-decisions?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/translate-data-into-clear-decisions?u=76281980&t=0)** The value of your work as a data scientist is not in the analysis itself-- it’s what someone does with it. Stakeholders don't need to hear about r-square values, query performance, or how many nodes you handled. They need to know, “What does this mean for us? What should we do next?” Your job is to bridge that gap. Here's a simple structure I use. What you found, then why it matters, and what to do about it. For example, instead of saying, “The model shows a 0.67 correlation between feature X and conversion,” you can say something, “Users who complete the profile within the first day are twice as likely to convert, and so prioritizing on-boarding could increase revenue.” It's the same sort of data but different impact. And another tip. Avoid edge language when it's not needed. If your insight is strong, say it plainly. Don’t bury it in “might,” “could I suggest,” unless the uncertainty is significant and relevant. I've learned that stakeholders don't want all the complexity. They want your judgment, so they're trusting you to pull signal from noise and give them a clear path forward. Keep a version of your findings that's technical for you and your team, but when presenting, focus on clarity and not detail. A good insight doesn't need to sound smart-- it needs to be useful.

#### [Craft a narrative for understanding](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/craft-a-narrative-for-understanding?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/craft-a-narrative-for-understanding?u=76281980&t=0)** A table of numbers won’t change anyone's mind, but a clear story might. As a data scientist, you can have the right analysis, solid findings, and a smart recommendation and still lose your audience if there is no structure. People don't remember charts. They remember what changed, why it matters, and what comes next. So that's the core of any good data narrative: contrast, urgency, and resolution. So you start with contrast. So what is different than expected? What changed over time? What surprised you? And that's what grabs attention. Then create urgency. Why does this matter now? Is there something at risk? Is there a window of opportunity? Show why the insight deserves action and not just curiosity. Finally, offer a resolution. What's the decision? What action should follow? Don't just highlight a problem-- show a clear path forward. And here's a quick example. Instead of walking through ten slides of tracking trends, lead with something like, you know, “Site visits dropped 25% after we changed the home page layout. The biggest losses were on mobile, and bounce rates spiked. Reverting to the previous design could recover traffic within days.” That's a story with contrast, urgency, and a clear resolution. What doesn't work well-- presenting everything in order of analysis or worse, dumping it all and expecting others to draw conclusions. That puts the burden on your audience and risks your work falling flat. You have to structure your findings like you respect their time, because if they understand the story, they will act on it.

#### [Communicate effectively across functions](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/communicate-effectively-across-functions?u=76281980)

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/communicate-effectively-across-functions?u=76281980&t=0)** The best analysis means nothing if no one understand it. [Data Science](../../Topics/Data%20Science.md) is a team sport who will work with product managers, marketers, designers, engineers, and most of them won’t think in [SQL](../../Skills/Data%20Science/SQL.md), let alone model assumptions. That's not a problem. But it does mean you'll have to adjust how you speak. The goal isn't to simplify the work; it’s to clarify the message. Start by understanding your audience. What do they need from you? What decisions are they trying to make? And what context do they already have? For example, a product manager might care about user trends and next steps, not how you handle outliers. A marketer might need to know which campaign worked and not the confidence interval behind it. When I'm preparing to share results, I ask, “What's the one thing this person needs to walk away with? What language will make it clear without needing a technical deep dive?” So avoid jargon unless you are sure they speak the same language. Dropoff rate might be fine, but precision recall tradeoff-- probably not. One practical habit: Keep a version of your findings for internal use and a cleaned-up version for stakeholders. That lets you stay accurate without overwhelming your audience. Good communication is not about dumbing things down. It’s choosing the right level of detail so everyone can move forward together.

I once built a dashboard with 15 filters, fully flexible, technically solid, and the stakeholders said it was overwhelming. We cut it down to three views with preset options. Same data, but much more usable. And feedbacks here made it better. You're not just delivering analysis-- you're building tools for others, so let them help you shape it. That's not a compromise. It's a real collaboration.

#### [Present insights with confidence](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/present-insights-with-confidence?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-data-scientist-27892157/present-insights-with-confidence?u=76281980&t=0)** You can have the best insight in the room, but if you deliver it with hesitation or you overload your audience, it won’t land. As a data scientist, presenting is not about confidence. It's about making people feel they are in good hands. That starts with knowing your material and, just as importantly, knowing your audience. When you present insights, lead with a takeaway, then walk through the why step by step using plain language. Avoid over-explaining unless someone asks for it. But don't just talk at people. Watch how they respond. Are they nodding? Are they confused? Are they silent? Invite questions early-- not with “Any questions?”-- but with something more specific like “Is this matching what you've been seeing on your end?” I've learned that the most effective presentation don't feel like lectures. They feel like conversations. And when pushback comes, you have to assume there is good intent. Sometimes people are skeptical because they have been burned by bad data before. So you have to acknowledge that. You have to walk them from your process, show how you handle edge cases, and where the limits are. Presenting with confidence doesn't mean having all the answers. It means being prepared, clear, and open to discussion. The goal is not just to inform, it's to be trusted. And so that's what turns a good presentation into a [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md) moment.
