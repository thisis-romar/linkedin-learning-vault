Advanced charts and graphs, however, are multidimensional and tell a deeper story. A box and whistle A whisker plot, for example, takes the bar chart to an entirely new level. Made famous by Florence Nightingale in the 1850s, these charts are also referred to as rose charts or the Nightingale rose chart. The stream graph is essentially a stacked area graph plotted on a central baseline rather than against a fixed straight axis. It's used to compare changes of relative proportions within categorical data over time. Unlike a stacked area graph, edges are rounded rather than straight. This, combined with plotting along a central baseline, makes the final visualization look more like a flowing river than a traditional graph. Each category is represented by a unique color, and considered its own stream in the flow. Hence the name, StreamGraph. Now this graph was popularized by the New York Times in 2008, when their graphics and data team released the interactive chart The Ebb and Flow of Movies, Box [Office](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Microsoft%20Office.md) Receipts from 1986 to 2008. Rather than giving each movie its own color category, color is used to represent box office sales, and interactivity is used to identify the movie associated with each stream.
>
> **[1:46](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/visualizing-advanced-charts-and-graphs/streamgraphs-explained-what-when-why-and-how?u=76281980&t=106)** This chart exemplifies the pros and cons of stream graphs. As we scroll through, we can quickly identify the most successful movies, how they trend over time, as well as overall box office trends.
