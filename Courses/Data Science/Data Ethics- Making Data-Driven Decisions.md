#### [Start with ethical objectivism](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/start-with-ethical-objectivism?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/start-with-ethical-objectivism?u=76281980&t=0)** - One of the biggest challenges is to try and come up with an ethical answer that everyone agrees is morally right. It's tough because there are moral philosophers, and probably people at your company who believe that there aren't universally right or wrong answers. They argue that everything is relative to the situation and the people involved. But this relativistic approach doesn't work well for organizations. In an organization, you probably won't get everyone to agree on an objectively correct answer. But you need to get everyone to agree that the question has an answer. Otherwise you won't be able to make any decisions. So the flip side of ethical relativism is something called ethical objectivism. These are ethical theories that focus on a one-size-fits-all approach to complex ethical questions. In this course, we're going to look at three of these theories. There's the deontological approach of Immanuel Kant, the utilitarian approach, and finally, virtue [Ethics](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Ethics.md). To start, let's look at the deontological approach. This is an ethical approach that focuses on a series of duties. Immanuel Kant was an 18th century German philosopher. He believed that moral laws guided everyone's actions. He felt that these moral laws were objective and universal, that there was a clear right and wrong answer. He also thought that these moral laws
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/start-with-ethical-objectivism?u=76281980&t=94)** are best understood through reasoning. In his view, it wasn't enough to discover why something was right or wrong, you had to able to explain why something was right or wrong. Even though many people experienced a push and pull between what they want to do and what they should do, Immanuel Kant said that what you want to do is irrelevant. Instead, you should focus on what he called dutifulness. It's where you follow moral rules regardless of the consequences. Deontological ethics clearly defines right from wrong. And this ethical objectivism made it easier to answer moral questions in a consistent way. But as you can imagine, a lot of people disagreed with this approach. To them, it seemed like occasionally there was a moral justification for lying. Your duties also shouldn't be so rigid that you end up doing things that seem cold or unjust. That's why two philosophers, Jeremy Bentham and [John](../../Glossary/Tool/John%20the%20Ripper.md) Stuart Mill, came up with a competing theory that they called utilitarianism. Here, instead of focusing on absolute duties, you look to see if the action's harm exceeds its benefits. They called this the Greatest Happiness Principle. Utilitarianism is a consequentialist theory. That's because it focuses on the consequences of an action. Does taking this action make people more or less happy? This is the opposite of Kant's deontology.
>
> **[3:12](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/start-with-ethical-objectivism?u=76281980&t=192)** Here, you have to do what's right no matter what the consequences. So instead of thinking of strict duties, you should instead ask yourself what creates the greatest amount of good for the greatest number of people. Finally, there's virtue ethics. Here, you'll try to have your organization act as one virtuous person. Then you figure out what this virtuous person would do to answer a complex moral question. Virtue ethics is a little bit of a mix between deontology and utilitarianism. On the one hand, virtue ethics sees each person as having a set of virtues. On the other hand, virtue ethics is also deeply concerned with the consequences. You want a moral person to do the right thing, because it's the right thing to do. We'll consider these theories as we take a closer look at common ethical questions that arise when it comes to [Data Ethics](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data%20Ethics.md).

#### [Think about your categorical imperatives](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/think-about-your-categorical-imperatives?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/think-about-your-categorical-imperatives?u=76281980&t=0)** - Organizations face difficult ethical choices all the time. A lot of these choices can be broken down into what you want to do versus what you ought to do. When I'm driving my car, I might want to ignore the red light so I can get there faster, but I feel a duty to stop my car, even if there's no other traffic. I do what I ought to do instead of what I want to do. Immanuel Kant called these the categorical imperatives. A categorical imperative is pretty much what it sounds like. You must categorically do something or not do something to act morally. And, remember, you should act this way no matter what the consequences. Kant said what you want to do is irrelevant. The moral law binds to everyone. Kant spent a lot of time trying to settle on the smallest number of imperatives. He came up with the four formulations of the categorical imperative, but the first three are the most famous. The first formulation is usually called the Universalizability Principle. It means that if you're doing something that would create a problem if everyone was doing it, then it's usually immoral. So think of it this way. When you're sitting in your car at a stoplight, you're following the duty to not act immoral, but you're also following the first formulation of the categorical imperative. That's because if everyone ignored stoplights, then it would be impossible to drive.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/think-about-your-categorical-imperatives?u=76281980&t=94)** It would create a serious problem if everyone acted this way. So it's not universalizable. The second formulation of the categorical imperatives is often called the Rule of Humanity. It says that you should not use other people as a means to an end. On the one hand, it's okay to use objects like a car as a means to an end, but you can't use people the same way. You might use your car to get to work, and if it breaks down, you might decide to sell it for parts but you can't treat people the same way. Cars exist to drive, but people exist for the benefit of themselves. Still, there are many ways to use people without acting immorally. You might use someone to fix your car, but your mechanic is doing this of their own free will. So it's not a means to an end. Now, if you knowingly wrote them a bad check, then you'd be acting immorally. The third formulation of the categorical imperative is often called the Formula of Autonomy. Kant says that you should always act in a way that encourages others to use their own free will. This is one of the reasons that Kant always says that you have a duty to tell the truth. If you lie, then you're taking away their ability to be autonomous. So if you have a terrible haircut, I have a duty to tell you the truth, even if it hurts your feelings. Otherwise, I'd be taking away your autonomy to try and improve your hairstyle.
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/data-ethics-making-data-driven-decisions-2022/think-about-your-categorical-imperatives?u=76281980&t=189)** As you'll see later, a lot of issues around [Data-driven Decision Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Data-driven%20Decision%20Making.md) conflicts with this third categorical imperative. Maybe if you gave the customer access to this decision making, they could make changes based on their own moral code. So as a project manager for a car company, you might argue to give people a few options when programming their car. That way, they can make a decision that's consistent with their own morality. But so far, most car companies have kept their algorithmic decision making a closely guarded secret.
