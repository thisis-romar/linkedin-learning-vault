So from a technical perspective, Excel formulas will work on any type of data, whether financial or nonfinancial, they are just numbers. So there isn't much of a difference between working with financial or nonfinancial data in Excel. But there are just a few things that we need to consider when presenting [[Financial Data]]. Financial data differs in at least three ways. The types of data, the timing, and the purpose. These each can affect the way that you analyze and present the data in Excel, and then in [[Microsoft PowerPoint|PowerPoint]]. Financial data typically involves monetary values such as revenue or expenses, profits, investments. Whereas nonfinancial data focuses on quantities or counts, percentages, or qualitative information. When finalizing and presenting the data, make sure that your formatting shows the dollars or percentages, et cetera, to make sure that you really get your meaning across. Secondly, financial data is often time bound. For example, monthly reports or quarterly earnings. Nonfinancial data, such as metrics are often a point in time. So with presentations, make sure that the layout shows the time series. A lot of presentations don't make sense if you don't explain the timeframe.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/excel-and-powerpoint-creating-high-impact-financial-presentations/how-financial-and-non-financial-data-differs-in-excel?u=76281980&t=95)** And lastly, the purpose. Financial data is primarily used for [[Financial Analysis]], budgeting, [[Forecasting]], and [[Decision-Making]] processes. So it's really important to include the context in the presentation. What are we using the presentation for? Are you just giving them historical information, or do you want them to actually make a decision based on your data?
