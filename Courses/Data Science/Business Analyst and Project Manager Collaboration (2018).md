> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/goals-of-the-roles?u=76281980&t=0)** - Many wonder if one person can be both the project manager and the BA on a project. It's an important question to ask and there are many factors that go into the answer. There are times when this can work and also times I wouldn't recommend it. Let's look at the essence of what each role does. A project manager is responsible for delivering the work of the project. This often includes defining the work and delivering the work within a budget and schedule with agreed upon quality levels. A business analyst is responsible for ensuring what the project delivers is valuable and meets the needs of the business and customers. Without one of these roles, chaos can break out, lackluster results can ensue, or simply the project fails. If a project has a PM and no BA or no one performing the BA role or perhaps the BA role isn't done well, the project is at a high risk of building the wrong thing. This is realized when users get the product and [react](../../Skills/Web%20Development/React.js.md) that it's not what they needed. Often it's too late and too much has been spent before leaders realize that there has been waste in building the wrong thing. On the flip side, if a project has a BA and no PM, or no one performing the PM role, or the PM role isn't done well, the project has a high risk of running into schedule and budget issues
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/goals-of-the-roles?u=76281980&t=94)** and the analysis takes too much priority over the budget and schedule, or potentially [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md) is impeded by focus on other areas. Depending on the project approach and methodology, various factors and roles can influence these dynamics. Each project is different and the context can change everything. For example, some projects follow a traditional approach with formal plans and formal documentation and the size and complexity of the project can be a big factor. The larger the project, the more a PM and/or multiple BAs are needed. If the project is quite small, one person may be able to play both roles. Having both roles represented is critical and hopefully can lead to some healthy conflict when what needs to be built conflicts with the budget and schedule needs. This is a healthy conflict where both roles can balance the decision making process and help business leaders make critical decisions. This duo of PM and BA is an awesome duo to provide projects with the balance to succeed.

#### [Project vs. product](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/project-vs-product-8540167?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/project-vs-product-8540167?u=76281980&t=0)** - Many confuse the terms project and product. Is this something you've experienced too? Product, project, what's the difference? A project is a temporary initiative to create a product, and a product is something that delivers value as the result of a project. It's easy to use them interchangeably in conversation, yet in project work, it's important to differentiate. The focus of each of these is different. The product lives on, while the project is temporary. The PM focuses on the project and the BA focuses on the product. Let's look at Pat the PM and Bailey the BA. Pat the PM and Bailey the BA are on a project together looking to transform their customer relationship management processes, or a CRM for short, and improve customer relationships. The organization built a legacy system years ago for CRM and it's no longer cost effective to maintain, nor is it meeting business goals and needs. They decided to invest in a new system and determine that purchasing rather than building software is the approach to take. The project is the initiative to make the change, it's temporary and has completion criteria. The product is the system and process that lives on after the project is complete.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/project-vs-product-8540167?u=76281980&t=95)** The project creates the product. It's common that smaller projects or a product team is formed to enhance the product as needed. Pat looks from the project perspective and Bailey looks at the product point of view. For the CRM project, this has Pat looking at the vendor management, resources, budget, schedule and overall plans for the project. Bailey's focus is on the groups that are impacted and how their usage and interaction with the system changes. Bailey is also looking at how any end customer touchpoints may be impacted. Bailey's focus is working with stakeholders to understand how the new CRM package works and what it takes to make it work in their environment to serve their operation and customers. Collaboration between Pat and Bailey is critical as they feed one another to get their work accomplished successfully. Bailey, working to define the product and its requirements, will run into scope questions, and scope questions feed the estimates and schedule that Pat's working on. Pat and Bailey work well together as a team because they both understand the difference between the project and the product and what it means to their roles and how they work together. They're in constant communication on areas where the project and the product overlap and they play off one another's work to get great results for stakeholders.

#### [The triple constraint](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/the-triple-constraint-8541081?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/the-triple-constraint-8541081?u=76281980&t=0)** - Pat the PM is worried about the project deadline, it's coming up quick. And Bailey is concerned about the quality. Requirements were rushed, scope was added along the way, and testing is showing lots of issues that will impact the business teams and customers if the team implements. Try to take a rubber band and stretch it three ways into a triangle. Now if we pull one of the corners out putting pressure on that corner, what happens to these other corners? They get stressed out, and they might break. The triple constraint of project management works the same. Cost, schedule and scope is what the triple constraint is made of. How this works is that if you increase the need or importance of one, the others get stressed and need attention too. So if we increase the scope, the cost and schedule need to increase as well. Makes sense right? Isn't it funny how easy it is to say yes to increasing one, without planning for the increase in the others? As easy as it is, and as much as it seems, our leaders expect magic. It's detrimental to project success to allow a pattern of thinking that one doesn't impact the others. Pat and Bailey are in the heat of this. Pat, concerned about missing the deadline, and Bailey, concerned about the quality due to rushed requirements and added scope. Project managers and BAs collaborate together with leaders to understand
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/the-triple-constraint-8541081?u=76281980&t=93)** which of the three triple constraint areas are the most important to meeting objectives and delivering value. The PM and BA need to be in sync together with both the leaders and the team in understanding which of the three will lead [Decision-Making](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Decision-Making.md). This decision can impact strategy and how well the project executes on a leader strategy. So for example, on a project implementing a new customer billing system, an organization that leads with cost as the driver will sacrifice schedule and scope to some degree to hit the cost objectives. And likewise, if scope is prioritized, schedule and costs will not be optimized as much. So, which to prioritize depends on the real business objectives. And in a perfect world, all three are balanced. Let's say the customer experience is the most important factor in making the project successful. And if the customer experience with the new billing system isn't at a certain level, the project isn't even worth doing. Then scope and quality need to lead over costs and schedule. If we demand a certain scope, quality, cost and schedule all be important, then the team needs to understand minimums in each area and if it's feasible. What's interesting to me is that many teams think that costs and schedule lead their projects. Yet, when I ask the leaders what they think,
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/the-triple-constraint-8541081?u=76281980&t=190)** they answer scope and quality and customer experience, meaning they want the right thing to be built at the right quality level. So, it's important for PMs and BAs to collaborate together with leaders to get at the true drivers and motivations of the project. Keep success in plain view with the triple constraint, by talking through which is leading and how the project will manage when the wrong one pulls the others.

#### [Portfolio-level collaboration](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/portfolio-level-collaboration-8536783?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/portfolio-level-collaboration-8536783?u=76281980&t=0)** - At the portfolio level, PMs and BAs are working to define projects related to strategic goals of the organization and align the projects together as a group of related projects. Project and program managers are looking at the budget and schedule linkages, as well as overall strategic alignment of the project goals. BAs are looking at strategic alignment, business architecture and the big picture of who is impacted and the value the groups need to get from these projects. Our team members, Pat the PM, and Bailey the BA, may be assigned to work on the portfolio level with other PMs and BAs. Or as part of the intake process to start up a large set of projects. Some key tactical things that Pat and Bailey collaborate on are first, defining the problem or opportunity statement. They create a shared understanding together with the leaders on the problem or opportunity, which helps with strategic alignment as they look at the portfolio of work. Next, they work together to define the value proposition for the impacted stakeholder groups and not forgetting about the customer. They think through each group and ask, research and discover how the project benefits the group. And next, they determine measurable goals and objectives to help chart the course
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/business-analyst-and-project-manager-collaboration-2018/portfolio-level-collaboration-8536783?u=76281980&t=93)** and keep scope tied and aligned to measurable outcomes.
