#### [CAP domains](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/cap-domains?u=76281980)

>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/cap-domains?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] There are seven performance domains the CAP program tests. The first domain is Business Problem or Question Framing. This is where everything begins, too. You need to have a clear understanding of what business problem to solve. Otherwise, you end up answering a wrong question, which is worse than not doing anything. That is you waste your time and money on nothing valuable to your business. The second domain is to turn the business problem into a data analytics problem so that the connections to the appropriate methodologies and software tools are clear to those attempting to develop optimal solutions. The third domain focuses on data and addresses data sources, collection, cleaning, transformation, validation, and relationships. Don't worry. Even if you're not familiar with these concepts, we'll go over each of them again later, in more depth. The fourth domain is Methodology Selection. There are many methodologies and tools to choose from and the challenge is finding the most appropriate one for a given problem. After acquiring and preparing data and deciding on a methodology, the logical next step is building an analytical model to first represent the phenomenon
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/cap-domains?u=76281980&t=96)** and to eventually produce a solution. This is similar to predicting weather. You feed your data into a weather model, which mimics the natural weather phenomena and can therefore tell you what tomorrow's weather will be like. The model is what solves a business problem, which is why it's significant enough to be the fifth CAP domain. Domain six is Deployment. Building a model requires lots of testing and calibration until the model is ready to be delivered to the field and used on a daily basis. The final and seventh domain is Model Lifecycle Management. Just like anything of significance out there, analytical models have a lifespan. We need to continuously monitor their effectiveness, calibrate them if necessary, or even retire it, if it's no longer serving its purpose. INFORMS specified these domains after a careful Job Task Analysis, or JTA study, as a result, they reflect what data analytics professionals have to go through daily when performing their jobs.

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/identifying-business-problems?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Narrator] As a data analytics professional, your first job is to work with business people to figure out what problem they want to solve. Sometimes they don't know what they want, which is why it's critical to carefully facilitate the process of identifying their needs, and help them develop a meaningful and significant problem statement at the end of the process. The first step in business problem framing is to identify people who can either affect or be affected by the business problem. We call these people stakeholders. As you learn more about the requirements, it's critical to think about whether the business problem warrants a data analytics solution. Not every problem is amenable to a data analytics treatment. > **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/identifying-business-problems?u=76281980&t=96)** It's common to revise a problem statement several times before being able to produce a satisfactory final version. Also, a data analytics project itself needs to make financial sense. It should be justified with expected business benefits before getting an approval. Finally, all the stakeholders need to agree with the problem statement before moving on to the next step. Let's take a closer look at each of these steps in this chapter.

#### [Determining the feasibility](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/determining-the-feasibility?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/determining-the-feasibility?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] Once you establish a solid problem statement, take a moment and assess whether the problem has an analytics solution or not. You should be asking questions like, Is it possible to implement the analytics solution with the resources under the organization's control? Do we have all the necessary data to perform an analysis? After determining analytical feasibility, think about whether solving the problem makes financial sense. One way to do this is cost benefit analysis. It's a ratio between net gain or loss resulting from a business commitment and the amount of resources committed. Let's say that an investor spends $10,000 to buy stocks. After 10 years, the stocks are worth $12,000 and sold. In this scenario, the net gain or ROI is $2,000 out of $10,000, which is 20%. Let's not worry about taxes or commissions in this scenario. Similarly, the investment in a data analytics project for investigating the loss of customers may be $10,000. If the findings of the project help attract a significant number of additional customers to the business
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/certified-analytics-professional-cap-cert-prep/determining-the-feasibility?u=76281980&t=94)** and brings in $12,000 in additional revenue, the ROI is also 20%. In this scenario, the data analytics project has a positive ROI and it's worth the investment. There could also be a negative ROI because of the difficulties associated with predicting a return. Businesses are taking a risk when embarking on a data analytics project. The question is how much risk they're willing to take? If the potential benefits exceed the estimated risk, the project is worth trying.
