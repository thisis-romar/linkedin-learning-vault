#### [Anatomy of a marketing prompt](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/anatomy-of-a-marketing-prompt?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/anatomy-of-a-marketing-prompt?u=76281980&t=0)** - The best marketing prompts for [[Generative AI]] start with thoughtful planning, and after this lesson, you'll be able to plan excellent prompts by considering a variety of factors which will add an authentic voice to your marketing content. First, ask yourself who you're talking to and who is your target market. You'll want to define the role and your buyer persona for generative AI for any content you make. For example, you might tell generative AI to take the role of a marketer that sells soccer gear to teenagers. Next, consider what kind of content you're creating and what you are hoping to achieve from it. Being literal with clear instructions will make sure you get what you want. You could say you want an 800 [[Microsoft Word|word]] blog post on what high school soccer players can do to get ready for interviews from college soccer coaches. Make sure it's content that target you define. In this case, high school soccer players would be very interested in if you have an existing blog post on a similar topic. You can also provide it to generative AI as reference text. Finally, you want to define the style of writing and provide context to the request. This includes defining the composition of the content created and setting boundaries on the results. You might say you want a casual, informative style that provides a step-by-step guide to a 30 minute interview with the coach. You may even further guide the output to include eight questions to expect and how to dress for the interview. Language like this allows you to guide the results to sound more like you would've written anyway and will make it easier when you add the final personal and authentic touches later.

#### [Provide clear prompt direction](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/provide-clear-prompt-direction?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/provide-clear-prompt-direction?u=76281980&t=0)** - When working with [[Generative AI]] on marketing content, it's important to define your guardrails, create boundaries, and be clear with your expectations. If you don't do this, you're basically telling generative AI to guess at what you mean and who knows what kind of results you'll get. The first way to ensure clear direction is to set a role in the prompt so that it's clear from what perspective you expect the output to be written from. This is the first step in making the output your very own. You can do this by imagining who might best be writing your content. Would it be an [[Email Marketing]] manager writing your next newsletter article, or maybe an [[E-Commerce]] manager writing a product description? It could even be the role of a CEO for a [[Thought Leadership]] piece that ends up on your blog. When you decide on what role generative AI takes, the flip side of the coin is thinking about the audience that will read your content. As most marketers have already done this kind of work to figure out their target audience, it's not hard to write that into your prompt. If your buyer persona is men and women age 35 to 45 who practice yoga, you would want to say so when you're selling yoga mats. You can also say which marketing platform the content is for, as the messaging might change between one social media platform and another. The last part of providing clear, prompt action is about assigning specific steps to the work that is done. An example of this could be something like directing the AI to write an 800 [[Microsoft Word|word]] blog post, and then immediately after providing a four sentence synopsis for social media platforms, and then provide a translation of the post in Spanish for your companion website.

It's important for you to set tone guidelines covering language style, formality level, and even emotional appeal in your writing. Setting a consistent tone for your target audience enhances engagement and brand loyalty, as your audience knows what to expect. Make sure to include clear direction in your prompts as to whether you'd like your content to sound professional, formal, funny, easygoing, or even sarcastic. The great thing about [[Generative AI]] is once you've decided on a specific tone, it's much easier to be consistent about it in everything you write. So what's the difference between tones? Well, a professional tone shows a higher level of formality that might be used in legal, academic, or corporate settings. This kind of writing might use official titles, complex sentence structures, and avoids contractions in language. A businesslike tone would be polite but not overly formal. On the other hand, an easygoing tone shows a low level of formality that might still be used in a business environment. We see this often in social media posts, where the language is more conversational. One more aspect of tone which can affect how your brand is perceived is how much you use emotional appeal. For example, if you run a nonprofit organization that is trying to raise funds for a worthy cause, your level of emotional appeal would be higher, something you'd want to indicate in your prompt writing. And if you're writing political content, the emotional appeal might be reflected in specific ways which match your audience's belief systems and political party. There are a lot of knobs you can turn

So before you even try running a prompt for the first time, make sure you've provided enough context to make the result of your work worthwhile. One of the first things you want to consider is where the content you're making sits within the sales funnel. If I'm writing a blog post, for instance, these are usually geared towards the consideration part of the funnel. Remember, you're the expert and generative AI is taking your lead. It's important to be literal when writing prompts. Like any good relationship, be clear about your expectations and let generative AI know what you need. When you mean what you say and say what you mean, you are providing guidelines that make your content more human and effective at the same time.

#### [Meet audience expectations](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/meet-audience-expectations?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/meet-audience-expectations?u=76281980&t=0)** - Rule number one with writing for AI is to never think of your prompt as a final product. It's a living and breathing document that you can and should make your own. Even if you've already run the prompt and reviewed the result, you can always go back and rewrite it to make it even better. This first look or two of what your original prompt created is your opportunity to make sure the AI results reflect the voice of your brand and meet audience expectations. So ask yourself, does the content seem to understand your buyer personas? If your buyer personas tend to be busy professionals, for instance, the content should be more concise and to the point. If it's a more creative persona, a more vivid story-driven content makes more sense. Demographic information like gender and age might mean the original prompt should meet certain guidelines to speak to that group. You can go even further to create authenticity in your brand by personalizing the content to be unique to your communication style. If you have specific readings, sign-offs, or phrases you commonly use, make sure to add them. This phase of content development with AI is so important because only you truly know who your customers are on an emotional level. Does the content in front of you meet their expectations as much as your own? Try adding a turn of phrase, a simile, a metaphor, or a story that is indicative of your brand to the mix.

#### [Tell a story](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/tell-a-story?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/tell-a-story?u=76281980&t=0)** - A great story tells about connection between people, a challenge that is overcome or something surprisingly creative and innovative that happens to save the day. Telling a story in this way can make your content more authentic and sound more like your brand before or after the prompt. There's a great book that came out a few years ago called Made to Stick. This outlined what makes stories memorable. The authors found three types of stories with specific styles of plot lines worked especially well. One plot line they outline focuses on connection with a story that might tug at your heartstrings or inspire you. Apple used this with its iconic "Think different" campaign, which featured famous people like Albert Einstein, Martin Luther King Jr, and Bob Dylan. These ads align their brand with great and/or creative minds. They used an existing emotional connection you already associate with these figures to their benefit. Another you might see often is the challenge plot. Typically, this is a David and Goliath story where a product or company overcomes adversity because they are better than their less nimble, larger competitor. Tesla began this way as a car company, fighting the huge Detroit car making machine. It's something that's inherent to their brand story. Finally, a creativity plot is where a creative approach to a common problem saves the day. Modern examples might be Iron Man's Tony [[Stark (Accessibility Software)|Stark]], who uses creativity as his most effective weapon. Stories like these when authentic and true, can be weaved into the story of your brand and your content so that people know it came from you and isn't fake.

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/writing-great-content-with-gen-ai-that-doesn-t-sound-fake/add-a-human-touch?u=76281980&t=0)** - Potential customers have expectations as to what they learn, think about, or even feel when they engage with you. Whether it's educational value, entertainment, or inspiration, your content may or may not deliver on that expectation after your first try with AI. Adding the human touch is an important part of making AI a partner rather than a force that would replace you. After your content is published, it's important to pay attention to audience feedback. Once you've learned something from feedback you've received, you can add that concept or approach to your future prompts. Basically, you can create your own content marketing, generative AI chatbot for your product or brand where you make sure AI references prior work to make it part of any content the tool creates. My prediction for the future is that much like most companies have a brand book to define and shape their brand, you'll also have a prompt book to help keep brand ideas consistent, and any prompt book should include oversight and review as your brand's voice is a crucial element that should be consistent across all of your content. Adding the human touch by making sure you are an active participant in the results, make sure you are in charge and nothing sounds fake.
