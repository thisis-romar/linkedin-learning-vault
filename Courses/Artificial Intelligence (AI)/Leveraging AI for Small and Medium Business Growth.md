The third category consists of any form of [[Generative AI]]. Most applications in this category are based on transformers, as in ChatGPT, where GPT stands for generative pre-trained transformer. Those transformers are machine learning algorithms that can perform not just one single tasks like face recognition, but a lot of different tasks like writing text or code, summarizing an email, drawing a picture, or interpreting a diagram. The fourth and now emerging category is [[AI Agents]]. AI agents are typically a combination of AI and automation tools that perform one task at a time and take over a whole workflow, a series of tasks. For example, an AI agent can help you to plan a business trip, budget the business trip, write an itinerary, look for the best flight rates, go on the airline's webpage to make a reservation, book a flight, and provide the information for payment. A single-purpose machine learning tool in the second category may include an expert system from the first category as does the user interface of generative AI applications or an AI agent. Nevertheless, this categorization is relevant for business purposes because the risks increase when you move from left to right. It makes your discussion and your strategy more effective.

#### [Estimating ROI for an AI use case](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/leveraging-ai-for-small-and-medium-business-growth/estimating-roi-for-an-ai-use-case?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/leveraging-ai-for-small-and-medium-business-growth/estimating-roi-for-an-ai-use-case?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Narrator] Investing in AI can be a game changer, but like any investment, business leaders need to assess the return on investment, ROI. ROI, in its simplest form, is the net profit from an investment divided by the cost of that investment. For instance, if an AI tool for contract analysis saves a company, say, $200,000 in salaries over two years, and the AI solution costs $160,000 to implement, the ROI is 20%. Seems straightforward, right? The reality is that calculating ROI for AI can be tricky. AI technology constantly evolves, making predicting long-term costs and benefits difficult. Think of the three main ways AI creates value: cost reduction, value enhancement, and business model disruption. Measuring ROI on cost savings applications is relatively easy, like the contract analysis example. While investing in an AI use case may generate savings over many years, I suggest only looking at the savings in the first two years. Anything in AI beyond the time horizon of two years is pure speculation. For example, if machine learning software to optimize your warehouse costs 40,000 and saves you 30,000 per year,
>
> **[1:37](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/leveraging-ai-for-small-and-medium-business-growth/estimating-roi-for-an-ai-use-case?u=76281980&t=97)** the ROI is two years at 30,000 minus the investment of 40,000 divided by the cost of 40,000, which equals an ROI of 50%. However, measuring the financial return becomes more complex when AI enhances value by personalizing customer experiences. The benefits can be measured as the additional revenue you generate multiplied by the expected contribution margin of this revenue. For example, let's assume a company generates yearly revenues of $2 million, and the contribution margin of this revenue is 20%. Let's further assume that an AI-based chatbot to answer customer requests costs $30,000 to set up and run for two years. It is expected that the chatbot will increase revenues by 5%. The benefit is calculated as two years multiplied by 5% revenue increase of $2 million, multiplied by the contribution margin. The net benefit is this amount minus the investment, and the ROE is the net benefit divided by the $30,000, resulting in an ROI of 33.3%. Now, when it comes to disruptive AI applications that fundamentally change how business model operates,
>
> **[3:14](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/leveraging-ai-for-small-and-medium-business-growth/estimating-roi-for-an-ai-use-case?u=76281980&t=194)** predicting ROI is almost impossible. These projects are often strategic bets with uncertain but potentially massive payoffs. For those AI applications, I suggest that leaders calculate the investments very carefully, and then decide if the strategic value of the AI applications and the learning from this project justifies the investment. The key takeaway should be this. If you can calculate an ROI, do it, but don't invest only in projects where you can estimate a positive ROI, because you will miss out on AI investments that help your company build future capabilities for growth.
