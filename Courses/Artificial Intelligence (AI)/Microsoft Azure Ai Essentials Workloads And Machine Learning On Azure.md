The term artificial intelligence was coined in 1956 during a small summer research project at Dartmouth College. [John](../../Glossary/Tool/John%20the%20Ripper.md) McCarthy, a mathematics professor there, claimed that learning and intelligence could be precisely described and simulated by machines. Now, what is intelligence anyway? The definition isn't clear. For example, is a dog intelligent? People will give you different answers. Some may say dogs aren't intelligent since they cannot pass IQ tests. But one can argue that IQ tests don't fully measure intelligence. In AI, we define intelligence using the Turing test, created by Alan Turing in 1950. In the Turing test, a computer and a person are tasked to answer questions provided by a human evaluator. If a human evaluator cannot tell a computer from a person, the computer is said to be intelligent. To make a computer think like us, we need a model of how we think. And to build this model, we can use a top-down or a bottom-up approach. The top-down approach, or knowledge-driven AI,

This approach involves programming rules and expert knowledge into the system. It involves breaking down problems into smaller subproblems and creating rules for each. The system then follows the step-by-step rules, like if-then statements or complex logic, to reach a solution. This creates a rule-based system. While initially successful, it was hard to keep the knowledge base accurate, up to date, and accounting for every scenario. Nowadays, most AI systems use the bottom-up approach. This approach models the simplest unit of the brain, the neuron. We build [Artificial Neural Networks](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Artificial%20Neural%20Networks.md) and teach them to solve problems using data examples. The system learns from data, automatically discovering patterns and rules. Instead of predefined rules, it processes vast amounts of data to extract insights. For example, consider computer chess playing systems. Early systems, built top-down, used Grandmasters' moves and expert rules. Newer chess systems, using the bottom-up approach, learn by playing themselves and learning from their mistakes, just like humans. Because they can play far more games in less time,

This course covers key AI tasks and capabilities, most of which use the bottom-up approach. Machine learning is often regarded as the foundation of AI systems, and it's the way we teach a computer model to predict and draw conclusions from data. Document intelligence is the capability to manage and process large volumes of data in [Forms](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Forms.md) and documents. Knowledge mining is the capability to extract useful information from large, [Unstructured Data](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Unstructured%20Data.md) sets to create searchable knowledge stores. Finally, [Generative AI](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Generative%20AI.md) is the capability to create visual content in formats like natural language, image, code, and more.

Now, the two main types of supervised learning are regression and classification. Regression predicts continuous values, such as crop yields, based on temperature and moisture and car prices based on engine size, horsepower, and other features. Classification, on the other hand, predicts categories and it comes in two [Forms](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Forms.md), binary and multi-class. Binary classification predicts one of two outcomes, like yes or no, or valuable or not valuable. For example, predicting the onset of diabetes based on clinical data, or whether a customer will default on a loan or not based on credit history. On the other hand, multi-class classification predicts from multiple categories,

However, there are some algorithms that handle multi-label classification, allowing a movie, for example, to be both comedy and horror. Now, unsupervised machine learning uses only feature data, the x values, finding patterns without labels, the y values. Clustering, a common unsupervised method, groups similar data points, identifying patterns without labels. For example, grouping flowers by size and number of petals, or grouping customers by demographics and purchasing behavior. While both clustering and multi-class classification group data, clustering does so without labels. The algorithm finds the relevant groupings. The algorithm uses these labeled images to be able to label the rest. It processes input from audio files or live microphone input by analyzing speech patterns and mapping them to words. This process typically involves two key models. Now speech synthesis is the reverse process, turning text into speech. A speech synthesis system requires the text to be spoken and a voice to vocalize the text. This technology is useful in phone apps that respond with voice, navigation systems providing directions, reading messages, emails, or books aloud, and broadcasting public announcements, such as in airports or train stations. These are only some of the ways speech recognition and synthesis can transform how we interact with technology, making communication more efficient and accessible.

However, company-specific confidential data isn't accessible on the public internet, meaning a standard generative AI model can't provide insights from this private data. To address this, we introduce the concepts of [retrieval-augmented generation](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Retrieval-Augmented%20Generation%20(RAG).md), or RAG, and [Fine Tuning](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Fine%20Tuning.md). Retrieval-augmented generation enhances the accuracy and reliability of generative AI models by incorporating facts from external knowledge bases. These may include contracts, service manuals, or any domain-specific information relevant to a company. When a user submits a query, the system retrieves the necessary information and uses it to generate a response. Think of RAG like an open-book exam. You bring all relevant books, notes, and documents to help answer questions without needing to memorize everything.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/microsoft-azure-ai-essentials-workloads-and-machine-learning-on-azure/customizing-language-models?u=76281980&t=95)** Similarly, RAG relies on external data sources. Once these sources are detached, the model can no longer provide accurate answers based on that information. Fine-tuning, on the other hand, is similar to a closed-book exam. Here the model is adapted to specific tasks by training it on a smaller focused dataset, embedding this knowledge directly into the model. Once fine-tuned, the model can answer questions without referring to external sources, just like how you rely on your memory during a closed-book exam. Unlike RAG, fine-tuning embeds the new data inside the model. Both RAG and fine-tuning can be used together to complement each other, maximizing the effectiveness of generative AI in business contexts. However, the specifics on when to use each approach along with their pros and cons are beyond the scope of this discussion. For more detailed insights, you can explore related articles on [Microsoft](../../Instructors/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Microsoft.md) Learn.
