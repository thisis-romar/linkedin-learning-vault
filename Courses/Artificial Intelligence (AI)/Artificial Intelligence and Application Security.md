#### [Garbage in = garbage out](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/artificial-intelligence-and-application-security/5967314?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/artificial-intelligence-and-application-security/5967314?u=76281980&t=0)** - Despite its many exciting and practical applications, [artificial intelligence](../../Topics/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI).md) is inherently limited and prone to mistakes. If you're familiar with computer science and software engineering, you may have heard the term garbage in, garbage out. Fundamentally, this concept simply means that the quality of the output of a system depends heavily on the quality of the input to that system. Artificial intelligence relies on very large amounts of data. Depending on the quality of the data and the amount of data, a query to any particular AI system is likely to produce a higher or lower quality response. The garbage in, garbage out concept holds true whether we're talking about baking a cake, using software to do your taxes, or leveraging AI to help you improve [Application Security](../../Skills/Cybersecurity/Application%20Security.md). If you use top quality ingredients to bake a cake, you're more likely to have a top quality finished product. If you use inferior ingredients, then the cake is more likely to result in inferior quality. If you're trying to do your taxes, but you input incorrect numbers or you input numbers into incorrect fields, your tax software may not be able to fix that for you, and it might produce incorrect results. If you're trying to use AI to help you
>
> **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/artificial-intelligence-and-application-security/5967314?u=76281980&t=93)** with application security, but the data you feed it is entirely composed of [Network Security](../../Skills/Cybersecurity/Network%20Security.md) vulnerabilities, then you're unlikely to get the high quality results you're looking for. Note that even if you use the best possible ingredients when baking a cake, it's still possible that something might go wrong in the baking process, resulting in a low quality cake. Similarly, high quality input to an AI system is not guaranteed to result in high quality output. The way a query is structured, the [Algorithms](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Algorithms.md) and the processing involved will also impact the quality of the output. If you're using AI for application security and your intention is to determine the existence of an injection vulnerability, then submitting a query about server side request forgery is not likely to get you the results that you want. When it comes to modern AI applications, this notion of garbage in garbage out most often manifests in a user submitting a prompt and receiving a response that is deemed to be correct, less than 30% of the time when compared to a subject matter expert manually producing response to the same query. Anytime we interact with AI systems, it's important to be aware of the quality of the data that went into the system so that we can appropriately set our expectations for the quality of the output.

Organizations are accountable for the outputs of their AI systems, and they should always be checked by an actual person before it is used and actioned upon.
