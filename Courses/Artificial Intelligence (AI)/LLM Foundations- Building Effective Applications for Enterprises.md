Overreliance deals with an enterprise trusting a model too much that business is dependent on the GenAI model for its success. A model can be also stolen, similar to software code. This may lead to exposing sensitive data used to train the model and can be used for malicious business purposes. How do we protect against these vulnerabilities? It begins with training or fine-tuning data that is used to train or fine-tune the models. First, there has to be lineage control, where an enterprise tracks the origin and history of the dataset.
>
> **[3:10](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/llm-foundations-building-effective-applications-for-enterprises/protecting-against-vulnerabilities?u=76281980&t=190)** Foundation models usually use a lot of data from public domain, which may introduce some of these vulnerabilities. Then comes access control to the training data. Sufficient controls should be in place to only allow authorized users and prevent attempts to poison the training dataset. When choosing a model or a plugin, safety assessments need to be made to ensure that the model has sufficient protections against the vulnerabilities discussed. This should be part of the selection criteria for models. In addition, it is recommended to use guardrails during inference to monitor model inputs and responses and prevent malicious attempts. Some popular guardrails include prompt injection detection, PII detection and redaction and [[Data Classification]] detection. Feedback from these guardrails need to be collected and monitored, and alerts need to be issued for violations. It is highly recommended to have human oversight for both the fine-tuning and inference processes to ensure that these vulnerabilities are managed.
