The concept of interconnected layers in ANNs is modeled after the structure of Biological [[Neural Networks]], BNNs. However, it's important to know that deep-learning algorithms are far less complex than biological neural networks. To help paint the clearer picture, let's compare artificial neural networks and biological neural networks. In terms of structure, ANNs are made of artificial neurons, also called nodes, connected by weighted edges. These nodes pass information to one another, and the weight determines the importance of that information. ANNs are inspired by the structure and function of biological neurons. BNNs, on the other side, are composed of biological neurons connected by synapses and are found in living organisms. When it comes to layers, ANNs are arranged in distinct layers, such as input, hidden, and output layers. This organization influences the architecture of deep-learning models. In contrast, the layers in BNNs are complex and not as clearly defined.
>
> **[1:36](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/what-is-deep-learning?u=76281980&t=96)** Task focus also differs significantly. ANNs are computationally specific, such as recognizing objects in images or generating sequences like text. BNNs, in contrast, underlying cognitive functions in living systems from perception to memory, and even [[Decision-Making]]. Information processing in ANNs involves applying mathematical functions to numerical input through weighted sums and activation functions. Activation functions decide whether a neuron should fire or pass its output to the next layer. BNNs, however, process information through electrical or chemical signals transmitted via synaptic connections. Learning mechanisms also set them apart. ANNs learn through methods such as back propagation. Back propagation is a method where model calculates the difference between its prediction and the actual results, then adjust the weight to reduce errors, while BNNs learn through synaptic plasticity, where the strength of synaptic connection changes based on your activity. Energy efficiency is another key distinction. ANNs often require significant computational resource, such as GPUs for training on large datasets, while BNNs have potential to be more energy efficient.

> **[3:12](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/what-is-deep-learning?u=76281980&t=192)** Research in this area is still ongoing. These architectures are dynamic and continuously evolving to enhance performance, efficiency, applicability as new challenges arrive, including in healthcare. To provide context on this evolution, let's explore some architectures from the earliest to the more recent designs. Multi-layer perceptron, MLP. Some notable CNN architectures are ResNet, DenseNet, and Inception. CNN-based architectures are already applied in healthcare for enabling tasks such as diagnosing pneumonia from chest x-ray or classifying skin lesions to identify melanoma. Recurrent [[Neural Networks]], RNNs. RNNs are designed to process sequential data, such as time series, text and speech. RNNs leverage recurrent connection to maintain information across time steps, allowing them to analyze sequences effectively. Examples of tasks they may be ideal for in healthcare are predicting patient outcomes based on historical vital signs or monitoring ECG signals for arrhythmias. Improvement on the basic RNN, such as long short-term memory networks, LSTMs, and gated recurrent units, GRUs, overcome issues like vanishing gradients, making them better suited for learning long-term dependencies. Transformers. Transformers have revolutionized deep learning. If you've recently started learning about deep learning,

This feature, combined with their highly parallelizable structure, allows transformer to process large datasets and perform tasks across text, images and sequences. Other notes were the architecture include autoencoders used for dimensionality reduction and anomaly detection, [[Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)|generative adversarial networks]], GANs. #### [Deep learning algorithms](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980)

>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] If you have been following this classroom from the beginning, we have covered [[Neural Networks]], their architectures, layers, and components. Now, let's explore the [[Deep Learning]] [[Algorithms]] that enable learning. Algorithms are step-by-step computational procedures used to train the neural network by optimizing its parameters, improving performance, and achieving the desired outcomes. They formed the foundation of the learning process, guiding the model in finding patterns within data. Before we dive into specific deep learning algorithms, let's explain some key concepts. Weights and biases, these are the primary parameters within the network that determine the strength of connections between neurons and how information flows through them. Weights represent the importance of specific input while biases allow the model to shift the activation functions output. By adjusting those values during training, the network learns to make better predictions. Loss function, this quantifies the difference between the model's prediction and the actual ground truth. Minimizing the loss function to improve model accuracy and reliability is the ultimate goal of the training process. It provides a quantitative way to evaluate how well the model is performing. Common loss functions include mean squared error, MSE,
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=95)** for regression tasks and cross-entropy for classification task. Back propagation, this is the cornerstone algorithm that enables neural networks to learn from their mistakes. It works by computing the gradient of the loss function with respect to each weight and bias in the network, starting from the output layer and moving backwards using the chain rule of [[Calculus]] to effectively calculate how each parameter contributed to the error, adjusting weight and biases in the direction that minimizes the loss function. This process allows the network to iteratively refine its parameters and improve its prediction over time. Think of this as what happens in multidisciplinary medical teams where each specialist analyzes their specific area of expertise, providing targeted feedback to refine the diagnosis and treatment plan. Just as this collaborative approach improves patient outcomes, back propagation ensures that all components of a neural network work together to achieve better predictive accuracy. Deep learning algorithms can be broadly categorized into optimization algorithms, regularization techniques, and learning rate schedulers. Optimization algorithms. Optimization algorithms are designed to adjust the weight
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=189)** and biases of a neural network in a way that minimizes the loss function. These algorithms guide the model towards optimal performance by iteratively updating its parameter during training. Some examples include gradient descent, a foundational algorithm that iteratively adjusts parameters in the direction of the steepest descent on the error surface, much like adjusting the medication dosage in small increments to find the optimal therapeutic effect while minimizing side effects. Stochastic gradient descent, SGD. This updates the parameter after processing each training example individually, making the training more efficient. Think of adjusting the dose of a medication for each patient in a clinical trial based on their individual responses rather than waiting for the data from the entire trial population. This approach allows for quicker insight and faster dose optimization. Adam, adaptive moment estimation. This combines the benefit of both momentum and adaptive learning rate, leading to faster convergence and improved performance. Imagine a smart infusion pump that adjust the rate of drug delivery based on both the patient's current vital signs, momentum, and long-term trend adaptive learning.
>
> **[4:45](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=285)** This ensures both immediate and sustained optimal therapeutic delivery. RMSprop, root mean square propagation. These are just the learning rates for each parameter individually, making it very efficient with recurrent neural networks. A good analogy of this is in personalized rehabilitation therapy where each muscle group is exercised at the pace and intensity tailored to its specific recovery progress. This ensures efficient and targeted improvement in overall functionalities based on recent performance rather than just current capability. Regularization techniques. These are essential to prevent overfitting. Overfitting is a common issue in both deep and traditional machine learning where a model performs well on training data but performs poorly on unseen test data. This occur when the model becomes overly complex and starts to memorize the training data instead of learning the underlying pattern. By introducing constraints or penalties, regularization improves the model's ability to generalize, making it more robust and capable of handling real world data. Some key regularization methods include dropout. This technique temporarily deactivate a random subset of neurons during training. This forces the network to develop redundant representation,
>
> **[6:22](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=382)** making it less reliant on individual neurons and improving its ability to generalize. Dropout is like alternating the use of different muscle groups during physical therapy exercise to build overall strength and reduce reliance on a single group. Early stopping, this monitors the model's performance on the separate validation sets during training. Training is stopped when the model's performance on the validation sets starts to degrade, preventing the model from overfitting to the training data. Early stopping is like adjusting the drug infusion based on therapeutic effects and side effects in the validation group. Just as you stop increasing the dose when the benefit plateaus and side effects appear, early stopping stops training when validation performance starts to decline, preventing overfitting. L1 regularization, Lasso, add a penalty term to the loss function based on the absolute values of the model's weight. This encourages sparsity in the model, meaning that many weights become zero effectively reducing the number of features used by the model. L1 regularization is like surgical precision in removing unnecessary tissue while preserving essential structures. As it completely eliminates some features, weights go to zero while maintaining critical ones.
>
> **[8:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=480)** L2 regularization, Ridge, this add a penalty term to the loss function based on the squared values of the weight. This encourages smaller weight overall, leading to a more generalized model. L2 regularization is like prescribing the low sodium diet to manage blood pressure, where the goal is to avoid extreme levels without completely eliminating sodium. Learning rate schedulers. The learning rate determines our largest step the optimization algorithm takes during parameter updates. Similar to medication dosing where too high a dose risk adverse effects and too low a dose may be ineffective, selecting an appropriate learning rate is critical. A high learning rate may prevent the model from converging while a low one can make training inefficient. Learning rate schedulers dynamically adjust the learning rate during training to optimize convergence, similar to our modern drug delivery system adjust dosing based on patient response. Some common strategies include step decay. This reduces the learning rate by a factor after a fixed number of training epochs, like a stage prednisone taper. This allows the model to adjust at each level before further reduction. Exponential decay. This decreases the learning rate exponentially
>
> **[9:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/deep-learning-fundamentals-for-healthcare/deep-learning-algorithms?u=76281980&t=573)** similar to drug elimination kinetics, allowing increasingly fine tuned adjustment as training progresses. Cyclic learning rate, this fluctuates the learning rate between the bounds, like interval training in cardiac rehabilitation, enabling the model to escape shallow minima and find better solutions. Warm restarts. Warm restarts periodically resets to high learning rate followed by gradual reduction, similar to pulse therapy in autoimmune conditions, helping overcome optimization plateaus while maintaining effectiveness. Modern implementation often combine these strategies or adapt them based on model performance, much like our combination therapy and personalized medicine adjust treatment plans based on patient response.
