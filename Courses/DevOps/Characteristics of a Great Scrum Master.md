#### [Be an honest broker](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/be-an-honest-broker?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/be-an-honest-broker?u=76281980&t=1)** - Have you ever had a manager who gave preference to a certain group? It's disheartening particularly when you're not in the preferred group. For this reason, it's precisely why being an honest broker is so important to be a great [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) master. What's an honest broker? Well, it's someone who is accepted by everyone where neutrality is important for the purpose of developing a solution without a preference for either party involved. Let's explore three characteristics of an honest broker. An honest broker provides balanced attention. As a scrum master, your time is spent where needed based upon helping the team achieve the best results each sprint. In this case, time is spent in a balanced fashion for both developers and product owners, the scrum team. No preference should be given to an individual or a functional group rather where the attention is needed based upon the circumstances keeping an eye toward the main goal, product success. A great scrum master acting as an honest broker encourages ideas to evolve toward a solution. In the world of improv, this is referred to as the art of the possible. Each player takes an approach of yes and rather than yes but. So when each idea is presented, everyone else on the team builds on that idea. As a scrum master who encourages ideas to evolve, you'll often keep the brainstorming process flowing by asking what else can we add to the idea? Drive toward the best idea. A great scrum master gives equal weight. An honest broker is neutral
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/be-an-honest-broker?u=76281980&t=95)** yet supportive of the ideas presented. Your input is biased towards building the best team. For example, let's think about when the product backlog is being refined during a sprint. Here are some questions an honest broker would ask. Does the product owner's voice get heard? Are her priorities being expressed to the team? Does the team have an opportunity to weigh in on those priorities? Are you doing a good job of bringing those voices together? A skill that cuts through each of these three characteristics is competent facilitation. It is your secret weapon to push forward through crucial conversations as an honest broker. As you consider these characteristics, think, are you an honest broker?

#### [Revealing root causes](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/revealing-root-causes?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/revealing-root-causes?u=76281980&t=1)** - The retrospective, much maligned, underutilized, often missing the mark because they devolve into complaint sessions, yet never really at root causes of issues. Inevitably, actionable experiments are not developed and everyone feels as though they've wasted time. If this sounds like your team, let's explore a few ways great [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) masters help teams reveal root causes. First, a great scrum master develops a shared pool of knowledge across the team. To accomplish this, do the following. Ensure everyone on the team is contributing to the conversation. Encourage everyone to discuss what went right and what went wrong. Listen to every voice and ensure it is heard by all so that we have the best information to mind for root causes. Second, a great scrum master identifies patterns, which deepen the understanding of the problem and garner common agreement. For example, individual members of the team indicate how the daily scrum was missed a few times during the spring. Some discuss how a few members were notoriously late. As a scrum master you may suggest the pattern inconsistent attendance of the daily scrum. One of my favorite practices is to physically group the ideas together on a board so it is visible and apparent. And third, a great scrum master facilitates exposing insights. Building on the example where we saw the pattern of inconsistent daily scrum attendance, there is likely a reason for this.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/revealing-root-causes?u=76281980&t=94)** If the team focuses on just the facts rather than eventually shifting to the underlying reasons why, then the team may not arrive at the proper resolution or no resolution at all. So utilizing thoughtful, yet analytical questions that inspire deeper thought beyond symptoms is a valuable tool. To go beyond symptoms and arrive at root causes through insights, I suggest these powerful questions. What have we learned as a result of the patterns we've uncovered? What seems obvious as a root cause? What's important here? How can se get more clarity? Revealing root causes remains a crucial tool to ensure one of the most important principles within [Agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md), continuous improvement. So when another issue plaguing the team arises, how will you help reveal the root cause?

These observations can range from team dynamics to engineering deficiencies and everywhere in between. Third, support the application of the Scrum framework. Knowing the framework and using the framework can be an ocean of difference. #### [Doggedly remove obstacles](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/doggedly-remove-obstacles?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/doggedly-remove-obstacles?u=76281980&t=0)** - One daily [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) after another, the team cites an impediment. During every retrospective, the same anchors to success exist. If this sounds like your team, let's talk about a few ways to remove these impediments so they never plague the team again. To properly remove an impediment, it has to be adequately identified. Here are a few techniques to identify an impediment. The first technique to identify an impediment is to listen for the obvious. A team member during a daily scrum or otherwise explicitly says, "I have an impediment." You dream of team members expressing their impediments this explicitly all the time. The second technique to identify an impediment is to decipher between the signal and the noise. Impediments can be stated, but amongst other information. Developing the listening skills to hone in on the impediments, separating them amongst other important, yet different information is vital. The next step is to identify who can best remove the impediment. Empowering the team to remove the impediment can be the most effective and longest-lasting. However, there are times when only someone outside the team can best remove an impediment. In cases such as this, ensure that she is accessible, empowered, and willing. The last step is to help the impediment-remover develop a strategy to remove the obstacle, if necessary. Here are some techniques. Help the remover understand the context of the impediment, its impact, and who it's impacting.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/doggedly-remove-obstacles?u=76281980&t=94)** This will help the remover become more invested in the actual solution. Guide the remover to develop a solution which is smart, specific, measurable, attainable, realistic and timely. The remover should ensure the solution is accepted by those who the impediment is impacting. No sense in developing a solution which isn't in line with those who originally cite the impediment. Now, start executing the solution. Get that impediment removed. Removing impediments can be painstaking, yet it is a critical component of helping the team and organizations succeed. Take this seriously.

#### [Ensure safety](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/ensure-safety?u=76281980)

> [!transcript]- Transcript
>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/ensure-safety?u=76281980&t=1)** - Study after study tells us that safety is the key to employee happiness, which leads to a more productive and successful workforce. Each of us craves it as a basic need. Without safety, our innovation, contributions, and success are all affected to the detriment of the team and the organization. Despite this, so many organizations lack the safety to be performing, let alone high performing. So first, what is safety? In this context, safety describes an environment where curiosity, risk-taking, challenging norms, and injecting opinions are offered with respect. Then what is a [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) master's role to ensure safety? In short, each aspect of safety should be what a scrum master exudes through personality and interaction with team members. Let's explore a few examples. Building a team of diverse people is challenging. One of those challenges is getting each person to know each other's strengths, weaknesses, and humanity. There is no better way of doing that than through curiosity. Great scrum masters will often suggest to replace judgment with curiosity when exchanging ideas and opinions. Risk-taking is essential when innovating or solving problems. The team needs to feel a sense of trying and failing without fear of being labeled failures. So great scrum masters will remind the team and the organization of these things when they have lost their way simply by asking, what's a radical idea?
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/characteristics-of-a-great-scrum-master/ensure-safety?u=76281980&t=95)** Teams will often get stuck in a rut. They become content with accepting conventional wisdom or typical practices. While there is certainly comfort in the conventional, it may be an anchor. Individuals need a sense of unconventional thinking at times. Encouraging the ability to challenge the norms is important to safety. A great scrum master can shake things up a bit just by asking the team, does that practice still apply to us now? Lastly, the ability for an individual to know she can inject an opinion or idea within the team toward a problem set is essential. It is one of the cornerstones for safety. As a scrum master, noticing what is and isn't being shared is critical. Don't miss an opportunity to provide an opening to those more silent voices in the room. Scrum masters model safety through these techniques, encouraging everyone else to follow suit. Safety becomes a behavior to be practiced by everyone, thereby making it part of the culture. How will you start modeling the behavior of safety today?
