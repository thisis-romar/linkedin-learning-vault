> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-scrum-for-developers-18798445/product-owner-the-what?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Sashi] [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) has three roles, product owner, Scrum master and development team member. All three together form what is known as the Scrum team. The product owner is accountable for the product backlog which is the to-do list of items for the development team. The product backlog is a list of features, enhancements, defects, and any kind of action items for the Scrum team, but more specifically for the development team. The product owner owns the product backlog. Scrum does not prohibit anyone from making changes to the product backlog, but the product owner is eventually responsible for the contents as well as the order of items in the product backlog. The product owner is responsible for making sure the development team works on the most important product backlog items. The product owner is a person and not a committee. Scrum enforces this rule to avoid conflicts that arise out of differences in opinion in a group of people. This does not mean the product owner is the only person to have opinion on the order in which product backlog can be implemented. The product owner can be influenced by members from within or outside the Scrum team, but the product owner has the final say on the order of product backlog items.
>
> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-scrum-for-developers-18798445/product-owner-the-what?u=76281980&t=94)** This makes product owner's role extremely challenging. The product owner shouldn't be confused with a project manager or a product manager.

[Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) execution means converting a subset of product backlog items into what is known as a potentially releasable increment. In simple words, a potentially releasable increment is functional software that can be released to the user community. This is what the development team builds. The development team should not be confused with a group of programmers. The team includes individuals with one or more skills in multiple areas such as design, coding, testing, documentation, deployment, process automation, et cetera. A self-organized team has to be cross-functional, which means they have all the skills needed to convert a set of product backlog items into a potentially releasable product increment. That is functional software. There are no sub-teams within the development team, and no hierarchy within the development team. For example, the team is not divided into testing team or documentation team

> **[0:00](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-scrum-for-developers-18798445/understanding-the-development-team?u=76281980&t=0)** - [Instructor] A common myth about [Scrum](../../Skills/DevOps/Scrum.md) is that it does not allow specialization. There is nothing stated in the scrum guide that disallows or discourages specialization. When it comes to skills, you can be an I-shaped person who has highly specialized skills in one area of knowledge, or you could be a T-shaped person who has in-depth knowledge in a smaller area but also has broad general knowledge in other areas. Knowing more than one skill can make you more effective as a team member regardless of whether you are doing scrum or not. Development team members can split their time among multiple teams or dedicate all their time to one team. If you're working in multiple teams, your productivity will be impacted because as you switch between teams, you undergo what is known as context switching, which can decrease productivity. So if you split your time up between teams too much, your productivity will likely drop. Another common consideration is the development team size. For the scrum guide, the team size should be 10 or fewer. If the scrum master or product owner also perform duties of the development team member, they are included in this count. This count is just a guideline, but not following it could have serious repercussions.
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-scrum-for-developers-18798445/understanding-the-development-team?u=76281980&t=95)** A team size less than three people will likely struggle to be self-organized or cross-functional due to skills deficiency. Teams larger than 10 will have serious communication and coordination problems. When Amazon was just a startup, Jeff Bezos defined an optimal team as the one that you can feed with two pizzas. I think the two pizza rule is applicable to scrum and is a practical advice. Think of component teams as highly specialized teams that work only in one area. For example, a component team is a team of data model designers or user interface experts. A feature team, on the other hand, is a team that can produce a vertically sliced piece of system functionality. For example, a team of user interface developers, business logic developers, and database programmers that can produce a fully-functional feature such as order processing or customer registration. [Agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md) approaches evaluate teams based on the business value they produce. That is why feature teams are preferred over component teams.

> **[1:34](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/agile-software-development-scrum-for-developers-18798445/scrum-master-the-servant-leader?u=76281980&t=94)** The term servant leader can be better understood with the help of a few examples. A servant leader does not demand their teammates to be more efficient. A servant leader helps their teammates become more efficient. Example one, Ashley lets the development team members execute their plan. No micromanagement here. Example two. Ashley coached and convinced the development team to not skip the daily scrum event. She played the role of a mentor. Example three. Ashley convinced executive management to buy [Test Automation](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Test%20Automation.md) software. These behaviors make Ashley the kind of scrum master agile teams would benefit from. Scrum masters often play a critical role in conflict resolution. Rather than being a judge, scrum masters treat each conflict on a case by case basis. This means a scrum master could let a team resolve an issue on their own, or get directly involved to deescalate a situation before it becomes a bigger problem. The scrum master intervenes based on the specific context of the situation.

The product backlog is one of the three artifacts that are required by Scrum. Think of artifacts as deliverables. The product backlog is owned by the product owner, but it can be updated by anyone. It is an open book exam with no time limit. You can pause the test and continue it later. You also get a free second attempt if you do not pass the first time, but the test has to be taken within 90 days of your CSM course. Also, you need to renew your CSM certification every two years.

In my opinion, the PSM I exam is of moderate difficulty. PSM II and III are tougher exams. For PSM I, you get 60 minutes to answer 80 questions and you need to get 85% or 68 questions right. You can take the test from anywhere, but you get one attempt and you have to finish the test in one sitting. The exam fee is $150. Access [scrum.org](https://scrum.org) and look for open assessments.
