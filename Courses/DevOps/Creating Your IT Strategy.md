"Failure to understand this and to harness technology "to a company's advantage will result in "one's company being a follower in an industry "as opposed to a shaper of it." I love this quote. I'm here to say that CIOs must recognize this to be true and seize the opportunity. How does this apply to you and how do you make this happen in your compony? Information technology leaders within corporations have historically been more comfortable with tactics than they have been with strategy. The reasons for this hearken back to a time when IT was clearly a support organization within enterprises. They more or less took orders from the [rest](../../Skills/Web%20Development/Representational%20State%20Transfer%20(REST).md) of the organization rather than driving any significant part of the strategic priorities of the enterprise. The best-run IT organizations were those that could take orders quickly, fulfill them efficiently, and then move on to the next order. The key metrics for these IT organizations were systems uptime, ensuring that systems were up and running all the time, and on-time, on-budget delivery of projects. Now that IT is more core to business units and functional areas across the enterprise, CIOs and their teams must see themselves as a strategic part of the company. The problem is many large enterprises do not translate enterprise level strategy to business unit strategy as well as they should. > **[1:33](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/creating-your-it-strategy/making-it-strategic-2?u=76281980&t=93)** versus sales role, versus the product and service area's roles, versus the merger and acquisitions department's role, or if they do go about that this is often not communicated to those outside of the small leadership teams in each business unit or functional area. One of the very few divisions of the enterprise, if not the only one, that must help each of those divisions drive those strategic imperatives is the information technology division. This is true because information technology is at the heart of how companies interact with customers, sell to customers, enhance customer experience, and deliver [products](../../Skills/Software%20Development/Microsoft%20Products.md) and services, therefore you as an IT leader are almost uniquely disadvantaged by organizations with poorly articulated plans. Think of your marketing department and your [human resources](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/Human%20Resources%20(HR).md) department. The marketing department should develop a strategy that is externally focused. That is to say the focus will be on your company's existing and potential customers. The HR department should develop a strategy that is internally focused. That is to say it should focus on your company's employees. Even in companies where these plans are well-articulated, your colleagues who lead each of those functions may not participate fully in the other's plans due to the differences of their areas of focus. IT, by contrast, needs to breathe life into those two plans and all plans in between. So how should you engage your colleagues across the enterprise to change the strategic conversation? IT should then deliver the "how" in the form of options to seize opportunities and to stave off threats. Second, document what you hear into a standard format. I've developed a strategic framework called OGTM. That stands for objectives, which are the division's business units or even enterprise's overarching pursuits for the next three to five years, goals, which are the quantifiable metrics that determine the degree to which an objective is being successfully reached, tactics, which are the various action available to a company that will help the company reach the goal, and finally measures, which are the quantifiable metric that determines the degree to which a tactic is being successfully pursued. By organizing the feedback that you garner from your conversations with your colleagues you will be better positioned to have a productive conversation on what IT can do to help push some of those tactics forward, for example, through creative IT projects. By doing this will all divisions and business units of the company, you as an IT leader can see before anyone else where there are common needs articulated across the different parts of the company. IT can again be the center of the conversation

Now by agile, I mean flexible, quick to action, quick to cancel initiatives that are not working, iterative in approach, more collaborative. There are five steps I want you to think about as you're developing your agile IT department. First, simplify your IT infrastructure. Second, define a clear [IT Strategy](../../Skills/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI)/IT%20Strategy.md). Clear plans help people more readily make decisions when there appears to be a fork in the road. If one's project is clearly connected to a larger plan, then the plan itself will help clarify whether to take the right or the left fork. That validation has to happen with the intended users of the projects, be they colleagues or the company's customers. The intermediate deliverables and milestones along the way toward the project's conclusion, help the company rethink what's possible. What additional bells and whistles need to be added to or subtracted from the project, and whether the project should be continued. And the value is not realized. Lastly, develop a culture that emphasizes collaboration, transparency, and [Accountability](../../Skills/Data%20Science/Accountability.md).
