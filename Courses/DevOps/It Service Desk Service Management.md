Service management builds a framework for your organization that you can use to identify and then deliver the required services, thus value from these services is co-created by the service provider and the service consumers. Organizations need to define services in a manner that customers and users will understand so that the outcomes customers expect are consistently delivered at a cost that the organization can afford. What exactly is a service? [ITIL](../../Skills/Network%20and%20System%20Administration/ITIL.md) 4 defines a service as a means of enabling value co-creation by facilitating the outcomes that customers want to achieve, without the customer having to manage the costs and risks of the service provisioning. In more direct terms, an IT service offering is one or more services designed to meet the needs of a particular customer segment. A service may be goods, access to resources and or a service action. For example, in the mobile industries, services may include mobile devices, [Wi-Fi](../../Glossary/Standard/Wi-Fi.md) data, or cellular service and even user supported provided by the service desk. But value is a perceived benefit, usefulness, or importance as perceived by the customer or user of the service. One significant challenge for the service provider is that multiple users with the same responsibilities will perceive different value from the exact same service. This makes it especially important to set customer and user expectations early in the process and document expected outcomes and quality benchmarks of the service. Let's think of ride share service as an example. The desired outcome is getting from point A to point B. Some people are willing to share their ride for a lower cost, which may mean a longer time to the destination. Others will not want to share their ride, but pay more to get there faster without social interaction. So value is omnidirectional, meaning value is created by a collaboration between the service provider and the service consumer. Ensuring that the outcomes achieved by the service are appropriate at an acceptable cost to both parties for the value obtained. To be successful, service providers need to understand how the consumer will use the service, expected outcomes and who the key stakeholders of the service are. These are all critical aspects of creating, defining and managing services.

#### [Key service desk practices](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980)

>
> **[0:01](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980&t=1)** - The purpose of the service desk practice is to capture the demand for incident resolution and service requests. Capturing demand is achieved by funneling all requests and incidents through a single point of contact, called a SPOC, for users to interact with a service provider. Traditionally, the service desk was recognized as a function or a team of people who not only recorded all interactions but were expected to resolve many of the repetitive issues submitted to the service desk. Once an incident was escalated outside of the service desk, it was considered the responsibility of the function that that person assigned was part of. For example, an event might have been escalated to the security team, and at that point, it would no longer fall under the service desk practice. However, with the adoption of practices versus processes, we must rethink terminology. There's a difference between the service desk practice and the service desk team. When anyone is updating an issue, at first point of contact or escalation, they are a part of the service desk practice. Thus, when a team member is resolving a customer issue submitted through the service desk, they are considered part of the service desk regardless of whether they're on the service desk team or not. So, in addition to providing channels for interacting with the service provided, for example, phone, chat, self-service, social media, the service desk is also typically responsible for measuring and reporting customer satisfaction with the service desk practice
>
> **[1:35](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980&t=95)** and for positively influencing the customer experience with the service provider. Service desk teams may be local, centralized, or virtual. As more organizations are maturing their [Problem Management](../../Skills/Network%20and%20System%20Administration/Problem%20Management.md) practices and adopt [Agile](../../Skills/DevOps/Agile%20Development.md), [DevOps](../../Topics/DevOps.md), the characteristics of incoming work to the service desk is rapidly changing. Some organizations will retain their tiered support model, while others will adopt a swarming model, where non-service desk staff will be involved in the initial investigation and diagnosis of incidents. The service desk practice also serves as an omnichannel communications hub for all bidirectional user communications interactions. Moving from multichannel to omnichannel communication is important to the customer experience. Multichannel is the use of multiple channels, phone, web, email, to engage customers. While multiple channels are used, the message may not be seamless or consistent across the touch points. Additionally, the customer experience may not be optimized across the various devices, phone, smartphone, and laptop, that are used by the customers. Omnichannel builds upon multichannel by building consistency in integration of the various devices, assuring that customers receive the same experience and message regardless of the channel or device. Additionally, in the omnichannel environment, the entire customer journey is documented
>
> **[3:09](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980&t=189)** and available for use. In the past, customers and users often had to restate their issue and what had transpired to each IT support person they interacted with. In the omnichannel model, all the information about the interaction is documented in one accessible record, making the process more efficient and customer-friendly. The service desk is the primary communications point for users, and the service desk will often review and develop communications about changes, releases, deployments, and are often expected to be the voice of the customer and other practices. Most organizations will still have a dedicated service desk team who may be responsible for and interact with many service desk practices, such as service desk, incident management, service request management, problem management, service level management, knowledge management, and change management. Let's talk briefly about the function of these practices and how they interrelate. It's important to understand that any given task may be part of more than one service desk practice. For example, when a desktop technician, network administrator, application developer are working on an incident request, they are part of the service desk practice, incident practice, and possibly the knowledge practice, change control practice, release, or even deployment practice, depending on the actions needed to resolve the issue. The incident practice focuses on minimizing negative impact
>
> **[4:43](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980&t=283)** of incidents by restoring normal service operations as quickly as possible. Normally, the service desk team will receive incidents from users, however, other teams will receive incidents from network monitoring and event management as well as infrastructure and platform management activities that will then need to be funneled into the incident practice. The service desk team will need to have visibility into all these activities to minimize any rework and understand when another person or team may already be working on an issue. The request practice typically makes up the largest volume of work for a service desk team and service desk practice. Service request management is responsible for handling the user-initiated requests, which are a component of each of the services provided. Each organization needs to clearly define their covered service request, which should be documented in the service level agreement. Often, service requests are initially handled in a self-service request portal via automated workflow, with the service desk team handling those requests which are not able to be automated or received outside of the request portal. The service desk practice also works with the problem practice when they raise an error for a known or repetitive issue and when providing a workaround for a known error. The service desk practice relies on an effective knowledge management practice to ensure efficient incident and request management handling. Knowledge management is also required for effective self-service

> **[6:15](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/it-service-desk-service-management/key-service-desk-practices?u=76281980&t=375)** in any [artificial intelligence](../../Topics/Artificial%20Intelligence%20(AI).md) applications, like chatbots or virtual assistants. The service desk practice also handles most user-initiated standard changes that are not automated. The service desk represents the customer at change, release, deployment meetings, ensuring that the customer, the service consumer, is represented. Training is provided and communication is clear so that the customer is prepared. Defining a service desk practice and its various components is an important part of developing your overall service management strategy that provides guidance into key practice areas.

The configuration system can identify how many seemingly disparate changes can impact one another, thus imperative in assessing change risk and impact. [Problem Management](../../Skills/Network%20and%20System%20Administration/Problem%20Management.md) is an output to change. As problems, we'll submit change requests to remove incident causes. IT Service Continuity Management covers reviewing individual changes for their impact to the IT and business continuity plans. Security needs to review changes for the impact each change has to security as well as the overall impact over time. Knowledge management provides dates and information used to assess the impact and risk of the changes determining the priority and level of change authority necessary. Incident management associates change and release-related incidents to the change that caused them. This information should be used in reviews to improve change and release practices. The ability to link incidents to changes should provide information to resolve change-related incidents faster. All of these interfaces must be built upon for successful inputs of change management and successful outputs.
